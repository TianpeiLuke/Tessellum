# Critical Thinking & Logical Argumentation Glossary

Quick-reference glossary for logical fallacies, argumentation patterns, and critical thinking concepts in the abuse_slipbox vault. Terms are organized by fallacy category following the taxonomy in Aristotle's *Sophistical Refutations* and modern treatments (Hamblin, 1970; Damer, 2009; Hartley, 2024).

Each entry links to the full term note in `resources/term_dictionary/` for detailed definitions, examples, detection strategies, and cognitive bias connections.

**See also**: [Cognitive Science & Decision Frameworks Glossary](acronym_glossary_cognitive_science.md) — the cognitive biases that logical fallacies exploit (WYSIATI, anchoring, framing effect, conformity bias, availability heuristic).

## Foundations

### Critical Thinking
**Full Name**: Critical Thinking
**Description**: The disciplined, self-directed process of analyzing and evaluating information, arguments, and evidence to form well-reasoned judgments. Encompasses two dimensions: **dispositions** (intellectual curiosity, open-mindedness, fair-mindedness) and **abilities** (interpretation, analysis, evaluation, inference, explanation, self-regulation — Facione's APA Delphi consensus). Major frameworks include Bloom's Taxonomy (1956/2001), Paul-Elder intellectual standards, Facione's six core skills, and Ennis's dispositions-plus-abilities model. **Critical thinking is a trainable skill set, not an innate talent** — from Dewey's "reflective thinking" (1910) through modern pedagogy.
**Documentation**: [Critical Thinking Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_critical_thinking.md)
**Source**: Dewey, J. (1910). *How We Think*; Facione, P.A. (1990). APA Delphi Report; Paul, R. & Elder, L. (2006). *Critical Thinking*
**Related**: [Logical Fallacies](#logical-fallacies), [Socratic Questioning](#socratic-questioning), [Argumentation](#argumentation), [Cognitive Bias](acronym_glossary_cognitive_science.md#cognitive-bias), [WYSIATI](acronym_glossary_cognitive_science.md#wysiati), [Analytical Reading](acronym_glossary_cognitive_science.md#analytical-reading)

### Knowledge Building Blocks
**Full Name**: Knowledge Building Blocks (Six Knowledge Atoms)
**Description**: Sascha Fast's inventory of six discrete types of knowledge atoms that provide objective criteria for note atomicity in the Zettelkasten method. The six types are: premises, logical form, conclusions, definitions, distinctions, and heuristics. **The fundamental knowledge atom is the argument — premises + logical form + conclusion** — giving practitioners a structural test ("Does my note contain one complete argument?") that replaces the vague heuristic "one idea per note." Developed in 2011 and published formally in 2025, the framework resolves the beetle-in-a-box problem: the term "atomic note" had no shared definition, so community advice was unreliable.
**Documentation**: [Knowledge Building Blocks Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_knowledge_building_blocks.md)
**Source**: Sascha (2025). "The Principle of Atomicity." zettelkasten.de
**Related**: [Argumentation](#argumentation), [Research Argument](#research-argument), [Logical Fallacies](#logical-fallacies), [Critical Thinking](#critical-thinking)

### Logical Fallacies
**Full Name**: Logical Fallacies
**Description**: Errors in reasoning that undermine the logical validity of an argument. Unlike factual errors, fallacies are structural — premises may be true but the reasoning connecting them to the conclusion is flawed. **Formal fallacies** are detectable from logical structure alone (e.g., affirming the consequent); **informal fallacies** depend on content and context (e.g., ad hominem, straw man). The 12 most common informal fallacies include ad hominem, straw man, false dilemma, slippery slope, appeal to emotion, appeal to authority, red herring, hasty generalization, circular reasoning, bandwagon, tu quoque, and post hoc. Each exploits specific cognitive biases: WYSIATI enables straw man, anchoring enables hasty generalization, conformity bias enables bandwagon. The SEEC framework (State, Evidence, Evaluate, Conclusion) provides systematic detection.
**Documentation**: Logical Fallacies Term
**Source**: Aristotle. *Sophistical Refutations* (c. 350 BCE); Hartley, M. (2024). *Critical Thinking Think Smarter*
**Related**: [Cognitive Bias](acronym_glossary_cognitive_science.md#cognitive-bias), [WYSIATI](acronym_glossary_cognitive_science.md#wysiati), [Socratic Questioning](#socratic-questioning), [Groupthink](#groupthink), [Framing Effect](acronym_glossary_cognitive_science.md#framing-effect), [Five Whys](#five-whys)

## Fallacies of Relevance

Premises are logically irrelevant to the conclusion — the argument changes the subject, attacks the person, or substitutes emotion for evidence.

### Ad Hominem
**Full Name**: Ad Hominem (Argument Against the Person)
**Description**: Countering an argument by attacking the character, motives, or credentials of the person making it rather than addressing the argument's content. Three variants: **abusive** (direct character attack), **circumstantial** (alleging bias from personal circumstances), and **tu quoque** (pointing out hypocrisy). The most common fallacy in meetings and code reviews due to ego involvement. Exploits the halo effect and System 1's tendency to evaluate the messenger rather than the message.
**Documentation**: Ad Hominem Term
**Related**: [Logical Fallacies](#logical-fallacies), [Tu Quoque](#tu-quoque), [Appeal to Authority](#appeal-to-authority), [Cognitive Bias](acronym_glossary_cognitive_science.md#cognitive-bias)

### Straw Man
**Full Name**: Straw Man Fallacy
**Description**: Misrepresenting an opponent's argument as a weaker or more extreme version, then refuting the distortion instead of the actual claim. Three forms: **exaggeration**, **oversimplification**, and **fabrication**. Exploits WYSIATI — audiences evaluate only the version presented to them. The **steelman principle** (representing opposing views in their strongest form) is the structural antidote.
**Documentation**: Straw Man Term
**Related**: [Logical Fallacies](#logical-fallacies), [WYSIATI](acronym_glossary_cognitive_science.md#wysiati), [Ad Hominem](#ad-hominem), [Red Herring](#red-herring)

### Appeal to Emotion
**Full Name**: Appeal to Emotion (Argumentum ad Passiones)
**Description**: Substituting emotional persuasion (fear, pity, anger, guilt) for concrete evidence. Bypasses System 2 entirely — emotional arousal reduces analytical scrutiny. Six variants: appeal to fear (ad metum), appeal to pity (ad misericordiam), appeal to flattery, appeal to spite, appeal to guilt, and wishful thinking. Kahneman's **affect heuristic** explains the mechanism: emotional reactions substitute for careful probability estimates.
**Documentation**: Appeal to Emotion Term
**Related**: [Logical Fallacies](#logical-fallacies), [Cognitive Bias](acronym_glossary_cognitive_science.md#cognitive-bias), [Framing Effect](acronym_glossary_cognitive_science.md#framing-effect), [Appeal to Authority](#appeal-to-authority)

### Appeal to Authority
**Full Name**: Appeal to Authority (Argumentum ad Verecundiam)
**Description**: Treating a statement as true solely because an authority figure endorses it, without evaluating relevance or independent evidence. Fallacious when: the authority is not an expert in the relevant domain, experts disagree, or the authority's opinion substitutes entirely for evidence. Note: citing qualified experts IS legitimate — the fallacy is uncritical deference. Exploits **authority bias** and **social proof**.
**Documentation**: Appeal to Authority Term
**Related**: [Logical Fallacies](#logical-fallacies), [Ad Hominem](#ad-hominem), [Bandwagon](#bandwagon), [Groupthink](#groupthink)

### Red Herring
**Full Name**: Red Herring (Ignoratio Elenchi)
**Description**: Introducing an irrelevant topic to divert attention from the original issue, making the audience forget or abandon the original argument. "The model accuracy is low, but look at how fast inference is" redirects from accuracy to speed. Exploits **attention as a scarce resource** — once redirected, the original point is often lost. Particularly effective in group settings where conversational direction is hard to track.
**Documentation**: Red Herring Term
**Related**: [Logical Fallacies](#logical-fallacies), [Straw Man](#straw-man), [Tu Quoque](#tu-quoque), [WYSIATI](acronym_glossary_cognitive_science.md#wysiati)

### Tu Quoque
**Full Name**: Tu Quoque (Appeal to Hypocrisy / Whataboutism)
**Description**: Deflecting criticism by pointing out that the critic engages in the same behavior. "You can't criticize our testing — your team ships bugs too." A subtype of both **ad hominem** (attacks the critic's standing) and **red herring** (changes the subject). Exploits the psychological power of perceived hypocrisy — we intuitively feel hypocrites have no right to criticize, even though the truth of a criticism is independent of the critic's behavior.
**Documentation**: Tu Quoque Term
**Related**: [Logical Fallacies](#logical-fallacies), [Ad Hominem](#ad-hominem), [Red Herring](#red-herring), [Straw Man](#straw-man)

## Fallacies of Presumption

Premises presuppose what they claim to prove, or assume false constraints on the available options.

### False Dilemma
**Full Name**: False Dilemma (False Dichotomy / Either-Or Fallacy)
**Description**: Presenting only two options when more exist, forcing a choice between extremes while ignoring alternatives. "Either we ship now or we lose the market" eliminates options C, D, and E. Exploits the **framing effect** — constraining the choice set constrains the conclusion. **MECE decomposition** is the structural antidote — it requires enumerating ALL options, not just the two most salient.
**Documentation**: False Dilemma Term
**Related**: [Logical Fallacies](#logical-fallacies), [Framing Effect](acronym_glossary_cognitive_science.md#framing-effect), [MECE](acronym_glossary_cognitive_science.md#mece), [Slippery Slope](#slippery-slope)

### Circular Reasoning
**Full Name**: Circular Reasoning (Begging the Question / Petitio Principii)
**Description**: An argument where the conclusion is assumed in one of its premises — the argument proves nothing because it merely restates its starting assumption. "This is the right approach because it's the best way to do it" is circular — "right" and "best" are synonyms with no independent evidence. The circularity is often obscured by verbose restatement using enough complexity to hide the repetition.
**Documentation**: Circular Reasoning Term
**Related**: [Logical Fallacies](#logical-fallacies), [Socratic Questioning](#socratic-questioning), [Appeal to Authority](#appeal-to-authority)

## Fallacies of Induction

Conclusions drawn from insufficient, unrepresentative, or misinterpreted evidence — errors of generalization, causation, and extrapolation.

### Hasty Generalization
**Full Name**: Hasty Generalization (Faulty Generalization)
**Description**: Drawing a broad conclusion from a small, unrepresentative, or insufficient sample. Exploits the **law of small numbers** (Tversky & Kahneman, 1971) — people overestimate the reliability of conclusions from small samples. The informal-logic equivalent of drawing conclusions from underpowered experiments. **Simpson's Paradox** shows how even large samples can mislead if not properly decomposed by subgroup.
**Documentation**: Hasty Generalization Term
**Related**: [Logical Fallacies](#logical-fallacies), [Simpson's Paradox](acronym_glossary_statistics.md#simpsons-paradox), [Cognitive Bias](acronym_glossary_cognitive_science.md#cognitive-bias), [Post Hoc](#post-hoc)

### Slippery Slope
**Full Name**: Slippery Slope Fallacy
**Description**: Asserting that a small first step will inevitably lead to extreme consequences without providing evidence for each causal link in the chain. Exploits the **availability heuristic** (vivid worst-case scenarios feel more probable) and **narrative fallacy** (a coherent story from A to Z feels compelling without evidence for each link). The **Five Whys** technique applied in reverse tests each causal transition.
**Documentation**: Slippery Slope Term
**Related**: [Logical Fallacies](#logical-fallacies), [Five Whys](#five-whys), [False Dilemma](#false-dilemma), [Appeal to Emotion](#appeal-to-emotion)

### Bandwagon
**Full Name**: Bandwagon Fallacy (Argumentum ad Populum)
**Description**: Arguing that something is true or good because many people believe or do it. Exploits **social proof** and **conformity bias**. Particularly insidious because popularity sometimes IS a legitimate signal (widely-used frameworks have more support and battle-testing), making the invalid reasoning harder to detect. The bandwagon effect is one mechanism through which **groupthink** operates — a reinforcing feedback loop of conformity.
**Documentation**: Bandwagon Term
**Related**: [Logical Fallacies](#logical-fallacies), [Groupthink](#groupthink), [Appeal to Authority](#appeal-to-authority), [Cognitive Bias](acronym_glossary_cognitive_science.md#cognitive-bias)

### Post Hoc
**Full Name**: Post Hoc Ergo Propter Hoc (After This, Therefore Because of This)
**Description**: Assuming that because event B followed event A, A caused B. The most common analytical error in data-driven organizations — observational data always contains temporal sequences that look causal but may reflect confounders, regression to the mean, or coincidence. Judea Pearl's **Ladder of Causation** provides the framework: observational data (rung 1) can establish correlation but not causation; causal claims require intervention (rung 2) or counterfactual reasoning (rung 3). The **Five Whys** counteracts post hoc by demanding evidence for each causal link.
**Documentation**: Post Hoc Term
**Related**: [Logical Fallacies](#logical-fallacies), [Five Whys](#five-whys), [Hasty Generalization](#hasty-generalization), [Cognitive Bias](acronym_glossary_cognitive_science.md#cognitive-bias)

## Inquiry & Analytical Methods

Methods and frameworks for detecting fallacies, analyzing root causes, and preventing collective reasoning failures.

### Socratic Questioning
**Full Name**: Socratic Questioning (Socratic Method)
**Description**: Disciplined method of inquiry that systematically probes assumptions, evidence, implications, and alternative perspectives through targeted questions rather than direct instruction. Named after Socrates' dialectical method as recorded by Plato. Six categories of Socratic questions: clarification, probing assumptions, probing reasons/evidence, questioning viewpoints, exploring implications, and questions about the question itself. The method exposes hidden biases and deepens understanding by requiring the thinker to justify beliefs. Central to Burger & Starbird's "Air" element in *The 5 Elements of Effective Thinking*. Socratic questioning is the primary tool for exposing logical fallacies.
**Documentation**: [Socratic Questioning Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_socratic_questioning.md)
**Related**: [Logical Fallacies](#logical-fallacies), [Five Whys](#five-whys), [Groupthink](#groupthink), [Cognitive Bias](acronym_glossary_cognitive_science.md#cognitive-bias), [Elaborative Interrogation](acronym_glossary_cognitive_science.md#elaborative-interrogation)

### Five Whys
**Full Name**: Five Whys (5 Whys / Five Why Analysis)
**Description**: Root cause analysis technique that identifies the fundamental cause of a problem by asking "Why?" iteratively — typically five times — to peel back successive layers of symptoms until the underlying cause is exposed. Developed by **Sakichi Toyoda** and popularized by **Taiichi Ohno** in the **Toyota Production System**. Classic example: machine stopped → fuse blew → overload → bearing not lubricated → pump shaft worn → no strainer installed (root cause: install a strainer, not replace the fuse). Simple, fast, and accessible — requires no statistical tools or specialized training. Best for linear causal chains; for complex multi-causal problems, use Fishbone diagrams (Ishikawa) or fault tree analysis instead. The method is an instance of Socratic questioning applied to causal analysis — each "Why?" counteracts the narrative fallacy and post hoc reasoning.
**Documentation**: Five Whys Term
**Source**: Ohno, T. (1988). *Toyota Production System*; Hartley, M. (2024). *Critical Thinking Think Smarter*
**Related**: [Socratic Questioning](#socratic-questioning), [Logical Fallacies](#logical-fallacies), [Post Hoc](#post-hoc), [Slippery Slope](#slippery-slope), [Groupthink](#groupthink), [MECE](acronym_glossary_cognitive_science.md#mece), [Systems Thinking](acronym_glossary_cognitive_science.md#systems-thinking)

### Groupthink
**Full Name**: Groupthink
**Description**: Psychological phenomenon where a group's desire for harmony and consensus overrides realistic appraisal of alternatives, leading to irrational decision-making. Coined by **Irving Janis** (1972) who analyzed catastrophic policy failures — Bay of Pigs, Pearl Harbor, Vietnam escalation — and identified eight symptoms: illusion of invulnerability, collective rationalization, belief in inherent morality, stereotyping out-groups, pressure on dissenters, self-censorship, illusion of unanimity, and self-appointed mindguards. Prevention requires **structural safeguards**: devil's advocate roles, independent sub-teams, anonymous input methods (Delphi Method), and leaders speaking last. Groupthink amplifies individual cognitive biases through social reinforcement — confirmation bias, WYSIATI, anchoring, and overconfidence all intensify when group conformity pressure suppresses dissent.
**Documentation**: Groupthink Term
**Source**: Janis, I.L. (1972). *Victims of Groupthink*; Sunstein, C.R. & Hastie, R. (2015). *Wiser: Getting Beyond Groupthink to Make Groups Smarter*
**Related**: [Logical Fallacies](#logical-fallacies), [Bandwagon](#bandwagon), [Appeal to Authority](#appeal-to-authority), [Socratic Questioning](#socratic-questioning), [Five Whys](#five-whys), [Cognitive Bias](acronym_glossary_cognitive_science.md#cognitive-bias), [WYSIATI](acronym_glossary_cognitive_science.md#wysiati)

### Steelmanning
**Full Name**: Steelmanning (Steel Man Argument)
**Description**: The practice of constructing the strongest possible version of an opponent's argument before attempting to refute it — the deliberate inverse of the straw man fallacy. Three steps: restate the opponent's argument in terms they would endorse, strengthen any weak points by supplying the best available evidence, then address this strengthened version rather than a convenient caricature. **Engaging with the strongest version produces better outcomes on every dimension**: it builds credibility (audiences respect fairness), surfaces genuine weaknesses rather than phantom ones, and occasionally reveals the opponent is partially correct. Emerged from the LessWrong/Effective Altruism rationalist community as a prescriptive antidote to pervasive straw-manning in public discourse.
**Documentation**: Steelmanning Term
**Related**: [Straw Man](#straw-man), [Logical Fallacies](#logical-fallacies), [Socratic Questioning](#socratic-questioning), [Active Listening](#active-listening), [Persuasion](#persuasion), [Research Argument](#research-argument)

### Active Listening
**Full Name**: Active Listening (Reflective Listening)
**Description**: Communication technique in which the listener fully concentrates on, comprehends, and responds to a speaker — going beyond passive hearing to demonstrate engagement and ensure accurate understanding. Developed by psychologists **Carl Rogers** and **Richard Farson** (1957) as part of Rogers' person-centered therapy. Core components: full attention, reflective paraphrasing ("So what you're saying is..."), clarifying questions, nonverbal engagement, withholding judgment, and emotional validation. **Serves two simultaneous purposes**: ensures accurate understanding (reduces miscommunication) and makes the speaker feel heard (builds trust and rapport). In argumentation, active listening is a powerful persuasion tool — by genuinely understanding the other person's position before responding, the listener gains both information for counterarguments and relational goodwill.
**Documentation**: Active Listening Term
**Related**: [Socratic Questioning](#socratic-questioning), [Steelmanning](#steelmanning), [Persuasion](#persuasion), [Logical Fallacies](#logical-fallacies), [Cognitive Bias](acronym_glossary_cognitive_science.md#cognitive-bias)

### Dialectical Adequacy
**Full Name**: Dialectical Adequacy
**Description**: The property of an argument or warrant that has successfully survived all available counter-arguments. **An argument is dialectically adequate when every known challenge has been addressed** — by refuting the counter-argument, incorporating it as an exception, or revising the argument's conditions. Synthesizes Dung's acceptability (argument defends itself against all attackers), van Eemeren's pragma-dialectics (standpoint "withstood reasonable criticism"), and Popper's falsification (knowledge grows by surviving severe tests). Stronger than accuracy: an accurate argument may succeed by chance, while a dialectically adequate argument succeeds because every known failure has been diagnosed, documented, and repaired. In the DKS, warrants converge toward dialectical adequacy through iterative cycles of classification, disagreement, gap analysis, and rule repair.
**Documentation**: [Dialectical Adequacy](../resources/term_dictionary/term_dialectical_adequacy.md)
**Related**: [Argumentation](#research-argument), [DKS](acronym_glossary_cognitive_science.md#dks---dialectic-knowledge-system), [Socratic Questioning](#socratic-questioning), Knowledge Building Blocks — Counter-Argument

### Persuasion
**Full Name**: Persuasion (Rhetoric / Art of Influence)
**Description**: The process of changing or reinforcing attitudes, beliefs, or behaviors through communication — using reasoning, evidence, emotional appeals, or credibility rather than coercion. Rooted in **Aristotle's** *Rhetoric* (c. 350 BCE) with three modes: **ethos** (speaker credibility), **pathos** (emotional engagement), **logos** (logical structure). Modern extensions include **Cialdini's six principles of influence** (reciprocity, commitment/consistency, social proof, authority, liking, scarcity) and **Kahneman's dual-process theory** (System 1 responds to emotional appeals, System 2 to logical argumentation). **The ethical boundary**: persuasion respects autonomy and provides genuine reasons; manipulation exploits cognitive vulnerabilities without awareness. Most effective persuasion combines all three Aristotelian modes.
**Documentation**: Persuasion Term
**Related**: [Logical Fallacies](#logical-fallacies), [Appeal to Emotion](#appeal-to-emotion), [Appeal to Authority](#appeal-to-authority), [Steelmanning](#steelmanning), [Active Listening](#active-listening), [Cognitive Bias](acronym_glossary_cognitive_science.md#cognitive-bias), [Bandwagon](#bandwagon)

### Research Argument
**Full Name**: Research Argument (Five-Component Model / Modified Toulmin Framework)
**Description**: Argument model from Booth et al.'s *The Craft of Research* consisting of five components: **claim** (what you want readers to believe — must be substantive, contestable, specific), **reasons** (why they should believe it), **evidence** (data/facts — must be accurate, sufficient, representative, authoritative, clear), **warrants** (general principles connecting evidence to claims — often implicit, community-specific), and **acknowledgments & responses** (anticipated objections and replies). Central insight: **most argument failures are warrant failures** — the evidence may be correct, but the principle connecting it to the claim is unshared by the audience. Each logical fallacy maps to a specific component violation: fallacies of relevance break warrants, fallacies of presumption break claims/reasons, fallacies of induction break evidence.
**Documentation**: Research Argument Term
**Source**: Booth, W.C. et al. (2024). *The Craft of Research* (5th ed.), Chapters 5–9; Toulmin, S.E. (1958). *The Uses of Argument*
**Related**: [Logical Fallacies](#logical-fallacies), [Socratic Questioning](#socratic-questioning), [Research Problem Formula](#research-problem-formula), [Analytical Reading](acronym_glossary_cognitive_science.md#analytical-reading)

### Research Problem Formula
**Full Name**: Research Problem Formula (Topic-Question-Significance-Application Template)
**Description**: Four-part sentence template from Booth et al. that transforms vague interests into researchable problems: **"I am studying [TOPIC] because I want to find out [QUESTION] in order to help my reader understand [SIGNIFICANCE] so that they might [APPLICATION]."** The "in order to" clause is the critical innovation — it forces a "So what?" answer before research begins. Includes four narrowing lenses (parts/wholes, history/change, categories/characteristics, value/evaluation) for moving from topic to question. Complements Mullaney & Rea's self-centered research: Mullaney finds the problem from personal curiosity; Booth structures it for an audience.
**Documentation**: Research Problem Formula Term
**Source**: Booth, W.C. et al. (2024). *The Craft of Research* (5th ed.), Chapters 1–2
**Related**: [Socratic Questioning](#socratic-questioning), [Five Whys](#five-whys), [Research Argument](#research-argument), [Question Storming](acronym_glossary_cognitive_science.md#question-storming)

## Rhetorical Modes

Aristotle's three modes of persuasion — the foundational taxonomy for understanding how speakers and writers establish credibility, engage emotions, and construct logical arguments.

### Ethos
**Full Name**: Ethos (Speaker Credibility / Character)
**Description**: One of Aristotle's three modes of persuasion — the rhetorical appeal based on the speaker's credibility, character, and trustworthiness. Aristotle identified three components: **phronesis** (practical wisdom and expertise), **arete** (moral virtue and integrity), and **eunoia** (goodwill toward the audience). Ethos operates through the audience's perception of *who is speaking* rather than what is said (logos) or how it makes them feel (pathos). Ethos is earned through demonstrated competence, consistent honesty, and fair treatment of opposing views — not through self-declaration. It functions as a **force multiplier** for logos and pathos: the same argument from a trusted source is more persuasive than from an unknown one. Ethos becomes fallacious when it substitutes for evidence (appeal to authority) rather than complementing it.
**Documentation**: Ethos Term
**Source**: Aristotle. *Rhetoric* (c. 350 BCE)
**Related**: [Persuasion](#persuasion), [Logos](#logos), [Pathos](#pathos), [Appeal to Authority](#appeal-to-authority), [Steelmanning](#steelmanning), [Active Listening](#active-listening), [Ad Hominem](#ad-hominem)

### Logos
**Full Name**: Logos (Logical Argument / Rational Appeal)
**Description**: One of Aristotle's three modes of persuasion — the rhetorical appeal based on the **logical structure and rational evidence** within an argument. Logos persuades through clarity of reasoning, strength of evidence, and validity of inferences from premises to conclusion. Aristotle's core unit is the **enthymeme** — a syllogism with one premise left implicit because the audience already accepts it. Logos operates through both deductive reasoning (general principle to specific conclusion) and inductive reasoning (specific examples to general principle). What counts as "logical" is audience-relative — different discourse communities accept different warrants and evidence standards. Logos can be counterfeited through logical fallacies (circular reasoning, hasty generalization, false cause) — arguments that mimic valid reasoning but contain structural flaws.
**Documentation**: Logos Term
**Source**: Aristotle. *Rhetoric* (c. 350 BCE)
**Related**: [Persuasion](#persuasion), [Ethos](#ethos), [Pathos](#pathos), [Logical Fallacies](#logical-fallacies), [Research Argument](#research-argument), [Socratic Questioning](#socratic-questioning), [Circular Reasoning](#circular-reasoning)

### Pathos
**Full Name**: Pathos (Emotional Appeal / Audience Engagement)
**Description**: One of Aristotle's three modes of persuasion — the speaker's ability to evoke emotions in the audience to influence their judgment. Aristotle devoted *Rhetoric* Book II to a systematic catalogue of emotions (anger, calm, fear, confidence, pity, indignation, envy, emulation), analyzing each through three dimensions: the mental state of the person, the persons toward whom the emotion is directed, and the situations that give rise to it. This tripartite framework makes pathos a **structured, audience-aware rhetorical practice** — not raw emotional manipulation. Pathos engages Kahneman's System 1 (fast, intuitive), reducing analytical resistance and making the audience receptive to the speaker's framing. The critical boundary: pathos becomes the [appeal to emotion fallacy](#appeal-to-emotion) when emotional appeals **substitute for** rather than **complement** logical reasoning and evidence.
**Documentation**: Pathos Term
**Source**: Aristotle. *Rhetoric* (c. 350 BCE)
**Related**: [Persuasion](#persuasion), [Ethos](#ethos), [Logos](#logos), [Appeal to Emotion](#appeal-to-emotion), [Cognitive Bias](acronym_glossary_cognitive_science.md#cognitive-bias), [Active Listening](#active-listening), [Framing Effect](acronym_glossary_cognitive_science.md#framing-effect)

### Argumentation
**Full Name**: Argumentation (Argumentation Theory)
**Description**: The interdisciplinary study and practice of constructing, presenting, analyzing, and evaluating arguments in natural language contexts. Unlike formal deductive logic, argumentation addresses how people actually reason and resolve disagreements. Three landmark traditions define the field: **Toulmin's** six-part model (claim, grounds, warrant, backing, qualifier, rebuttal), **Perelman's** New Rhetoric (audience adherence and the universal audience), and **pragma-dialectics** (van Eemeren and Grootendorst's ten rules of critical discussion). Aristotle's tripartite foundation — dialectic (*Topics*), rhetoric (*Rhetoric*), and formal logic (*Prior Analytics*) — persists in all modern approaches. **Arguments are defeasible** (revisable by new evidence or stronger counter-arguments), audience-relative (success depends on shared warrants), and multi-modal (combining logos, ethos, and pathos).
**Documentation**: [Argumentation Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_argumentation.md)
**Source**: Aristotle; Toulmin (1958); Perelman (1958); van Eemeren & Grootendorst (2004)
**Related**: [Research Argument](#research-argument), [Persuasion](#persuasion), [Ethos](#ethos), [Logos](#logos), [Pathos](#pathos), [Logical Fallacies](#logical-fallacies), [Steelmanning](#steelmanning), [Socratic Questioning](#socratic-questioning)

## Computational Argumentation

Formal, machine-checkable structures that operationalise argumentation theory inside AI systems. These terms underpin recent work on explainable and contestable LLM-based reasoning (Freedman et al., AAAI 2025) and the broader argumentation-and-machine-learning literature (Rago et al., 2024).

### QBAF - Quantitative Bipolar Argumentation Framework
**Full Name**: Quantitative Bipolar Argumentation Framework
**Description**: A formal quadruple `<A, R-, R+, tau>` of arguments, attack edges, support edges, and a base-score function `tau: A -> [0,1]` used to represent and reason about networks of weighted, conflicting arguments. **QBAFs generalise Dung's (1995) abstract argumentation framework with a bipolar attack/support distinction (Cayrol & Lagasquie-Schiex 2005) and a quantitative base-score annotation (Baroni, Rago & Toni 2019).** A *gradual semantics* such as DF-QuAD aggregates each argument's base score with the dialectical contributions of its attackers and supporters into a deterministic strength `sigma(α) ∈ [0,1]`. QBAFs are the structured, contestable substrate underlying ArgLLMs (Freedman et al. 2025) and a wide range of explainable-AI, recommender, and claim-verification systems.
**Documentation**: [QBAF Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_qbaf.md)
**Source**: Baroni, Rago & Toni (2019); Cayrol & Lagasquie-Schiex (2005); Dung (1995)
**Related**: [DF-QuAD](#df-quad---discontinuity-free-quantitative-argumentation-debate), [Contestability](#contestability), [Argumentation](#argumentation), [Dialectical Adequacy](#dialectical-adequacy)

### DF-QuAD - Discontinuity-Free Quantitative Argumentation Debate
**Full Name**: Discontinuity-Free Quantitative Argumentation Debate
**Description**: A gradual semantics for QBAFs that maps a base score plus aggregated attacker and supporter strengths to a deterministic strength `sigma(α) ∈ [0,1]`. **The "discontinuity-free" name reflects its key design property — the output is a continuous function of the input base scores, fixing a smoothness defect of the earlier QuAD semantics.** The aggregation uses a noisy-OR-style combination `F(v_1,...,v_n) = 1 - prod(1-v_i)` over attackers and supporters, then asymmetrically scales the base score toward 0 (attackers dominate) or 1 (supporters dominate). DF-QuAD satisfies the standard Baroni-Rago-Toni principles for gradual semantics and, per Freedman et al. (2025), provably satisfies both Base Score and Argument Relation Contestability — making it the default semantics in ArgLLMs and many explainable-AI argumentation pipelines.
**Documentation**: [DF-QuAD Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_df_quad.md)
**Source**: Rago, Toni, Aurisicchio & Baroni (2016); Baroni, Rago & Toni (2019)
**Related**: [QBAF](#qbaf---quantitative-bipolar-argumentation-framework), [Contestability](#contestability), [Argumentation](#argumentation)

### Contestability
**Full Name**: Contestability (Contestable AI)
**Description**: The formal property of an AI system whereby an external agent can disagree with, intervene on, and **demonstrably alter** the system's decisions through targeted modifications to its internal reasoning artefacts. **Contestability is interactive and goes beyond explainability — it requires editable handles plus measurable, deterministic downstream effect on the verdict.** In the ArgLLM setting (Freedman et al. 2025), contestability is operationalised as two formal properties of a gradual semantics over a QBAF: *Base Score Contestability* (monotonic dependence of the verdict on each argument's base score) and *Argument Relation Contestability* (monotonic dependence on adding or removing attackers and supporters). DF-QuAD is proven to satisfy both, providing the engineering basis for human oversight, regulatory "right to challenge," and investigator-overrideable automated decisions.
**Documentation**: [Contestability Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_contestability.md)
**Source**: Freedman et al. (2025); Leofante, Ayoobi, Dejl & Toni (2024); Henin & Le Métayer (2022)
**Related**: [QBAF](#qbaf---quantitative-bipolar-argumentation-framework), [DF-QuAD](#df-quad---discontinuity-free-quantitative-argumentation-debate), [Dialectical Adequacy](#dialectical-adequacy), [Argumentation](#argumentation), [Steelmanning](#steelmanning)
