# Cognitive Science & Decision Frameworks Glossary

**Purpose**: Quick reference for cognitive science concepts, behavioral economics principles, learning science techniques, and problem-solving frameworks used in the vault's book digest notes. These terms provide the theoretical foundation for understanding human judgment, effective learning, and structured inquiry.

## Cognitive Psychology

### System 1 and System 2
**Full Name**: Dual-Process Theory of Cognition
**Description**: Daniel Kahneman's framework distinguishing two modes of thought: **System 1** operates fast, automatically, and intuitively with little effort or sense of voluntary control; **System 2** operates slowly, deliberately, and analytically, requiring focused attention and mental effort. Most cognitive biases arise from System 1's reliance on heuristics when System 2 fails to engage. Central organizing framework for understanding judgment and decision-making errors.
**Documentation**: [System 1 and System 2 Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_system_1_and_system_2.md)
**Source**: Kahneman, D. (2011). *Thinking, Fast and Slow*
**Related**: [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias), [WYSIATI](#wysiati), [Availability Heuristic](#availability-heuristic), [Anchoring](#anchoring)

### Cognitive Bias
**Full Name**: Cognitive Bias
**Description**: Systematic deviation from rationality in judgment and decision-making, caused by System 1 heuristics operating on incomplete information. Over 200 cognitive biases have been catalogued, including anchoring, availability, framing, confirmation bias, and many others. Understanding cognitive biases is essential for designing decision systems, evaluating evidence, and building analytical frameworks that counteract human judgment errors.
**Documentation**: [Cognitive Bias Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_cognitive_bias.md)
**Source**: Tversky, A. & Kahneman, D. (1974). "Judgment under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases"
**Related**: [System 1 and System 2](#system-1-and-system-2), [Anchoring](#anchoring), [Availability Heuristic](#availability-heuristic), [Framing Effect](#framing-effect), [WYSIATI](#wysiati)

### WYSIATI
**Full Name**: What You See Is All There Is
**Description**: Kahneman's acronym for the tendency of System 1 to construct the most coherent story possible from currently available information, drawing confident conclusions without considering what information might be missing. WYSIATI explains overconfidence, framing effects, and base-rate neglect — the mind does not flag absent evidence. Critical concept for any analytical system: if your data pipeline shows only partial information, analysts will form confident judgments as if the partial view is the whole picture.
**Documentation**: [WYSIATI Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_wysiati.md)
**Source**: Kahneman, D. (2011). *Thinking, Fast and Slow*, Chapter 7
**Related**: [System 1 and System 2](#system-1-and-system-2), [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias), [Availability Heuristic](#availability-heuristic), [Framing Effect](#framing-effect)

### Zeigarnik Effect
**Full Name**: Zeigarnik Effect
**Description**: The psychological phenomenon whereby incomplete or interrupted tasks are remembered better than completed ones. Discovered by **Bluma Zeigarnik** (1927), a doctoral student of Kurt Lewin, who showed that participants recalled interrupted tasks ~90% better than completed tasks. The mechanism is Lewin's **tension system**: starting a task creates a *quasi-need* that keeps it active in working memory until resolved. Baumeister & Masicampo (2011) showed that merely **making a concrete plan** discharges the tension — the brain treats a credible plan as equivalent to completion. The related **Ovsiankina Effect** (1928) describes the behavioral complement: the urge to *resume* interrupted tasks. The Zeigarnik Effect is the cognitive foundation of David Allen's "open loops" concept in GTD and underpins the Zettelkasten's fleeting note capture mechanism. A 2025 meta-analysis found the Ovsiankina Effect more robustly replicable than the classic memory advantage.
**Documentation**: [Zeigarnik Effect Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_zeigarnik_effect.md)
**Source**: Zeigarnik, B. (1927). "Über das Behalten von erledigten und unerledigten Handlungen." *Psychologische Forschung*, 9, 1–85; Masicampo, E.J. & Baumeister, R.F. (2011). "Consider It Done!" *JPSP*, 101(4), 667–683
**Related**: [Open Loops](#open-loops), [Trusted System](#trusted-system), [Flow State](#flow-state), [System 1 and System 2](#system-1-and-system-2), [Fleeting Notes](#fleeting-notes)

### Open Loops
**Full Name**: Open Loops (Zeigarnik Effect Applied to Productivity)
**Description**: Any commitment, task, idea, or piece of information that has your attention but has not been captured, clarified, and organized into a trusted external system. Coined by David Allen in *Getting Things Done* (2001). Grounded in the **Zeigarnik Effect** (1927) — incomplete tasks remain active in working memory, consuming cognitive resources even when not consciously attended to. Baumeister & Masicampo (2011) showed that merely making a concrete plan to complete a task reduces the Zeigarnik Effect — the brain treats a captured commitment similarly to a completed one. Open loops generate **extraneous cognitive load** (Sweller, 1988), reducing capacity for focused analytical work. The solution is complete externalization into a trusted system, not selective prioritization.
**Documentation**: [Open Loops Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_open_loops.md)
**Source**: Allen, D. (2015). *Getting Things Done*; Zeigarnik, B. (1927). "Über das Behalten von erledigten und unerledigten Handlungen"
**Related**: [System 1 and System 2](#system-1-and-system-2), [WYSIATI](#wysiati), [Trusted System](#trusted-system), [Flow State](#flow-state), [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias), [BASB](#basb)

**See also**: [Groupthink](acronym_glossary_critical_thinking.md#groupthink) — moved to [Critical Thinking Glossary](acronym_glossary_critical_thinking.md)

### Confirmation Bias
**Full Name**: Confirmation Bias
**Description**: The tendency to search for, interpret, favor, and recall information that confirms existing beliefs while filtering out contradictory evidence. **Considered the most pervasive and dangerous cognitive bias** because it creates self-reinforcing echo chambers — the more you believe something, the more evidence you find for it. Operates at every stage: selective search, biased interpretation of ambiguous evidence, and preferential recall of confirming memories.
**Documentation**: [Confirmation Bias Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_confirmation_bias.md)
**Source**: Wason, P.C. (1960). "On the failure to eliminate hypotheses in a conceptual task"; Nickerson, R.S. (1998). "Confirmation bias: A ubiquitous phenomenon in many guises." *Review of General Psychology*
**Related**: [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias), [WYSIATI](#wysiati), [Narrative Fallacy](#narrative-fallacy), [System 1 and System 2](#system-1-and-system-2)

### Survivorship Bias
**Full Name**: Survivorship Bias
**Description**: Systematic error of learning only from "survivors" (successes, visible cases) while ignoring the far larger population of failures, dropouts, or invisible cases. Produces wildly optimistic models of what leads to success. Classic example: studying successful entrepreneurs without examining those who applied identical strategies and failed. **The fundamental flaw in any analysis that draws conclusions from a non-random, filtered sample.Documentation**: [Survivorship Bias Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_survivorship_bias.md)
**Source**: Wald, A. (1943). WWII aircraft armor analysis; Dobelli, R. (2013). *The Art of Thinking Clearly*, Ch. 1
**Related**: [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias), [Base-Rate Neglect](#base-rate-neglect), [Narrative Fallacy](#narrative-fallacy), [Silent Evidence](#silent-evidence)

### Silent Evidence
**Full Name**: Silent Evidence (The Cemetery)
**Description**: The systematically invisible population of failures, near-misses, and unrealized possibilities that never enters our analysis because it failed to survive, was never recorded, or was never considered. Coined by **Nassim Nicholas Taleb (2007)** in *The Black Swan*, who also calls it "the cemetery." Silent evidence is broader than survivorship bias --- it encompasses not just filtered-out failures but also unconsidered possibilities and unexamined counterfactuals. **The bias is self-concealing: the very process that creates silent evidence (failure, destruction, non-publication) also ensures it cannot be observed.** Classic illustration: Diagoras asked "Where are the pictures of those who prayed, then drowned?" when shown portraits of shipwreck survivors who had prayed.
**Documentation**: [Silent Evidence Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_silent_evidence.md)
**Source**: Taleb, N.N. (2007). *The Black Swan*, Ch. 8: "Giacomo Casanova's Unfailing Luck"; Taleb, N.N. (2001). *Fooled by Randomness*, Ch. 11
**Related**: [Survivorship Bias](#survivorship-bias), [Narrative Fallacy](#narrative-fallacy), [Black Swan](#black-swan), [WYSIATI](#wysiati), [Base-Rate Neglect](#base-rate-neglect)

### Sunk Cost Fallacy
**Full Name**: Sunk Cost Fallacy (Concorde Effect)
**Description**: The irrational tendency to continue investing time, money, or effort into a failing endeavor because of resources already committed, even when future costs outweigh future benefits. Rational decision-making considers only prospective costs and benefits; past investments are irrecoverable and should be irrelevant. **Named after the Concorde supersonic jet, which both British and French governments continued funding despite knowing it would never be commercially viable.Documentation**: [Sunk Cost Fallacy Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_sunk_cost_fallacy.md)
**Source**: Arkes, H.R. & Blumer, C. (1985). "The psychology of sunk cost." *Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes*
**Related**: [Loss Aversion](#loss-aversion), [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias), [Commitment Device](#commitment-device)

### Hindsight Bias
**Full Name**: Hindsight Bias ("I Knew It All Along" Effect)
**Description**: The tendency to view past events as having been predictable and inevitable, despite having no objective basis for predicting them beforehand. After learning an outcome, people systematically overestimate the probability they would have assigned to it in advance. **Distorts learning from experience** by making both successes and failures seem more foreseeable than they were, encouraging false confidence in future predictions.
**Documentation**: [Hindsight Bias Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_hindsight_bias.md)
**Source**: Fischhoff, B. (1975). "Hindsight ≠ foresight: The effect of outcome knowledge on judgment under uncertainty"
**Related**: [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias), [Overconfidence Effect](#overconfidence-effect), [Outcome Bias](#outcome-bias), [Narrative Fallacy](#narrative-fallacy)

### Overconfidence Effect
**Full Name**: Overconfidence Effect
**Description**: Systematic tendency to overestimate one's knowledge, predictive accuracy, and ability to control outcomes. Manifests in three forms: (1) **overestimation** of actual performance, (2) **overplacement** relative to others, and (3) **overprecision** in the accuracy of beliefs (too-narrow confidence intervals). **Experts are more prone than novices**, because expertise in one domain creates an illusion of competence in adjacent domains.
**Documentation**: [Overconfidence Effect Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_overconfidence_effect.md)
**Source**: Lichtenstein, S., Fischhoff, B., & Phillips, L.D. (1982). "Calibration of probabilities"; Moore, D.A. & Healy, P.J. (2008)
**Related**: [Planning Fallacy](#planning-fallacy), [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias), [WYSIATI](#wysiati), [Illusion of Control](#illusion-of-control)

### Social Proof
**Full Name**: Social Proof (Informational Social Influence)
**Description**: The psychological tendency to look to others' behavior as a guide for one's own, especially in situations of uncertainty. When people are unsure what to do, they follow the crowd — assuming that if many people are doing something, it must be correct. Exploited in marketing (bestseller lists, customer reviews), compliance techniques, and social engineering. **The fact that many people believe something provides zero evidence of its correctness.Documentation**: [Social Proof Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_social_proof.md)
**Source**: Cialdini, R.B. (1984). *Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion*; Asch, S.E. (1956). Conformity experiments
**Related**: [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias), [Groupthink](acronym_glossary_critical_thinking.md#groupthink), [Authority Bias](#authority-bias)

### Halo Effect
**Full Name**: Halo Effect
**Description**: Cognitive bias where a positive impression in one domain (e.g., physical attractiveness, brand reputation) unconsciously influences evaluation in unrelated domains (e.g., competence, trustworthiness). First described by Thorndike (1920) in military officer evaluations. **One salient positive trait creates a "halo" that biases all subsequent judgments**, making the halo effect a major source of error in interviews, performance reviews, and product evaluations.
**Documentation**: [Halo Effect Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_halo_effect.md)
**Source**: Thorndike, E.L. (1920). "A constant error in psychological ratings"; Nisbett, R.E. & Wilson, T.D. (1977)
**Related**: [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias), [Fundamental Attribution Error](#fundamental-attribution-error), [Contrast Effect](#contrast-effect)

### Narrative Fallacy
**Full Name**: Narrative Fallacy (Story Bias)
**Description**: The human compulsion to construct coherent causal stories from sequences of events, even when the events are random or only loosely connected. Stories impose a false sense of understanding, predictability, and inevitability on the past. **The most dangerous feature of the narrative fallacy is that it makes the world seem more predictable than it is**, encouraging overconfidence in predictions and underappreciation of luck and randomness.
**Documentation**: [Narrative Fallacy Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_narrative_fallacy.md)
**Source**: Taleb, N.N. (2007). *The Black Swan*, Ch. 6; Kahneman, D. (2011). *Thinking, Fast and Slow*
**Related**: [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias), [Hindsight Bias](#hindsight-bias), [WYSIATI](#wysiati), [Survivorship Bias](#survivorship-bias)

### Fundamental Attribution Error
**Full Name**: Fundamental Attribution Error (Correspondence Bias)
**Description**: The systematic tendency to overweight dispositional (character, personality) explanations for others' behavior while underweighting situational (context, circumstances) explanations. When someone fails, we attribute it to their character; when we fail, we attribute it to circumstances. **This asymmetry distorts performance evaluation, root cause analysis, and any system where humans judge other humans' actions.Documentation**: [Fundamental Attribution Error Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_fundamental_attribution_error.md)
**Source**: Ross, L. (1977). "The intuitive psychologist and his shortcomings"; Jones, E.E. & Harris, V.A. (1967)
**Related**: [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias), [Self-Serving Bias](#self-serving-bias), [Halo Effect](#halo-effect)

### Green Lumber Fallacy
**Full Name**: Green Lumber Fallacy (Green Lumber Problem)
**Description**: The mistake of confusing one type of knowledge for another — specifically, assuming that the knowledge which appears most important for success in a domain is what actually drives practical results. Introduced by **Nassim Nicholas Taleb (2012)** in *Antifragile*, named after trader Joe Siegel who made a fortune trading "green lumber" while thinking it was lumber painted green (it actually means freshly cut, unseasoned wood). **The knowledge that matters for practical success is often NOT the knowledge that seems most important intellectually** — Siegel's success depended on reading order flow and managing risk, not on knowing what green lumber physically was. Maps onto Aristotle's distinction between phronesis (practical wisdom) and episteme (theoretical knowledge).
**Documentation**: [Green Lumber Fallacy Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_green_lumber_fallacy.md)
**Source**: Taleb, N.N. (2012). *Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder*; Paul, J. & Moynihan, B. (1994). *What I Learned Losing a Million Dollars*
**Related**: [Narrative Fallacy](#narrative-fallacy), [Black Swan](#black-swan), [Ludic Fallacy](#ludic-fallacy), [Platonicity](#platonicity), [Survivorship Bias](#survivorship-bias), [Overconfidence Effect](#overconfidence-effect), [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias)

### Cognitive Dissonance
**Full Name**: Cognitive Dissonance
**Description**: The psychological discomfort experienced when holding two contradictory beliefs, or when behavior conflicts with beliefs. People are strongly motivated to reduce this discomfort — typically by changing beliefs to match behavior rather than changing behavior to match beliefs. **Explains why people double down on failing strategies** (changing their assessment of the strategy's merit rather than abandoning it) and why post-purchase rationalization is universal.
**Documentation**: [Cognitive Dissonance Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_cognitive_dissonance.md)
**Source**: Festinger, L. (1957). *A Theory of Cognitive Dissonance*
**Related**: [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias), [Confirmation Bias](#confirmation-bias), [Sunk Cost Fallacy](#sunk-cost-fallacy), [Self-Serving Bias](#self-serving-bias)

### Status Quo Bias
**Full Name**: Status Quo Bias
**Description**: Systematic preference for the current state of affairs, where any deviation from the baseline is perceived as a loss. Discovered by **Samuelson and Zeckhauser (1988)** through experiments showing people disproportionately stick with default options. Driven by loss aversion (changes carry perceived losses), endowment effect (overvaluing what we already have), and mere exposure (familiarity breeds preference). Explains low organ donation rates under opt-in systems and employee inertia in retirement plan enrollment.
**Documentation**: [Status Quo Bias Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_status_quo_bias.md)
**Source**: Samuelson, W. & Zeckhauser, R. (1988). "Status quo bias in decision making." *Journal of Risk and Uncertainty*
**Related**: [Loss Aversion](#loss-aversion), [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias), [Choice Architecture](#choice-architecture), [Anchoring](#anchoring)

### Omission Bias
**Full Name**: Omission Bias
**Description**: The tendency to judge harmful actions (commissions) as morally worse than equally harmful omissions, even when the outcomes are identical. **Complementary to action bias** — omission bias is the moral judgment, action bias is the behavioral response. Spranca, Minsk & Baron (1991) demonstrated that people rate a person who poisons someone as more culpable than one who withholds an antidote, even when both result in death. Drives vaccine hesitancy, reluctance to withdraw medical treatment, and passive complicity in organizational failures.
**Documentation**: [Omission Bias Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_omission_bias.md)
**Source**: Spranca, M., Minsk, E., & Baron, J. (1991). "Omission and commission in judgment and choice." *Journal of Experimental Social Psychology*
**Related**: [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias), [Status Quo Bias](#status-quo-bias), [Loss Aversion](#loss-aversion)

### Paradox of Choice
**Full Name**: Paradox of Choice (Choice Overload)
**Description**: The counterintuitive finding that increasing the number of available options can decrease satisfaction and increase decision paralysis. **Barry Schwartz (2004)** synthesized research showing that beyond a threshold, more choices lead to analysis paralysis, regret amplification, and unrealistic expectations. Iyengar and Lepper's (2000) "jam study" found that a display of 24 jam flavors attracted more browsers but produced only 1/10th the purchases of a 6-flavor display. Distinguishes "maximizers" (who seek the best) from "satisficers" (who seek good enough).
**Documentation**: [Paradox of Choice Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_paradox_of_choice.md)
**Source**: Schwartz, B. (2004). *The Paradox of Choice: Why More Is Less*
**Related**: [Decision Fatigue](#decision-fatigue), [Status Quo Bias](#status-quo-bias), [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias)

### Mental Accounting
**Full Name**: Mental Accounting
**Description**: The cognitive process of categorizing, evaluating, and tracking financial activities in separate mental "accounts" rather than treating money as fungible. **Richard Thaler (1985, 1999)** demonstrated that people treat a $20 theater ticket differently from a $20 bill — losing the ticket deters attendance while losing the bill does not, even though both represent $20 losses. Creates systematic violations of economic rationality: people maintain expensive credit card debt alongside low-interest savings, and treat windfalls differently from earned income.
**Documentation**: [Mental Accounting Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_mental_accounting.md)
**Source**: Thaler, R.H. (1999). "Mental Accounting Matters." *Journal of Behavioral Decision Making*
**Related**: [Prospect Theory](#prospect-theory), [Loss Aversion](#loss-aversion), [Framing Effect](#framing-effect), [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias)

### Mental Model
**Full Name**: Mental Model
**Description**: An internal cognitive representation of external reality that the mind constructs to understand, predict, and reason about systems and processes. **Kenneth Craik (1943)** coined the term in *The Nature of Explanation*, proposing that the mind builds "small-scale models" of reality to anticipate events through a three-step process of translation, derivation, and re-translation. **Peter Senge (1990)** extended the concept to organizational learning, defining mental models as deeply ingrained assumptions that influence understanding and action -- and argued that surfacing and scrutinizing them is essential for learning organizations. **Donella Meadows** emphasized exposing mental models "to the light of day" and testing them against evidence as a core practice of systems thinking.
**Documentation**: [Mental Model Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_mental_model.md)
**Source**: Craik, K. (1943). *The Nature of Explanation*; Senge, P. (1990). *The Fifth Discipline*
**Related**: [Systems Thinking](#systems-thinking), [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias), [WYSIATI](#wysiati), [Confirmation Bias](#confirmation-bias)

### Antilibrary
**Full Name**: Antilibrary
**Description**: A personal collection of unread books that serves as a deliberate reminder of the vast territory of one's ignorance, rather than a display of erudition. Popularized by **Nassim Nicholas Taleb (2007)** in *The Black Swan*, inspired by Umberto Eco's 30,000-volume library (mostly unread). **The core insight is that unread books are more valuable than read ones because they map the boundaries of what you do not know.** A growing antilibrary signals intellectual humility -- the Socratic recognition that knowledge expansion reveals ever-larger domains of ignorance. Taleb's ideal practitioner, the "antischolar," treats unread books as tools for navigating Extremistan rather than trophies of learning.
**Documentation**: [Antilibrary Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_antilibrary.md)
**Source**: Taleb, N.N. (2007). *The Black Swan: The Impact of the Highly Improbable*, Chapter 1
**Related**: [Black Swan](#black-swan), [Narrative Fallacy](#narrative-fallacy), [Silent Evidence](#silent-evidence), [Confirmation Bias](#confirmation-bias), [Socratic Questioning](../resources/term_dictionary/term_socratic_questioning.md)

### Black Swan
**Full Name**: Black Swan (Taleb)
**Description**: A high-impact, low-probability event that is (1) an outlier beyond normal expectations, (2) carries extreme consequences, and (3) is retrospectively rationalized as if it were predictable. **Nassim Nicholas Taleb (2007)** argued that Black Swans drive most historical, scientific, and financial developments, yet our cognitive architecture (narrative fallacy, hindsight bias) blinds us to their possibility before they occur and makes them seem obvious afterward. Key implication: robust systems should be designed to survive Black Swans rather than predict them.
**Documentation**: [Black Swan Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_black_swan.md)
**Source**: Taleb, N.N. (2007). *The Black Swan: The Impact of the Highly Improbable*
**Related**: [Narrative Fallacy](#narrative-fallacy), [Survivorship Bias](#survivorship-bias), [Hindsight Bias](#hindsight-bias), [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias), [Platonicity](#platonicity), [Ludic Fallacy](#ludic-fallacy), [Antilibrary](#antilibrary)

### Platonicity
**Full Name**: Platonicity (The Platonic Fold)
**Description**: Taleb's term for the human tendency to mistake the map for the territory -- to prefer clean, elegant theoretical models and categories over the messy complexity of reality. Named after Plato's theory of ideal Forms, Platonicity describes the gap between what we know (our models) and what we think we know (our confidence in those models). **The "Platonic fold" is the dangerous boundary where the model diverges from reality, and it is precisely where Black Swan events are produced.** The concept extends Korzybski's "the map is not the territory" into a systematic cognitive bias that drives overconfidence, Gaussian blindness, and category reification.
**Documentation**: [Platonicity Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_platonicity.md)
**Source**: Taleb, N.N. (2007). *The Black Swan: The Impact of the Highly Improbable*
**Related**: [Black Swan](#black-swan), [Narrative Fallacy](#narrative-fallacy), [Ludic Fallacy](#ludic-fallacy), [WYSIATI](#wysiati), [Mental Model](#mental-model)

### Ludic Fallacy
**Full Name**: Ludic Fallacy (from Latin *ludus* = game)
**Description**: The error of applying the clean, well-defined uncertainty of games and mathematical models to the messy, unbounded uncertainty of real life. Coined by **Nassim Nicholas Taleb (2007)** in *The Black Swan*, the term captures the fundamental mismatch between closed-world probability (where rules, outcomes, and distributions are known) and open-world uncertainty (where you do not know what you do not know). **In games you know the odds; in real life you don't know the odds and you don't know the full set of possible outcomes.** The ludic fallacy is a specific instance of [Platonicity](#platonicity) -- preferring neat abstractions over messy reality -- and is the cognitive mechanism that blinds us to Black Swan events.
**Documentation**: [Ludic Fallacy Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_ludic_fallacy.md)
**Source**: Taleb, N.N. (2007). *The Black Swan: The Impact of the Highly Improbable*
**Related**: [Black Swan](#black-swan), [Platonicity](#platonicity), [Narrative Fallacy](#narrative-fallacy), [Overconfidence Effect](#overconfidence-effect), [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias), [Gambler's Fallacy](../resources/term_dictionary/term_gambler_fallacy.md)

### Domain Dependence
**Full Name**: Domain Dependence
**Description**: The inability to transfer knowledge, insight, or reasoning from one domain (where it was learned) to another domain where it is equally applicable. Coined by **Nassim Nicholas Taleb (2012)** in *Antifragile*, the concept explains why people who understand a principle in one context systematically fail to recognize the identical structure in a different context. **The mechanism is rooted in the brain's modular architecture -- knowledge is stored in context-bound cognitive modules that do not communicate across domains.** Domain dependence worsens with expertise (the deeper the specialization, the tighter the binding) and explains the persistent gap between classroom learning and real-world application.
**Documentation**: [Domain Dependence Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_domain_dependence.md)
**Source**: Taleb, N.N. (2012). *Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder*
**Related**: [Ludic Fallacy](#ludic-fallacy), [Narrative Fallacy](#narrative-fallacy), [Platonicity](#platonicity), [Black Swan](#black-swan), [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias), [Framing Effect](#framing-effect), [System 1 and System 2](#system-1-and-system-2)

### Epistemic Arrogance
**Full Name**: Epistemic Arrogance
**Description**: Nassim Nicholas Taleb's term for the systematic tendency to overestimate what we know relative to what we actually know, introduced in *The Black Swan* (2007). The core mechanism is a dual failure: **overestimating the scope of our knowledge while simultaneously underestimating the space of uncertainty**, compressing confidence intervals far too narrowly. Taleb distinguishes "competent arrogants" (experts in domains with good feedback, like surgery) from "incompetent arrogants" (experts in domains with poor feedback, like macroeconomics). The antidote is epistemic humility --- Taleb's ideal of the "epistemocrat" who holds their own knowledge in greatest suspicion.
**Documentation**: [Epistemic Arrogance Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_epistemic_arrogance.md)
**Source**: Taleb, N.N. (2007). *The Black Swan: The Impact of the Highly Improbable*
**Related**: [Overconfidence Effect](#overconfidence-effect), [WYSIATI](#wysiati), [Narrative Fallacy](#narrative-fallacy), [Black Swan](#black-swan), [Platonicity](#platonicity), [Ludic Fallacy](#ludic-fallacy)

### Hedonic Treadmill
**Full Name**: Hedonic Treadmill (Hedonic Adaptation)
**Description**: The observed tendency of humans to return to a relatively stable level of happiness despite major positive or negative life events or changes. **Brickman and Campbell (1971)** coined the term; Brickman, Coates & Janoff-Bulman (1978) showed that lottery winners were not significantly happier than controls, and paraplegics not as unhappy as expected. Explains why material gains produce diminishing returns on happiness and why lifestyle inflation fails to improve well-being. The Easterlin Paradox (1974) extended this to national economic growth.
**Documentation**: [Hedonic Treadmill Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_hedonic_treadmill.md)
**Source**: Brickman, P. & Campbell, D.T. (1971). "Hedonic relativism and planning the good society"
**Related**: [Loss Aversion](#loss-aversion), [Contrast Effect](#contrast-effect), [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias)

### EI / EQ - Emotional Intelligence
**Full Name**: Emotional Intelligence (Emotional Quotient)
**Description**: The ability to perceive, use, understand, manage, and handle emotions — both one's own and those of others. Formally defined by **Peter Salovey and John D. Mayer (1990)** as a cognitive ability, popularized by **Daniel Goleman (1995)**, and operationalized for workplace application by **Travis Bradberry and Jean Greaves (2009)**. The Bradberry-Greaves four-skill model organizes EQ into two competence domains: personal competence (self-awareness + self-management) and social competence (social awareness + relationship management). TalentSmart research across 500,000+ individuals found EQ accounts for 58% of job performance — the single strongest predictor, outperforming IQ and technical skills. Unlike IQ (largely fixed) and personality (stable), EQ is a flexible, trainable skill; neuroplasticity allows the brain to strengthen the communication pathway between the limbic system and prefrontal cortex through deliberate practice of emotional regulation strategies.
**Documentation**: [Emotional Intelligence](../resources/term_dictionary/term_emotional_intelligence.md)
**Source**: Salovey, P. & Mayer, J.D. (1990). *Imagination, Cognition and Personality*; Goleman, D. (1995). *Emotional Intelligence*; Bradberry, T. & Greaves, J. (2009). *Emotional Intelligence 2.0*

### Self-Awareness (EQ)
**Full Name**: Self-Awareness (Emotional Intelligence)
**Description**: The ability to accurately perceive your own emotions in the moment and understand your tendencies across situations. Identified by Daniel Goleman (1995) as the first component of emotional intelligence and positioned by Bradberry and Greaves (2009) as the foundational skill upon which all other EQ skills depend — you cannot manage emotions you do not recognize, read others without understanding your own emotional lens, or navigate relationships without self-knowledge. TalentSmart research found that only 36% of people can accurately identify their emotions as they happen, revealing a massive trainable gap. A separate study by Tasha Eurich (2018) found that while 95% of people believe they are self-aware, only 10-15% meet empirical criteria.
**Documentation**: [Self-Awareness](../resources/term_dictionary/term_self_awareness.md)
**Source**: Bradberry, T. & Greaves, J. (2009). *Emotional Intelligence 2.0*; Goleman, D. (1995). *Emotional Intelligence*
**Related**: [Growth Mindset](#growth-mindset), [Mental Model](#mental-model), [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias)

### Self-Management (EQ)
**Full Name**: Self-Management (Emotional Intelligence)
**Description**: The ability to use awareness of your emotions to stay flexible and positively direct your behavior. Self-management depends directly on self-awareness — you cannot manage what you do not notice. It is not the suppression of emotions but the conscious regulation of emotional reactions so that they serve rather than sabotage your goals. Bradberry and Greaves (2009) identify 17 actionable strategies including counting to 10, breathing techniques, sleep optimization, and self-talk management; Goleman's original model (1995) used the term "self-regulation" and identified sub-competencies of emotional self-control, transparency, adaptability, achievement orientation, and optimism.
**Documentation**: [Self-Management](../resources/term_dictionary/term_self_management.md)
**Source**: Bradberry, T. & Greaves, J. (2009). *Emotional Intelligence 2.0*; Goleman, D. (1995). *Emotional Intelligence*
**Related**: [Self-Awareness (EQ)](#self-awareness-eq), [Habit Loop](#habit-loop), [Grit](#grit), [System 1 and System 2](#system-1-and-system-2)

### Social Awareness (EQ)
**Full Name**: Social Awareness (Emotional Intelligence)
**Description**: The ability to accurately pick up on the emotions of other people and understand what is really going on with them. Requires self-awareness as its foundation — you cannot read others accurately without first understanding your own emotional lens. The core practice is empathic listening without judgment, attending to words, tone, facial expressions, and body language simultaneously. **Daniel Goleman** (1995) identifies empathy as the central component, defining it at three levels: perceiving others' feelings, sensing unspoken concerns, and understanding the issues behind another's feelings. **Bradberry and Greaves** (2009) position social awareness as the third of four learnable EQ skills and the first outward-facing competency, emphasizing that 55% of communication is nonverbal and that socially aware individuals read body language, catch the mood of a room, and recognize the right timing for sensitive conversations.
**Documentation**: [Social Awareness](../resources/term_dictionary/term_social_awareness.md)
**Source**: Bradberry, T. & Greaves, J. (2009). *Emotional Intelligence 2.0*; Goleman, D. (1995). *Emotional Intelligence*

### Amygdala Hijack
**Full Name**: Amygdala Hijack (Emotional Hijacking)
**Description**: An immediate, intense, and disproportionate emotional response triggered by the amygdala before the prefrontal cortex can intervene with rational processing. Coined by **Daniel Goleman (1995)** in *Emotional Intelligence*, the concept draws on Joseph LeDoux's discovery of dual neural pathways: a fast "low road" (thalamus to amygdala, ~12ms) that triggers fight-flight-freeze responses, and a slow "high road" (thalamus to cortex to amygdala, ~30-40ms) that provides contextual evaluation. During the temporal gap between these pathways, the amygdala can initiate a full physiological cascade — adrenaline, cortisol, elevated heart rate, tunnel vision — before conscious awareness determines whether the threat is real. The mechanism evolved for physical survival but fires identically for social threats (criticism, rejection, perceived disrespect), making it a primary source of disproportionate emotional reactions in modern environments.

**Source**: Goleman, D. (1995). *Emotional Intelligence*; LeDoux, J. (1996). *The Emotional Brain*

## Behavioral Economics

### Antifragility
**Full Name**: Antifragility
**Description**: A property of systems that increase in capability as a result of stressors, shocks, volatility, and disorder, introduced by Nassim Nicholas Taleb in *Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder* (2012). The mathematical core is **convex payoff asymmetry** -- antifragile systems have more upside than downside from random events. Taleb's Fragile-Robust-Antifragile triad classifies all systems by their response to volatility: fragile systems break, robust systems resist, and antifragile systems improve. Key heuristics include the barbell strategy, via negativa, optionality, and the Lindy effect. Examples include biological evolution, adaptive immune systems, startup ecosystems, and chaos engineering practices.
**Documentation**: [Antifragility](../resources/term_dictionary/term_antifragility.md)
**Related**: [Prospect Theory](#prospect-theory), [Loss Aversion](#loss-aversion), [Emergence](../resources/term_dictionary/term_emergence.md), [Feedback Loop](../resources/term_dictionary/term_feedback_loop.md), [Circle of Influence](#circle-of-influence)

### Prospect Theory
**Full Name**: Prospect Theory
**Description**: Descriptive model of decision-making under risk (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979) that replaced expected utility theory as the dominant behavioral model. Key principles: (1) outcomes are evaluated relative to a **reference point**, not in absolute terms; (2) the value function is **concave for gains** (risk-averse) and **convex for losses** (risk-seeking); (3) **losses are weighted ~2x more than equivalent gains** (loss aversion). Won Kahneman the 2002 Nobel Prize in Economics.
**Documentation**: [Prospect Theory Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_prospect_theory.md)
**Source**: Kahneman, D. & Tversky, A. (1979). "Prospect Theory: An Analysis of Decision under Risk." *Econometrica*, 47(2), 263–291
**Related**: [Loss Aversion](#loss-aversion), [Framing Effect](#framing-effect), [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias), [Peak-End Rule](#peak-end-rule)

### Loss Aversion
**Full Name**: Loss Aversion
**Description**: The empirical finding that losses are psychologically weighted approximately 1.5–2.5x more heavily than equivalent gains. A pillar of prospect theory, loss aversion explains the endowment effect (overvaluing what you already own), status quo bias (preferring current state), and sunk cost fallacy (continuing investments due to prior losses). In customer behavior analysis, loss aversion shapes how customers frame refund/return decisions and how they respond to policy changes.
**Documentation**: [Loss Aversion Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_loss_aversion.md)
**Source**: Kahneman, D. & Tversky, A. (1979). "Prospect Theory"; Kahneman, D. (2011). *Thinking, Fast and Slow*
**Related**: [Prospect Theory](#prospect-theory), [Framing Effect](#framing-effect), [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias)

### Anchoring
**Full Name**: Anchoring Effect (Anchoring and Adjustment Heuristic)
**Description**: Cognitive bias where initial exposure to a number or value disproportionately influences subsequent judgments and estimates, even when the anchor is arbitrary or irrelevant. Demonstrated in Tversky & Kahneman's (1974) seminal experiments where spinning a rigged wheel of fortune influenced estimates of African nations in the UN. Anchoring affects negotiations, pricing, sentencing, risk assessment, and any domain where numerical estimates are made after exposure to reference values.
**Documentation**: [Anchoring Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_anchoring.md)
**Source**: Tversky, A. & Kahneman, D. (1974). "Judgment under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases." *Science*, 185, 1124–1131
**Related**: [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias), [System 1 and System 2](#system-1-and-system-2), [Availability Heuristic](#availability-heuristic), [Framing Effect](#framing-effect)

### Anchoring Effect
**Full Name**: Anchoring Effect (Anchoring Bias)
**Description**: Cognitive bias where individuals rely too heavily on the first piece of information encountered when making judgments. In operations, affects investigators anchoring on initial risk scores and ML threshold setting anchoring on historical targets. **Mitigated by structured decision frameworks and multiple independent assessments.Documentation**: [Anchoring Effect Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_anchoring_effect.md)
**Related**: [Sugar Index](../resources/term_dictionary/term_sugar_index.md), [ARI](../resources/term_dictionary/term_ari.md)

### Availability Heuristic
**Full Name**: Availability Heuristic
**Description**: Mental shortcut where the ease of recalling examples from memory substitutes for objective assessment of frequency or probability. Events that are vivid, recent, emotionally charged, or media-prominent are judged as more likely than they are. First described by Tversky & Kahneman (1973). In analytical contexts, the availability heuristic causes investigators to overweight recent or dramatic cases when estimating base rates, leading to systematically skewed risk assessments.
**Documentation**: [Availability Heuristic Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_availability_heuristic.md)
**Source**: Tversky, A. & Kahneman, D. (1973). "Availability: A Heuristic for Judging Frequency and Probability." *Cognitive Psychology*, 5(2), 207–232
**Related**: [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias), [System 1 and System 2](#system-1-and-system-2), [WYSIATI](#wysiati)

### Barbell Strategy
**Full Name**: Barbell Strategy
**Description**: A risk management approach popularized by Nassim Nicholas Taleb that combines extreme safety with extreme speculation while avoiding moderate-risk positions entirely. The typical allocation places 85-90% in ultra-safe instruments and 10-15% in high-convexity speculative bets. **The core insight is that medium risk gives the worst of both worlds -- vulnerable to Black Swan events with limited upside.** The strategy creates a positively skewed return profile that benefits from volatility rather than being harmed by it. Beyond finance, the barbell principle extends to career strategy, knowledge acquisition, and life decisions.
**Documentation**: [Barbell Strategy Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_barbell_strategy.md)
**Related**: [Loss Aversion](#loss-aversion), [Prospect Theory](#prospect-theory), [Commitment Device](#commitment-device), [Narrative Fallacy](../resources/term_dictionary/term_narrative_fallacy.md), [Circle of Influence](#circle-of-influence)

### Framing Effect
**Full Name**: Framing Effect
**Description**: Cognitive bias where identical options or outcomes described in different ways produce systematically different choices. Tversky & Kahneman's (1981) "Asian Disease Problem" showed that describing outcomes as "200 saved" vs. "400 will die" (same scenario) flipped preferences from risk-averse to risk-seeking. Framing effects demonstrate that human preferences are constructed during the choice process, not merely revealed. Critical for understanding how question wording, policy presentation, and information display shape decisions.
**Documentation**: [Framing Effect Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_framing_effect.md)
**Source**: Tversky, A. & Kahneman, D. (1981). "The Framing of Decisions and the Psychology of Choice." *Science*, 211, 453–458
**Related**: [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias), [Prospect Theory](#prospect-theory), [Loss Aversion](#loss-aversion), [WYSIATI](#wysiati)

### Kano Model
**Full Name**: Kano Model (Attractive Quality Theory)
**Description**: A product development and customer satisfaction framework developed by Noriaki Kano in 1984 that classifies features by their nonlinear impact on satisfaction. The model identifies five categories: must-be (basic expectations that only prevent dissatisfaction), one-dimensional (performance features with linear satisfaction), attractive (delighters that create disproportionate satisfaction), indifferent, and reverse. **The key insight is that must-have features can never create satisfaction, only prevent dissatisfaction — an asymmetry rooted in loss aversion and hedonic adaptation.** Features decay over time from delighters to performance to must-haves as customer expectations rise and competitors adopt similar offerings.
**Documentation**: [Kano Model](../resources/term_dictionary/term_kano_model.md)
**Related**: [Loss Aversion](#loss-aversion), [Hedonic Treadmill](../resources/term_dictionary/term_hedonic_treadmill.md), [Choice Architecture](#choice-architecture), [Peak-End Rule](#peak-end-rule)

### Lindy Effect
**Full Name**: Lindy Effect (Lindy's Law)
**Description**: A theorized phenomenon by which the future life expectancy of a non-perishable entity -- such as a technology, idea, or book -- is proportional to its current age. Named after Lindy's delicatessen in New York City, mathematically formalized by Benoit Mandelbrot in 1982, and extended to all non-perishable entities by Nassim Nicholas Taleb in *Antifragile* (2012). **The critical distinction is between perishable things (where aging increases mortality) and non-perishable things (where survival signals robustness and decreases expected mortality).** The mathematical foundation rests on Pareto (power-law) distributions, connecting the concept to [fat-tailed](../resources/term_dictionary/term_fat_tails.md) phenomena and Extremistan. Applications include technology selection, reading prioritization, and forecasting the durability of institutions.
**Documentation**: [Lindy Effect](../resources/term_dictionary/term_lindy_effect.md)
**Related**: [Antifragility](#antifragility), [Barbell Strategy](#barbell-strategy), [Black Swan](../resources/term_dictionary/term_black_swan.md), [Pareto Principle](../resources/term_dictionary/term_pareto_principle.md)

### Planning Fallacy
**Full Name**: Planning Fallacy
**Description**: Systematic tendency to underestimate the time, cost, and risk of future actions while overestimating their benefits. Driven by "inside-view" thinking — focusing on the specifics of the current plan while ignoring base rates from similar past projects ("outside view"). Kahneman & Tversky (1979) first identified it; Buehler, Griffin, & Ross (1994) provided extensive experimental evidence. The antidote is reference class forecasting: using distributional data from comparable past projects rather than building estimates from the current plan's details.
**Documentation**: [Planning Fallacy Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_planning_fallacy.md)
**Source**: Kahneman, D. & Tversky, A. (1979); Buehler, R., Griffin, D., & Ross, M. (1994). "Exploring the 'Planning Fallacy'." *JPSP*, 67(3), 366–381
**Related**: [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias), [WYSIATI](#wysiati), [System 1 and System 2](#system-1-and-system-2)

### Choice Architecture
**Full Name**: Choice Architecture (Nudge Theory)
**Description**: The practice of designing the environment in which people make decisions to predictably influence their choices without restricting freedom. Introduced by **Richard Thaler** and **Cass Sunstein** in *Nudge* (2008). Core insight: there is no neutral way to present choices — order, defaults, framing, and context systematically influence decisions. The NUDGES framework (iNcentives, Understand mappings, Defaults, Give feedback, Expect error, Structure complex choices) provides actionable design principles. The **default effect** is the most powerful tool: opt-out organ donation achieves ~99% participation vs. ~12% for opt-in. Embodies "libertarian paternalism" — guiding choices toward beneficial outcomes while preserving freedom.
**Documentation**: [Choice Architecture Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_choice_architecture.md)
**Source**: Thaler, R.H. & Sunstein, C.R. (2008). *Nudge: Improving Decisions About Health, Wealth, and Happiness*
**Related**: [Framing Effect](#framing-effect), [Anchoring](#anchoring), [Loss Aversion](#loss-aversion), [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias), [System 1 and System 2](#system-1-and-system-2), [Commitment Device](#commitment-device)

### Commitment Device
**Full Name**: Commitment Device (Ulysses Contract / Precommitment)
**Description**: A deliberate arrangement that restricts future choices to prevent short-term impulses from overriding long-term intentions. Addresses **time-inconsistent preferences** — people overvalue immediate gratification (hyperbolic discounting) even when their "planning self" would choose differently. Named after Odysseus ordering his crew to tie him to the mast before passing the Sirens. Types include: restriction (delete the app), cost escalation (bet money on a goal), social accountability (habit contract), default manipulation (auto-enrollment), irreversibility (prepay annually), and temporal separation (24-hour waiting period). In system design, examples include pre-commit hooks, type systems, and role-based access control.
**Documentation**: [Commitment Device Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_commitment_device.md)
**Source**: Bryan, G., Karlan, D., & Nelson, S. (2010). "Commitment Devices." *Annual Review of Economics*, 2(1), 671–698; Clear, J. (2018). *Atomic Habits*, Ch. 14 & 17
**Related**: [Loss Aversion](#loss-aversion), [Planning Fallacy](#planning-fallacy), [Choice Architecture](#choice-architecture), [Habit Loop](#habit-loop), [Prospect Theory](#prospect-theory)

### Peak-End Rule
**Full Name**: Peak-End Rule
**Description**: Principle that experiences are evaluated and remembered primarily based on two moments: the **peak** (most intense point) and the **ending**, largely ignoring duration. Discovered through Kahneman's cold-water experiments (1993) and colonoscopy studies with Redelmeier (1996). Reveals the gap between the "experiencing self" (moment-by-moment hedonic flow) and the "remembering self" (retrospective evaluation). Implications for customer experience design: endings and peak moments matter far more than total duration for satisfaction ratings.
**Documentation**: [Peak-End Rule Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_peak_end_rule.md)
**Source**: Kahneman, D., Fredrickson, B.L., Schreiber, C.A., & Redelmeier, D.A. (1993). "When More Pain Is Preferred to Less." *Psychological Science*, 4(6), 401–405
**Related**: [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias), [System 1 and System 2](#system-1-and-system-2), [Prospect Theory](#prospect-theory)

### Skin in the Game
**Full Name**: Skin in the Game (SITG)
**Description**: The principle that decision-makers must bear the consequences of their decisions, formalized by Nassim Nicholas Taleb in *Skin in the Game: Hidden Asymmetries in Daily Life* (2018), the fifth volume of the Incerto. The core requirement is **symmetry -- whoever captures the upside of a decision must also be exposed to its downside**. Taleb traces the concept from Hammurabi's Code (~1754 BC) through the Silver Rule to modern agency theory, arguing it functions simultaneously as a risk management heuristic, an ethical framework, and an epistemological filter. Key sub-concepts include the Bob Rubin trade (asymmetric payoff extraction), the minority rule, and the Intellectual Yet Idiot (IYI). Skin in the game enables system-level antifragility by ensuring agents who make poor decisions are eliminated, allowing the system to learn from failure.
**Documentation**: [Skin in the Game](../resources/term_dictionary/term_skin_in_the_game.md)
**Related**: [Antifragility](#antifragility), [Barbell Strategy](#barbell-strategy), [Lindy Effect](#lindy-effect), [Black Swan](../resources/term_dictionary/term_black_swan.md), [Loss Aversion](#loss-aversion)

### Tunneling
**Full Name**: Tunneling
**Description**: A concept introduced by Nassim Nicholas Taleb in *The Black Swan* (2007) describing the tendency to focus on a small number of well-defined sources of uncertainty while ignoring the much larger, unknown sources. **The more sophisticated one's risk modeling tools become, the stronger the tunneling effect -- expertise deepens the tunnel rather than widening the field of vision.** Tunneling is the risk-management instantiation of Kahneman's WYSIATI principle: modeled risks are treated as the complete risk landscape, with entire categories of unmodeled risk rendered invisible. The concept connects to Rumsfeld's known/unknown taxonomy -- tunneling is systematic overinvestment in known unknowns at the expense of unknown unknowns.
**Documentation**: [Tunneling](../resources/term_dictionary/term_tunneling.md)
**Related**: [Barbell Strategy](#barbell-strategy), [Antifragility](#antifragility), [WYSIATI](#wysiati), [Black Swan](../resources/term_dictionary/term_black_swan.md), [Ludic Fallacy](../resources/term_dictionary/term_ludic_fallacy.md)

### Via Negativa
**Full Name**: Via Negativa (The Negative Way)
**Description**: A principle asserting that we gain more reliable knowledge by identifying what something is *not* than by affirming what it *is*, and that improvement comes through removal of harmful elements rather than addition of beneficial ones. Rooted in apophatic theology (Pseudo-Dionysius, Maimonides), where God is described by what God is not. Nassim Nicholas Taleb extended it in *Antifragile* (2012) as a decision heuristic: **negative knowledge is more robust than positive knowledge because what we know to be wrong cannot turn out to be right.** The principle yields practical rules like "first, do no harm" (medicine), "don't lose money" (Buffett), and "eliminate the downside" (risk management), and serves as a charlatanism detector -- genuine experts give negative advice (what to avoid), while charlatans give positive advice (what to do).
**Documentation**: [Via Negativa](../resources/term_dictionary/term_via_negativa.md)
**Related**: [Antifragility](#antifragility), [Barbell Strategy](#barbell-strategy), [Lindy Effect](#lindy-effect), [Black Swan](../resources/term_dictionary/term_black_swan.md), [Loss Aversion](#loss-aversion), [Circle of Influence](#circle-of-influence)

## Learning Science

### Retrieval Practice
**Full Name**: Retrieval Practice (Testing Effect)
**Description**: Evidence-based learning strategy where actively recalling information from memory strengthens long-term retention more effectively than re-reading, re-studying, or passive review. The act of retrieval itself modifies and strengthens memory traces — it is a learning event, not merely an assessment event. Roediger & Karpicke (2006) demonstrated that students who practiced retrieval retained 80% after one week vs. 36% for those who re-studied. Foundation strategy in *Make It Stick* (Brown, Roediger, & McDaniel, 2014).
**Documentation**: [Retrieval Practice Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_retrieval_practice.md)
**Source**: Brown, P.C., Roediger, H.L., & McDaniel, M.A. (2014). *Make It Stick: The Science of Successful Learning*
**Related**: [Spaced Repetition](#spaced-repetition), [Desirable Difficulties](#desirable-difficulties), [Interleaving](#interleaving), [Elaborative Interrogation](#elaborative-interrogation)

### Spaced Repetition
**Full Name**: Spaced Repetition (Distributed Practice)
**Description**: Learning technique where review sessions are distributed over increasing time intervals rather than concentrated in a single session (massed practice / cramming). The forgetting that occurs between sessions forces deeper retrieval effort, strengthening long-term retention. Rooted in Ebbinghaus's (1885) forgetting curve research. Modern implementations include Anki, SuperMemo, and Leitner systems. Spacing is one of the most robust findings in cognitive psychology, with effect sizes of d = 0.4–0.6 across hundreds of studies.
**Documentation**: [Spaced Repetition Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_spaced_repetition.md)
**Source**: Brown, P.C., Roediger, H.L., & McDaniel, M.A. (2014). *Make It Stick*; Ebbinghaus, H. (1885). *Memory: A Contribution to Experimental Psychology*
**Related**: [Retrieval Practice](#retrieval-practice), [Desirable Difficulties](#desirable-difficulties), [Interleaving](#interleaving)

### Interleaving
**Full Name**: Interleaving (Interleaved Practice)
**Description**: Learning strategy of alternating between different topics, problem types, or skills within a single study session, as opposed to blocked practice (completing all problems of one type before moving to the next). Builds discrimination ability — learners must identify which strategy applies to each problem, developing flexible mental models. Rohrer & Taylor (2007) showed interleaving improved test scores by 43% despite feeling less fluent during practice. The discomfort is a feature, not a bug — it signals deeper processing.
**Documentation**: [Interleaving Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_interleaving.md)
**Source**: Brown, P.C., Roediger, H.L., & McDaniel, M.A. (2014). *Make It Stick*; Rohrer, D. & Taylor, K. (2007). "The shuffling of mathematics problems improves learning."
**Related**: [Retrieval Practice](#retrieval-practice), [Spaced Repetition](#spaced-repetition), [Desirable Difficulties](#desirable-difficulties)

### Elaborative Interrogation
**Full Name**: Elaborative Interrogation
**Description**: Learning technique of asking "Why is this true?" or "Why does this make sense?" to deepen encoding through self-generated explanations that connect new information to existing knowledge. Dunlosky et al. (2013) rated it as having moderate-to-high utility across learning contexts. The technique works by forcing the learner to activate prior knowledge and build causal connections, creating multiple retrieval pathways. In knowledge management, every link between notes is an act of elaborative interrogation — explaining *why* two concepts connect.
**Documentation**: [Elaborative Interrogation Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_elaborative_interrogation.md)
**Source**: Brown, P.C., Roediger, H.L., & McDaniel, M.A. (2014). *Make It Stick*; Dunlosky, J. et al. (2013). "Improving Students' Learning With Effective Learning Techniques." *Psychological Science in the Public Interest*, 14(1), 4–58
**Related**: [Socratic Questioning](acronym_glossary_critical_thinking.md#socratic-questioning), [Retrieval Practice](#retrieval-practice), [Desirable Difficulties](#desirable-difficulties)

### Dual Coding Theory
**Full Name**: Dual Coding Theory (DCT)
**Description**: Allan Paivio's (1971) cognitive theory proposing that the mind processes information through two independent but interconnected systems: a **verbal system** (operating on "logogens") and a **nonverbal imagery system** (operating on "imagens"). Items encoded in both systems create redundant memory traces, producing stronger recall than either code alone. DCT explains the picture superiority effect, concreteness effects in memory, and is the cognitive foundation for Mayer's multimedia learning principles and mind mapping's effectiveness.
**Documentation**: [Dual Coding Theory Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_dual_coding_theory.md)
**Source**: Paivio, A. (1971). *Imagery and Verbal Processes*; Paivio, A. (1986). *Mental Representations: A Dual Coding Approach*
**Related**: [Desirable Difficulties](#desirable-difficulties), [Elaborative Interrogation](#elaborative-interrogation), [Retrieval Practice](#retrieval-practice), [Deliberate Practice](#deliberate-practice)

### Desirable Difficulties
**Full Name**: Desirable Difficulties Framework
**Description**: Framework introduced by Robert Bjork (1994) proposing that learning strategies which introduce effortful processing — such as retrieval practice, spacing, interleaving, and generation — feel harder during acquisition but produce significantly stronger long-term retention and transfer than easier alternatives. The "desirability" criterion is that the difficulty must trigger deeper cognitive processing, not merely add irrelevant obstacles. Explains the "illusion of competence" — fluent re-reading feels effective but produces shallow encoding, while effortful retrieval feels difficult but produces durable learning.
**Documentation**: [Desirable Difficulties Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_desirable_difficulties.md)
**Source**: Bjork, R.A. (1994). "Memory and metamemory considerations in the training of human beings." In *Metacognition: Knowing about Knowing*; Brown, P.C. et al. (2014). *Make It Stick*
**Related**: [Retrieval Practice](#retrieval-practice), [Spaced Repetition](#spaced-repetition), [Interleaving](#interleaving), [Elaborative Interrogation](#elaborative-interrogation), [Dual Coding Theory](#dual-coding-theory), [System 1 and System 2](#system-1-and-system-2)

### Deliberate Practice
**Full Name**: Deliberate Practice
**Description**: Structured, purposeful form of practice designed to improve specific aspects of performance through focused effort, immediate feedback, and progressive challenge. Coined by psychologist **Anders Ericsson** (1993), it distinguishes expert-level skill acquisition from mere repetition. Four essential criteria: (1) specific sub-skill targeted, (2) full concentration, (3) immediate diagnostic feedback, (4) difficulty just beyond current ability (the stretch zone). The mechanism is the development of increasingly sophisticated **mental representations** — internal models experts use to process information and recognize patterns. Popularly (and inaccurately) summarized as "10,000 hours to mastery" — Ericsson's actual finding: 10,000 hours of *deliberate* practice, not naive repetition.
**Documentation**: [Deliberate Practice Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_deliberate_practice.md)
**Source**: Ericsson, K.A., Krampe, R.T., & Tesch-Römer, C. (1993). "The Role of Deliberate Practice in the Acquisition of Expert Performance." *Psychological Review*; Ericsson, A. & Pool, R. (2016). *Peak*
**Related**: [Desirable Difficulties](#desirable-difficulties), [Flow State](#flow-state), [Retrieval Practice](#retrieval-practice), [Habit Loop](#habit-loop)

### Grit
**Full Name**: Grit (Perseverance and Passion for Long-Term Goals)
**Description**: Psychological construct defined as **perseverance and passion for long-term goals**, combining consistency of interest (sustained direction) and perseverance of effort (resilience through setbacks). Introduced by Angela Duckworth (2007), the theory posits that effort "counts twice" in achievement: Talent x Effort = Skill, and Skill x Effort = Achievement. Grit is operationalized through four psychological assets — Interest, Practice, Purpose, and Hope — and measured via the Grit Scale. Critics note high overlap with Big Five Conscientiousness (ρ =.84 per Credé meta-analysis) and modest incremental validity.
**Documentation**: [Grit Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_grit.md)
**Source**: Duckworth, A.L. et al. (2007). *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*; Duckworth, A.L. (2016). *Grit: The Power of Passion and Perseverance*
**Related**: [Growth Mindset](#growth-mindset), [Deliberate Practice](#deliberate-practice), [Desirable Difficulties](#desirable-difficulties), [Compound Effect](#compound-effect)

### Growth Mindset
**Full Name**: Growth Mindset (Incremental Theory of Intelligence)
**Description**: The belief that human abilities — intelligence, talent, and personality — are not fixed but can be developed through effort, effective strategies, and support. Developed by Stanford psychologist **Carol S. Dweck** from her implicit theories of intelligence framework (Dweck & Leggett, 1988), it contrasts with a fixed mindset (entity theory) which views abilities as innate and unchangeable. Mindsets function as meaning systems that organize goals, effort beliefs, attributions, and responses to setbacks — students with a growth mindset adopt learning goals, persist through difficulty, and treat failure as informational feedback. Growth mindset interventions (e.g., teaching neuroplasticity) have shown effects for at-risk students, though meta-analyses debate effect sizes and replication.
**Documentation**: [Growth Mindset Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_growth_mindset.md)
**Source**: Dweck, C.S. (2006). *Mindset: The New Psychology of Success*; Dweck, C.S. & Leggett, E.L. (1988). *Psychological Review*
**Related**: [Deliberate Practice](#deliberate-practice), [Desirable Difficulties](#desirable-difficulties), [Flow State](#flow-state), [Compound Effect](#compound-effect)

### Active Reading
**Full Name**: Active Reading (Demanding Reading)
**Description**: Foundational principle of **Mortimer J. Adler**'s reading methodology: reading is an active skill that can be systematically developed, not passive information reception. Operationalized through four universal questions every reader must ask: (1) What is this about as a whole? (2) What is being said in detail? (3) Is it true? (4) What of it? The depth of answers increases across four cumulative reading levels (Elementary → Inspectional → Analytical → Syntopical). Most people plateau at elementary reading; active reading means deliberately climbing to higher levels. The distinction between reading for *information* (text at your level) vs. reading for *understanding* (text above your level) is central — only the latter produces intellectual growth.
**Documentation**: [Active Reading Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_active_reading.md)
**Source**: Adler, M.J. & Van Doren, C. (1972). *How to Read a Book: The Classic Guide to Intelligent Reading*. Chapters 1, 5
**Related**: [Analytical Reading](#analytical-reading), [Inspectional Reading](#inspectional-reading), [Syntopical Reading](#syntopical-reading), [Elaborative Interrogation](#elaborative-interrogation), [Retrieval Practice](#retrieval-practice), [Desirable Difficulties](#desirable-difficulties)

### Inspectional Reading
**Full Name**: Inspectional Reading (Pre-Reading / Survey Reading)
**Description**: The second level of Adler's four-level reading framework, designed to extract maximum understanding from a text in limited time. Consists of two complementary techniques: (1) **Systematic skimming** — read title, TOC, index, publisher blurb, pivotal chapters, and spot-check paragraphs (5–30 minutes) to determine if deeper reading is worthwhile and map the structure; (2) **Superficial reading** — read straight through without stopping at difficult passages (1–2 hours) to get a first-pass understanding. Adler's most counterintuitive rule: "Do not try to understand every word or page of a difficult book the first time through." Inspectional reading is not laziness — it is a **prerequisite** for analytical reading, like consulting a map before navigating a city.
**Documentation**: [Inspectional Reading Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_inspectional_reading.md)
**Source**: Adler, M.J. & Van Doren, C. (1972). *How to Read a Book*. Chapter 4
**Related**: [Active Reading](#active-reading), [Analytical Reading](#analytical-reading), [Syntopical Reading](#syntopical-reading), [Progressive Summarization](#progressive-summarization)

### Analytical Reading
**Full Name**: Analytical Reading (Close / Deep Reading)
**Description**: The third and central level of Adler's four-level reading framework — "thorough reading, complete reading, or good reading — the best reading you can do." Operationalized through **15 explicit rules** in three sequential stages: **Stage 1 — Structural** (classify, state unity, enumerate parts, define problems); **Stage 2 — Interpretive** (come to terms, grasp propositions, know arguments, determine what's solved/unsolved); **Stage 3 — Critical** (understand before criticizing, disagree respectfully with reasons, apply four specific critiques: uninformed, misinformed, illogical, incomplete). The cardinal rule: **understanding must precede judgment** — you must say "I understand" before "I agree/disagree." Unless you can show the author fails one of the four critiques (Rules 12-15), you are obligated to agree or suspend judgment. The 15 rules constitute a systematic algorithm for extracting understanding from any demanding text.
**Documentation**: [Analytical Reading Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_analytical_reading.md)
**Source**: Adler, M.J. & Van Doren, C. (1972). *How to Read a Book*. Chapters 6-12
**Related**: [Active Reading](#active-reading), [Inspectional Reading](#inspectional-reading), [Syntopical Reading](#syntopical-reading), [Socratic Questioning](acronym_glossary_critical_thinking.md#socratic-questioning), [Elaborative Interrogation](#elaborative-interrogation), [MECE](#mece), [Literature Notes](#literature-notes), [Desirable Difficulties](#desirable-difficulties)

### Syntopical Reading
**Full Name**: Syntopical Reading (Comparative Reading)
**Description**: The fourth and highest level of Adler's reading framework — his most original contribution. Involves reading multiple works on the same subject, constructing **neutral terminology** that transcends individual authors, framing questions all authors address (explicitly or not), mapping where they agree and disagree, and analyzing the discussion with **dialectical objectivity** (examining all sides without bias). Five core steps: (1) find relevant passages, (2) bring authors to terms (construct shared vocabulary), (3) get questions clear, (4) define issues (arrange opposing answers), (5) analyze the discussion. The **syntopical paradox**: you need to know which books to read to read syntopically, but can't know without reading syntopically — solved by iterating with inspectional reading to refine the bibliography. The Zettelkasten method implements syntopical reading as a permanent practice: atomic notes with neutral terminology linked across sources.
**Documentation**: [Syntopical Reading Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_syntopical_reading.md)
**Source**: Adler, M.J. & Van Doren, C. (1972). *How to Read a Book*. Chapter 20
**Related**: [Active Reading](#active-reading), [Analytical Reading](#analytical-reading), [Inspectional Reading](#inspectional-reading), [Zettelkasten](#zettelkasten), [Permanent Notes](#permanent-notes), [Hub Notes](#hub-notes), [Index Notes](#index-notes), [Progressive Summarization](#progressive-summarization)

### Radiant Thinking
**Full Name**: Radiant Thinking
**Description**: Concept coined by Tony Buzan describing the brain's natural tendency to process information in radiating, associative patterns rather than linear sequences. Every piece of information acts as a center from which numerous associative pathways branch outward, each association becoming a new center of further associations. **Mind maps are the external expression of radiant thinking** — visual tools that mirror this internal associative architecture by placing a central idea at the hub with branching connections radiating outward. The concept was formally articulated in *The Mind Map Book* (Buzan & Buzan, 1993) and underpins all of Buzan's learning and creativity methodology.
**Documentation**: [Radiant Thinking](../resources/term_dictionary/term_radiant_thinking.md)
**Related**: [Divergence and Convergence](../resources/term_dictionary/term_divergence_and_convergence.md), [Hub Notes](../resources/term_dictionary/term_hub_notes.md), [Elaborative Interrogation](#elaborative-interrogation), [Systems Thinking](../resources/term_dictionary/term_systems_thinking.md)

### Design Thinking
**Full Name**: Design Thinking
**Description**: Human-centered problem-solving methodology structured as five phases: **Empathize** (understand user needs), **Define** (frame the problem), **Ideate** (generate solutions), **Prototype** (build quick models), **Test** (validate with users). Popularized by IDEO and Stanford d.school. Emphasizes iterative learning through rapid prototyping rather than linear analysis. Berger's *A More Beautiful Question* argues that design thinking's power lies in its questioning structure — each phase is driven by a different type of question (Why? → What if? → How?).
**Documentation**: [Design Thinking Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_design_thinking.md)

### Question Storming
**Full Name**: Question Storming (QStorming / Question Formulation Technique)
**Description**: Brainstorming variant where participants generate only questions (not answers) about a challenge or topic. Rules: (1) ask as many questions as possible, (2) do not stop to discuss/answer, (3) write every question as stated, (4) change statements into questions. Opens new lines of inquiry by suspending the drive to solve and instead focusing on how a problem is framed. Developed by the Right Question Institute (Rothstein & Santana) as the Question Formulation Technique (QFT). Featured in Berger's *A More Beautiful Question* and Fadem's *The Art of Asking*.

## Problem-Solving Frameworks

### Heuristic
**Full Name**: Heuristic (from Greek *heuriskein*, "to find")
**Description**: Any approach to problem solving or decision making that employs a practical method not guaranteed to be optimal but sufficiently effective for reaching an approximate goal. The term spans three traditions: **Polya's mathematical tradition** (heuristic as a teachable art of discovery — analogy, decomposition, working backwards), Simon/Kahneman's cognitive tradition (heuristic as a mental shortcut producing systematic biases), and AI's computational tradition (heuristic as an approximate algorithm trading optimality for speed). The unifying thread is the contrast with algorithms: where an algorithm guarantees correctness, a heuristic provides a practical shortcut with no such guarantee.
**Documentation**: [Heuristic Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_heuristic.md)
**Source**: Polya, G. (1945). *How to Solve It*; Tversky, A. & Kahneman, D. (1974). "Judgment Under Uncertainty"
**Related**: [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias), [Availability Heuristic](#availability-heuristic), [Anchoring](#anchoring), [MECE](#mece), [Satisficing](#satisficing)

### MECE
**Full Name**: Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive
**Description**: Decomposition principle requiring that categories have **no overlap** (mutually exclusive) and **cover the entire problem space** (collectively exhaustive). Originated at McKinsey & Company as a foundational analytical tool for structured problem solving. Ensures analysis has no gaps and no double-counting. Applied in Hartley's *Strategic Problem Solving* as the key discipline for the Decompose step. In knowledge management, MECE thinking validates vault architecture: entry points should be collectively exhaustive indices, term notes should be mutually exclusive concept definitions.
**Documentation**: [MECE Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_mece.md)
**Source**: Minto, B. (1987). *The Minto Pyramid Principle*; Hartley, M. (2024). *Strategic Problem Solving*
**Related**: [Systems Thinking](#systems-thinking), [Design Thinking](#design-thinking), [Planning Fallacy](#planning-fallacy)

### Systems Thinking
**Full Name**: Systems Thinking
**Description**: Analytical approach that examines how parts influence one another within a whole, focusing on interconnections, feedback loops, delays, and emergent behavior rather than isolated components. Originated with Ludwig von Bertalanffy's General System Theory (1968) and developed by Jay Forrester (system dynamics), Donella Meadows (*Thinking in Systems*, 2008), and Peter Senge (*The Fifth Discipline*, 1990). Prevents local optimization that degrades overall system performance. In Hartley's *Strategic Problem Solving*, systems thinking ensures that solving one problem does not create new problems elsewhere in the system.
**Documentation**: [Systems Thinking Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_systems_thinking.md)
**Source**: Meadows, D. (2008). *Thinking in Systems: A Primer*; Hartley, M. (2024). *Strategic Problem Solving*
**Related**: [MECE](#mece), [Design Thinking](#design-thinking), [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias), [System Archetypes](#system-archetypes)

### System Archetypes
**Full Name**: System Archetypes (also: System Traps, Systemic Structures)
**Description**: Recurring structural patterns of interacting reinforcing and balancing feedback loops that generate predictable, problematic behavior over time across diverse systems. Coined by Peter Senge in *The Fifth Discipline* (1990) and independently framed as "system traps" by Donella Meadows in *Thinking in Systems* (2008). **The core set includes ten archetypes -- Policy Resistance, Tragedy of the Commons, Drift to Low Performance, Escalation, Success to the Successful, Shifting the Burden, Rule Beating, Seeking the Wrong Goal, Limits to Growth, and Growth and Underinvestment -- each with a characteristic causal loop diagram and behavior-over-time signature.** Archetypes serve as diagnostic templates: recognizing which pattern is operating reveals the underlying causal structure and high-leverage intervention points.
**Documentation**: [System Archetypes Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_system_archetypes.md)
**Source**: Senge, P. (1990). *The Fifth Discipline*; Meadows, D. (2008). *Thinking in Systems*
**Related**: [Systems Thinking](#systems-thinking), [MECE](#mece), [Paradigm Shift](#paradigm-shift)

**See also**: [Five Whys](acronym_glossary_critical_thinking.md#five-whys) — moved to [Critical Thinking Glossary](acronym_glossary_critical_thinking.md)

### Stocks and Flows
**Full Name**: Stocks and Flows (also: Levels and Rates)
**Description**: The fundamental building blocks of system dynamics models. A **stock** is an accumulation -- a quantity measurable at a point in time (inventory, population, backlog). A **flow** is the rate of change that adds to or subtracts from a stock over time (production rate, birth rate, resolution rate). Stocks create system inertia and memory because they can only change gradually through their flows; they also act as buffers that decouple inflow timing from outflow timing. Introduced by **Jay Forrester** as "levels and rates" in *Industrial Dynamics* (1961) and popularized by Donella Meadows in *Thinking in Systems* (2008).
**Documentation**: [Stocks and Flows Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_stocks_and_flows.md)
**Source**: Forrester, J.W. (1961). *Industrial Dynamics*; Meadows, D.H. (2008). *Thinking in Systems: A Primer*
**Related**: [Systems Thinking](#systems-thinking), [Compound Effect](#compound-effect), [P/PC Balance](#ppc-balance)

### Emergence
**Full Name**: Emergence
**Description**: The formation of collective properties, behaviors, or structures that arise from interactions among a system's components but are not present in those components in isolation. Emergence is classified into **weak emergence** (reducible in principle through simulation, e.g., flocking, traffic jams) and **strong emergence** (ontologically irreducible, e.g., consciousness — debated). Originated with the British Emergentists (Mill, Morgan, Broad) in the 19th-20th centuries and revived by complexity science. **Emergence is the central concept opposing reductionism**, asserting that higher-level phenomena require their own explanatory frameworks.
**Documentation**: [Emergence Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_emergence.md)
**Related**: [Systems Thinking](#systems-thinking), [Adjacent Possible](#adjacent-possible), [Paradigm Shift](#paradigm-shift)

### Leverage Points
**Full Name**: Leverage Points (Places to Intervene in a System)
**Description**: A ranked hierarchy of twelve types of intervention points in complex systems, proposed by Donella Meadows in her 1999 essay. Leverage points are places where a small, well-targeted change produces disproportionately large effects on system behavior. **The framework's key insight is that most effort targets low-leverage parameters (tax rates, quotas), while transformative change requires intervening at high-leverage points that alter system goals, rules, or paradigms.** Abson et al. (2017) grouped the 12 points into four categories: Parameters, Feedbacks, Design, and Intent. The higher the leverage point, the more the system resists change at that point.
**Documentation**: [Leverage Points Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_leverage_points.md)
**Source**: Meadows, D.H. (1999). "Leverage Points: Places to Intervene in a System." Sustainability Institute
**Related**: [Systems Thinking](#systems-thinking), [Paradigm Shift](#paradigm-shift), [Pareto Principle](#pareto-principle-8020-rule), [Circle of Influence](#circle-of-influence)

### Pareto Principle (80/20 Rule)
**Full Name**: Pareto Principle (80/20 Rule)
**Description**: Empirical observation that roughly 80% of effects come from 20% of causes, recurring across economics, business, computing, and daily life. Named by quality engineer Joseph Juran after economist Vilfredo Pareto, who observed that ~80% of Italy's land was owned by ~20% of the population. **The key insight is the imbalance between inputs and outputs, not the exact ratio** — actual distributions may be 70/30, 90/10, or 99/1. The principle prescribes identifying the "vital few" high-impact causes and reallocating resources from the "trivial many." It applies recursively: the top $4\%$ ($20\% \times 20\%$) produces $\sim 64\%$ of results.
**Documentation**: [Pareto Principle Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_pareto_principle.md)
**Source**: Koch, R. (1997). *The 80/20 Principle*; Juran, J.M. (1951). *Quality Control Handbook*
**Related**: [Heuristic](#heuristic), [MECE](#mece), [Systems Thinking](#systems-thinking), [Compound Effect](#compound-effect)

**See also**: [Power Law](acronym_glossary_statistics.md#power-law), [Zipf's Law](acronym_glossary_statistics.md#zipfs-law) — moved to [Statistics & Causal Inference Glossary](acronym_glossary_statistics.md)

### Paradigm Shift
**Full Name**: Paradigm Shift
**Description**: A fundamental change in the basic assumptions, concepts, and practices of a discipline or worldview. Coined by philosopher of science **Thomas S. Kuhn** in *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* (1962) to describe how scientific fields undergo revolutionary transformations when accumulated anomalies force abandonment of the reigning paradigm. **Kuhn's cycle**: Normal Science → Anomaly Accumulation → Crisis → Revolution → New Normal Science. Key property: paradigm shifts are **incommensurable** — practitioners of the old and new paradigms literally see different worlds and cannot fully translate between frameworks. Covey adopted the concept in *The 7 Habits* as the foundation for personal change: lasting effectiveness requires shifting paradigms (how we see the world), not just changing behaviors.
**Documentation**: [Paradigm Shift Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_paradigm_shift.md)
**Source**: Kuhn, T.S. (1962). *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions*; Covey, S.R. (1989). *The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People*
**Related**: [Growth Mindset](#growth-mindset), [Systems Thinking](#systems-thinking), [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias), [Confirmation Bias](#confirmation-bias)

### Circle of Influence
**Full Name**: Circle of Influence (vs. Circle of Concern)
**Description**: A three-zone model from Stephen Covey's *The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People* (1989, Habit 1: Be Proactive) that classifies where a person directs their mental and emotional energy. **Circle of Concern** contains everything you care about (weather, economy, others' behavior); **Circle of Influence** is the subset you can actually affect (your choices, skills, responses); **Circle of Control** (innermost) is what you directly determine. **Proactive people focus energy on the Circle of Influence, which causes it to expand over time. Reactive people focus on the Circle of Concern, which causes their Circle of Influence to shrink.** The model serves as a diagnostic tool: the language someone uses (reactive: "I have to," "If only"; proactive: "I choose to," "I will") reveals which circle dominates their focus.
**Documentation**: [Circle of Influence Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_circle_of_influence.md)
**Source**: Covey, S.R. (1989). *The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People*, Habit 1
**Related**: [Growth Mindset](#growth-mindset), [Systems Thinking](#systems-thinking), [Status Quo Bias](#status-quo-bias), [Barbell Strategy](#barbell-strategy), [Antifragility](#antifragility), [Via Negativa](#via-negativa)

### P/PC Balance
**Full Name**: P/PC Balance (Production / Production Capability)
**Description**: Covey's effectiveness formula from *The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People* (1989), based on Aesop's goose-and-golden-egg fable. **P** = Production (the desired results); **PC** = Production Capability (the ability to produce). Effectiveness requires balancing both across three asset types: physical (equipment maintenance), financial (capital preservation), and human (developing skills and relationships). **Overemphasis on P destroys PC** (killing the goose for more eggs); overemphasis on PC (endless preparation) never produces results. The principle operates asymmetrically — depleting PC is fast, rebuilding it is slow.
**Documentation**: [P/PC Balance Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_p_pc_balance.md)
**Source**: Covey, S.R. (1989). *The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People*
**Related**: [Systems Thinking](#systems-thinking), [Compound Effect](#compound-effect), [Loss Aversion](#loss-aversion), [Emotional Bank Account](#emotional-bank-account)

### Time Management Matrix
**Full Name**: Time Management Matrix (Eisenhower Matrix / Covey's Four Quadrants)
**Description**: A 2x2 prioritization framework classifying activities by **Urgency** (demands immediate attention) and **Importance** (contributes to mission and values). **Q1** (Urgent + Important): crises, deadline-driven projects — manage these. **Q2** (Not Urgent + Important): prevention, planning, relationship-building, personal development — **focus here**. **Q3** (Urgent + Not Important): interruptions, some meetings — avoid or delegate. **Q4** (Not Urgent + Not Important): busywork, time wasters — eliminate. Originated with Eisenhower's 1954 speech; formalized by Covey in Habit 3 and expanded in *First Things First* (1994). **The key insight: effective people minimize Q1 by investing in Q2** — more planning and prevention means fewer crises.
**Documentation**: [Time Management Matrix Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_time_management_matrix.md)
**Source**: Covey, S.R. (1989). *The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People*, Habit 3; Eisenhower, D.D. (1954)
**Related**: [Pareto Principle](#pareto-principle-8020-rule), [Circle of Influence](#circle-of-influence), [Systems Thinking](#systems-thinking), [Compound Effect](#compound-effect)

### Inside-Out Approach
**Full Name**: Inside-Out Approach (vs. Outside-In Approach)
**Description**: The central philosophy of Stephen Covey's *The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People* (1989). Lasting change starts with self — character, paradigms, motives — not with external conditions or other people. **Contrasts with the Outside-In Approach** that blames circumstances and tries to change others first. Connected to **Character Ethics** (integrity, humility, courage, patience — "primary greatness") vs. **Personality Ethics** (social image, techniques, quick fixes — "secondary greatness"). Covey argues only the inside-out approach produces sustainable effectiveness: technique mastery without character integrity is manipulation.
**Documentation**: [Inside-Out Approach Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_inside_out_approach.md)
**Source**: Covey, S.R. (1989). *The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People*, Part I
**Related**: [Paradigm Shift](#paradigm-shift), [Growth Mindset](#growth-mindset), [Circle of Influence](#circle-of-influence), [Emotional Bank Account](#emotional-bank-account)

### Natural Planning Model
**Full Name**: Natural Planning Model (NPM)
**Description**: Five-phase project planning framework from David Allen's *Getting Things Done* (2001) that captures how the mind naturally approaches any multi-step task: (1) **Define purpose and principles** — why are we doing this?; (2) **Envision the outcome** — what does success look like?; (3) **Brainstorm** — what are all possible ideas?; (4) **Organize** — what are the components, sequences, priorities?; (5) **Identify next actions** — what is the very next physical thing to do? Explicitly top-down then bottom-up: starts with purpose (highest abstraction) and ends with a concrete physical action. Contrasts with "unnatural planning" where teams jump to organizing without establishing purpose or brainstorming options. Phase 2 leverages the reticular activating system — clearly defining outcomes primes the brain to notice relevant resources.
**Documentation**: [Natural Planning Model Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_natural_planning_model.md)
**Source**: Allen, D. (2015). *Getting Things Done*, Chapter 3: "Getting Projects Creatively Under Way"
**Related**: [MECE](#mece), [Systems Thinking](#systems-thinking), [Design Thinking](#design-thinking), [Open Loops](#open-loops), [Trusted System](#trusted-system), [Divergence and Convergence](#divergence-and-convergence)

### Divergence and Convergence
**Full Name**: Divergence and Convergence (Divergent and Convergent Thinking)
**Description**: Two fundamental modes of creative and analytical work. **Divergent thinking** generates multiple ideas, explores possibilities, and expands the solution space without judgment. **Convergent thinking** evaluates options, selects the best, and narrows toward a single output. Introduced by psychologist J.P. Guilford (1956) in the Structure of Intellect model. The British Design Council's **Double Diamond** (2004) formalized the most widely used model: two successive diverge-converge cycles — first to define the right problem, then to find the right solution. In Forte's CODE method: Capture + Organize are divergent, Distill + Express are convergent. The critical insight: **mixing modes simultaneously causes friction** — trying to generate and evaluate at the same time produces neither good ideas nor good evaluations. Neuroscience (Beaty et al., 2016) suggests divergent thinking involves the default mode network while convergent thinking involves the executive control network.
**Documentation**: [Divergence and Convergence Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_divergence_and_convergence.md)
**Source**: Guilford, J.P. (1956). "The Structure of Intellect"; Forte, T. (2022). *Building a Second Brain*, Chapter 8
**Related**: [CODE Method](#code-method), [Design Thinking](#design-thinking), [Natural Planning Model](#natural-planning-model), [Question Storming](#question-storming), [BASB](#basb)

## Innovation & Creativity

### Adjacent Possible
**Full Name**: Adjacent Possible
**Description**: Concept from theoretical biologist Stuart Kauffman, popularized by Steven Johnson in *Where Good Ideas Come From* (2010). The adjacent possible describes the set of all first-order possibilities available at any moment — innovations that can be created by recombining currently existing components, knowledge, and infrastructure. **Innovation is not about leaping to distant futures but about systematically exploring what's next.** Each new innovation expands the frontier, revealing doors that were previously invisible.
**Documentation**: [Adjacent Possible Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_adjacent_possible.md)
**Source**: Johnson, S. (2010). *Where Good Ideas Come From*; Kauffman, S. (1995). *At Home in the Universe*
**Related**: [Liquid Network](#liquid-network), [Slow Hunch](#slow-hunch), [Exaptation](#exaptation), [Systems Thinking](#systems-thinking)

### Liquid Network
**Full Name**: Liquid Network
**Description**: Steven Johnson's concept describing the optimal environmental state for innovation — one that is neither too rigid (solid, like hierarchical organizations) nor too chaotic (gaseous, like unstructured brainstorming). In a liquid network, ideas are **dense enough to interact but flexible enough to reconfigure** into new combinations. Historical examples include Enlightenment-era coffeehouses, MIT Building 20, and the early internet. The Zettelkasten's graph-based architecture is itself a liquid network of ideas.
**Documentation**: [Liquid Network Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_liquid_network.md)
**Source**: Johnson, S. (2010). *Where Good Ideas Come From*
**Related**: [Adjacent Possible](#adjacent-possible), [Slow Hunch](#slow-hunch), [Serendipity](#serendipity), [Design Thinking](#design-thinking)

### Slow Hunch
**Full Name**: Slow Hunch
**Description**: Johnson's concept that most breakthrough ideas develop gradually as vague, incomplete hunches that linger over months or even decades before encountering the missing piece that completes them — challenging the myth of the sudden eureka moment. **The challenge is keeping hunches alive long enough to find their partners**, which requires systems for recording and revisiting partial ideas. Darwin's theory of natural selection gestated across years of notebook entries. The commonplace book and Zettelkasten are technologies for cultivating slow hunches.
**Documentation**: [Slow Hunch Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_slow_hunch.md)
**Source**: Johnson, S. (2010). *Where Good Ideas Come From*
**Related**: [Commonplace Book](#commonplace-book), [Liquid Network](#liquid-network), [Serendipity](#serendipity), [Retrieval Practice](#retrieval-practice)

### Serendipity
**Full Name**: Serendipity (in Innovation Theory)
**Description**: In Johnson's framework, serendipity is not pure luck but the systematic product of specific environmental and cognitive conditions: diverse information environments (liquid networks), prepared minds (broad knowledge), and associative mental states (walking, daydreaming). **Serendipity can be designed for** by increasing the surface area for unexpected connections. Activities that promote associative thinking allow the brain's default mode network to make connections that focused analytical thinking suppresses. Coined by Horace Walpole in 1754.
**Documentation**: [Serendipity Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_serendipity.md)
**Source**: Johnson, S. (2010). *Where Good Ideas Come From*; Walpole, H. (1754)
**Related**: [Liquid Network](#liquid-network), [Slow Hunch](#slow-hunch), [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias), [System 1 and System 2](#system-1-and-system-2)

### Exaptation
**Full Name**: Exaptation
**Description**: Concept from evolutionary biology (Gould & Vrba, 1982) where a trait evolved for one function is co-opted for an entirely different function. Feathers evolved for thermoregulation and were later exapted for flight. Johnson extended this to innovation: many breakthroughs arise when a technology from one domain is repurposed for an unexpected application in another. **Diverse networks are ~3x more innovative** because they increase the chance of seeing unexpected uses for existing tools. The World Wide Web, designed for physicists, was exapted into the foundation of modern digital life.
**Documentation**: [Exaptation Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_exaptation.md)
**Source**: Gould, S.J. & Vrba, E. (1982). "Exaptation: A Missing Term in the Science of Form"; Johnson, S. (2010). *Where Good Ideas Come From*
**Related**: [Adjacent Possible](#adjacent-possible), [Liquid Network](#liquid-network), [Interleaving](#interleaving), [Design Thinking](#design-thinking)

### Brainwriting
**Full Name**: Brainwriting (6-3-5 Method)
**Description**: A structured, silent ideation method where participants write ideas independently before building on each other's thinking — eliminating the "loudest voice wins" dynamic of traditional brainstorming. Invented by **Bernd Rohrbach (1968) as the 6-3-5 method**: 6 participants, 3 ideas per round, 5-minute rotations, generating up to 108 ideas in 30 minutes. The written format ensures equal participation, prevents production blocking, and creates a durable record. Brainwriting is a **divergent** technique that pairs with convergent methods (e.g., Shark Tank, dot-voting) for decision-making.
**Documentation**: [Brainwriting Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_brainwriting.md)
**Source**: Rohrbach, B. (1969). "Kreativ nach Regeln." *Absatzwirtschaft*, 12, 73-75
**Related**: [Shark Tank Ideation](#shark-tank-ideation), [Groupthink](acronym_glossary_critical_thinking.md#groupthink), [Divergence and Convergence](#divergence-and-convergence), [Design Thinking](#design-thinking), [Question Storming](#question-storming)

### Shark Tank Ideation
**Full Name**: Shark Tank Ideation (Competitive Pitch Evaluation)
**Description**: A competitive pitch-based innovation method adapted from the TV show format, where teams pitch ideas to a panel of decision-makers ("sharks") who probe, challenge, and make explicit invest/pass decisions. Each pitch follows a structured formula: Problem (30s) → Idea (90s) → Impact (60s) → Resources Needed (60s) → Ask (30s). **The format serves as a structured convergent selection mechanism** that narrows the funnel after divergent ideation phases like brainwriting. The competitive element raises preparation quality, the panel format distributes decision authority, and the time constraint forces clarity.
**Documentation**: [Shark Tank Ideation Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_shark_tank_ideation.md)
**Related**: [Brainwriting](#brainwriting), [Divergence and Convergence](#divergence-and-convergence), [Design Thinking](#design-thinking), [6-Pager](../resources/term_dictionary/term_6pager.md)

### Commonplace Book
**Full Name**: Commonplace Book
**Description**: Historical knowledge management practice of maintaining a personal notebook for recording quotes, observations, ideas, and reading excerpts organized by theme rather than chronologically. Systematized by Erasmus (1512) and Locke (1706). Users periodically re-read and recombine entries, enabling unexpected connections. Johnson positions it as **the original technology for cultivating slow hunches and enabling serendipity**. Practitioners include Darwin, Milton, Bacon, Locke, and Newton. The commonplace book is the direct historical precursor to the Zettelkasten method.
**Documentation**: [Commonplace Book Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_commonplace_book.md)
**Source**: Johnson, S. (2010). *Where Good Ideas Come From*; Locke, J. (1706). *A New Method of a Common-Place-Book*

## Knowledge Management & Note-Making

### Beetle in a Box
**Full Name**: Beetle in a Box (Wittgenstein's Thought Experiment)
**Description**: A thought experiment from Wittgenstein's *Philosophical Investigations* (1953, section 293) in which everyone has a box they call "beetle" but no one can look into anyone else's box. **The key insight is that the private object "cancels out" — it plays no role in determining meaning, which is instead grounded in public use and shared practice.** Wittgenstein's original target was sensation language (e.g., "pain"), but the diagnostic applies broadly: any community using a term without shared public criteria for its application is engaged in a beetle-in-a-box language game. Sascha Fast (zettelkasten.de, 2025) applies this diagnosis to the Zettelkasten community's use of "atomic note," arguing that without concrete definitions of what constitutes a knowledge atom, the term functions as a floating signifier.
**Documentation**: [Beetle in a Box Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_beetle_in_a_box.md)
**Related**: [Shuhari](#shuhari), [Permanent Notes](#permanent-notes), [Socratic Questioning](../resources/term_dictionary/term_socratic_questioning.md)

### DKS - Dialectic Knowledge System
**Full Name**: Dialectic Knowledge System
**Description**: A knowledge architecture where empirical observations are abstracted into behavioral patterns, classified by competing arguments (automated model vs. human expert), and refined through structured disagreement that updates operational rules via a closed feedback loop. **The key structural requirement is that the system produces falsifiable claims (arguments) that can be challenged by independent evidence, not just descriptions that cannot be counter-argued.** A DKS has 7 components: observation source, two independent argument generators, disagreement detection, counter-argument capture, pattern discovery, and rule improvement. The term was coined in the Abuse SlipBox argument trail to name the design pattern independently discovered in Athelas-Conv (conversation investigation) and proposed in NexusTrace (behavioral pattern memory).
**Documentation**: [Dialectic Knowledge System](../resources/term_dictionary/term_dialectic_knowledge_system.md)
**Related**: [Knowledge Building Blocks](../resources/term_dictionary/term_knowledge_building_blocks.md), [Argumentation](../resources/term_dictionary/term_argumentation.md), [Zettelkasten](#zettelkasten), [MO Slipbox](../resources/term_dictionary/term_mo_slipbox.md)

### Fleeting Notes
**Full Name**: Fleeting Notes (Zettelkasten Capture Stage)
**Description**: Temporary, informal captures of ideas, thoughts, or reactions — "mere reminders of what is in your head" (Ahrens). The first stage of the Zettelkasten note pipeline, designed to be processed into [literature notes](#literature-notes) or [permanent notes](#permanent-notes) within 1-2 days, then discarded. Fleeting notes have no required structure or permanent home — they exist only to prevent ideas from being lost before elaboration. The danger is confusing capture with understanding: "just collecting unprocessed fleeting notes inevitably leads to chaos" (Ahrens). Leverages the Zeigarnik effect — capturing an idea externally closes the cognitive open loop.
**Documentation**: [Fleeting Notes Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_fleeting_notes.md)
**Source**: Ahrens, S. (2022). *How to Take Smart Notes*, Ch. 2; Doto, B. (2024). *A System for Writing*, Part I
**Related**: [Literature Notes](#literature-notes), [Permanent Notes](#permanent-notes), [Open Loops](#open-loops), [Trusted System](#trusted-system), [BASB](#basb)

### Literature Notes
**Full Name**: Literature Notes (Reference Notes)
**Description**: Concise summaries of source material written **in your own words**, with full bibliographic reference. The second stage of the Zettelkasten note pipeline — the bridge between raw capture ([fleeting notes](#fleeting-notes)) and refined understanding ([permanent notes](#permanent-notes)). The critical requirement is rephrasing: literature notes must never be verbatim copies. The act of translating source material into your own language leverages the **generation effect** — self-generated content is remembered far better than passively received content. Doto uses the term **reference notes** — functionally identical but emphasizing their role as citeable source references.
**Documentation**: [Literature Notes Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_literature_notes.md)
**Source**: Ahrens, S. (2022). *How to Take Smart Notes*, Ch. 2, 10; Doto, B. (2024). *A System for Writing*, Part I
**Related**: [Fleeting Notes](#fleeting-notes), [Permanent Notes](#permanent-notes), [Progressive Summarization](#progressive-summarization), [Elaborative Interrogation](#elaborative-interrogation), [Desirable Difficulties](#desirable-difficulties)

### Permanent Notes
**Full Name**: Permanent Notes (Main Notes)
**Description**: Self-contained, context-independent ideas stored in the Zettelkasten (slip-box). Each note contains **one idea** written "as if for publication" — understandable without knowing the original context, linked to other permanent notes, and filed by retrieval context rather than topic. The core building blocks of the Zettelkasten: they accumulate over time, form clusters through linking, and serve as raw material for writing projects. Doto uses the broader term **main notes** — "the fundamental building blocks of your future writing." The one-idea-per-note constraint is what makes recombination possible: "the restriction to one idea per note is also the precondition to recombine them freely later" (Ahrens). Andy Matuschak's "evergreen notes" is a variant of this concept.
**Documentation**: [Permanent Notes Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_permanent_notes.md)
**Source**: Ahrens, S. (2022). *How to Take Smart Notes*, Ch. 11; Doto, B. (2024). *A System for Writing*, Part I
**Related**: [Fleeting Notes](#fleeting-notes), [Literature Notes](#literature-notes), [Folgezettel](#folgezettel), [Intermediate Packets](#intermediate-packets), [Compound Effect](#compound-effect), [Adjacent Possible](#adjacent-possible)

### Hub Notes
**Full Name**: Hub Notes (Retrieval / Navigation Notes)
**Description**: Navigational notes in the Zettelkasten whose primary function is **retrieval** — helping the note maker find where specific trains of thought live in the slip-box. Each hub note contains a list of annotated links pointing to the first note in a Folgezettel sequence. Doto defines them as "lists of links annotated by title" that serve as jumping-off points. Hub notes answer "Where?" while structure notes answer "How?" — the key distinction is finding vs. developing. Johannes Schmidt identified hub Zettels in Luhmann's archive as cards with above-average link density functioning as "highways between topics." In the vault, entry points serve the hub note function.
**Documentation**: [Hub Notes Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_hub_notes.md)
**Source**: Doto, B. (2024). *A System for Writing*, Ch. 6; Schmidt, J.F.K. (2018). "Niklas Luhmann's Card Index"
**Related**: [Permanent Notes](#permanent-notes), [Folgezettel](#folgezettel), [Index Notes](#index-notes), [Trusted System](#trusted-system)

### Index Notes
**Full Name**: Index Notes / Keyword Register (Schlagwortregister)
**Description**: Keyword-based entry points into the Zettelkasten, mapping topic keywords to a small number of note IDs where relevant trains of thought begin. Luhmann's Schlagwortregister contained only ~3,200 keywords for ~67,000 notes, with each keyword pointing to just 1-4 notes — deliberately sparse. The register gets you *into the neighborhood*; internal linking does the rest. Ahrens emphasizes choosing keywords "with an eye towards the topics you are working on" — think like a writer ("When will I want this?"), not an archivist ("Where does this belong?"). The register is NOT a tagging system; it is a separate lookup artifact.
**Documentation**: [Index Notes Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_index_notes.md)
**Source**: Ahrens, S. (2022). *How to Take Smart Notes*; Schmidt, J.F.K. (2018). "Niklas Luhmann's Card Index"
**Related**: [Hub Notes](#hub-notes), [Permanent Notes](#permanent-notes), [Folgezettel](#folgezettel), [Trusted System](#trusted-system), [Commonplace Book](#commonplace-book)

### Folgezettel
**Full Name**: Folgezettel (Follow-up Note / Continuation Note)
**Description**: Luhmann's alphanumeric sequencing system for placing permanent notes in the Zettelkasten. Each note receives a unique ID (e.g., `21/3d7a4`) that encodes its position in a branching sequence of related ideas — alternating numbers (sequence) and letters (branching). The ID has no topical significance; it serves only as a permanent address and a record of the connection made at filing time. Doto identifies folgezettel as a source of **eufriction** — beneficial friction (analogous to eustress) that "slows the note maker down just enough to force them to think about what they're doing." Key misconception corrected: initial numbers do NOT create discrete topical sections — topics rupture and diverge across branches. Luhmann accumulated ~90,000 notes over ~40 years using this system.
**Documentation**: [Folgezettel Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_folgezettel.md)
**Source**: Doto, B. (2024). *A System for Writing*, Part II; Ahrens, S. (2022). *How to Take Smart Notes*
**Related**: [Permanent Notes](#permanent-notes), [Desirable Difficulties](#desirable-difficulties), [Compound Effect](#compound-effect), [Trusted System](#trusted-system)

### SECI Model - Knowledge Conversion Spiral
**Full Name**: SECI Model (Nonaka-Takeuchi Model)
**Description**: A model of organizational knowledge creation (Nonaka, 1990; Nonaka & Takeuchi, 1995) defining four modes of knowledge conversion: Socialization (tacit→tacit), Externalization (tacit→explicit), Combination (explicit→explicit), and Internalization (explicit→tacit). **AI/NLP is strongest for Combination mode and can partially assist Externalization, but Socialization remains fundamentally human.** The concept of "Ba" (shared context) was added in 1998. Criticized for linearity and Japanese-organization bias (Gourlay, 2006).
**Documentation**: [SECI Model Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_seci_model.md)
**Related**: [Tacit Knowledge](#tacit-knowledge), [Permanent Notes](#permanent-notes), [Zettelkasten](../resources/term_dictionary/term_zettelkasten.md)

### Tacit Knowledge
**Full Name**: Tacit Knowledge (Polanyi, 1966)
**Description**: Knowledge that is difficult to articulate, formalize, or transfer — "we can know more than we can tell" (Polanyi). Encompasses skills, intuitions, mental models, and experiential understanding. **Contrasts with explicit knowledge (codifiable in documents); the two exist on a spectrum, not as a binary.** In organizations, tacit knowledge includes expert judgment, cultural norms, and relational navigation skills. Most vulnerable to loss through employee turnover. The SECI model provides a framework for converting tacit knowledge into organizational explicit knowledge.
**Documentation**: [Tacit Knowledge Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_tacit_knowledge.md)
**Related**: [SECI Model](#seci-model---knowledge-conversion-spiral), [Beetle in a Box](#beetle-in-a-box), [Shuhari](#shuhari), [Fleeting Notes](#fleeting-notes)

### Shuhari
**Full Name**: Shuhari (守破離 — Obey, Break, Leave)
**Description**: A Japanese three-stage mastery model originating from Noh theater (Zeami Motokiyo, 14th century) and later adopted in martial arts, agile software development, and knowledge management. **The core insight is that rigid rules help beginners (SHU) but harm intermediate practitioners who need principle-level understanding (HA), while masters (RI) generate appropriate techniques from internalized principles.** Sascha applies Shuhari to Zettelkasten learning: SHU practitioners follow "one idea per note" rigidly, HA practitioners understand atomicity as a principle and adapt implementation to context, and RI practitioners create novel note structures freely. Teaching aims to move learners to at least HA — where they distinguish principles from techniques.
**Documentation**: [Shuhari Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_shuhari.md)
**Related**: [Deliberate Practice](#deliberate-practice), [Permanent Notes](#permanent-notes), [Folgezettel](#folgezettel), [Desirable Difficulties](#desirable-difficulties)

### CLOG - Creative Log / Catalog Log
**Full Name**: CLOG (Creative Log / Catalog Log)
**Description**: A project-organized workflow management tool introduced by Bob Doto in *A System for Writing* (2024) for tracking active Zettelkasten projects. Unlike chronological daily logs, a CLOG groups entries by project — recording what was worked on, what was discovered, and what needs attention next. **The CLOG bridges the gap between the Zettelkasten (where ideas live) and the writer's active workflow (where projects progress).** It captures creative momentum and prevents context loss when returning to a project after days or weeks.
**Documentation**: [CLOG Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_clog.md)
**Related**: [Interstitial Journaling](#interstitial-journaling---task-transition-journaling), [Sleeping Folder](#sleeping-folder), [Trusted System](#trusted-system), [Triangle of Creativity](#triangle-of-creativity)

### Eufriction
**Full Name**: Eufriction (Beneficial Friction)
**Description**: A term coined by Bob Doto (~2022) describing productive difficulty in a Zettelkasten workflow — friction that generates deeper engagement with material rather than obstructing it. The concept applies most directly to the folgezettel numbering system, where **the requirement to place each new note in a specific sequential position forces the practitioner to think carefully about where an idea belongs relative to existing ideas.** The prefix "eu-" (Greek: good, well) parallels "eustress" (beneficial stress). Eufriction is the Zettelkasten equivalent of Bjork's desirable difficulties — obstacles that enhance long-term understanding.
**Documentation**: [Eufriction Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_eufriction.md)
**Related**: [Folgezettel](#folgezettel), [Desirable Difficulties](#desirable-difficulties), [Deliberate Practice](#deliberate-practice), [Hidden Relevance](#hidden-relevance)

### Fetzenwissen
**Full Name**: Fetzenwissen (Fragmentary Knowledge)
**Description**: A term coined by Austrian satirist Karl Kraus in 1907 (in *Die Fackel*) meaning "scrap knowledge" — knowledge composed of disconnected fragments that lack coherent integration. Bob Doto identifies Fetzenwissen as one of eight common Zettelkasten mistakes in *A System for Writing* (2024): **creating notes that capture isolated snippets or highlights without developing them into complete, contextualised thoughts.** A note suffering from Fetzenwissen is technically "atomic" in size but lacks the conceptual depth that atomicity is meant to serve. It contrasts with properly developed permanent notes where each captures one *developed* knowledge building block.
**Documentation**: [Fetzenwissen Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_fetzenwissen.md)
**Related**: [Permanent Notes](#permanent-notes), [Verknüpfungszwang](#verknüpfungszwang---compulsive-linking), [Fleeting Notes](#fleeting-notes), [Triangle of Creativity](#triangle-of-creativity)

### Hidden Relevance
**Full Name**: Hidden Relevance (Emergent Connections)
**Description**: A concept described by Bob Doto in *A System for Writing* (2024) referring to unexpected connections that surface through sustained Zettelkasten practice. **When a practitioner consistently creates well-developed atomic notes and links them thoughtfully, the accumulating network reveals relationships between ideas that were not apparent at the time of capture.** Hidden relevance is not forced or designed — it emerges naturally from the structure of a well-maintained knowledge network over time. It parallels Steven Johnson's "slow hunch" concept and represents the Zettelkasten's unique value proposition over other note-taking methods.
**Documentation**: [Hidden Relevance Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_hidden_relevance.md)
**Related**: [Slow Hunch](#slow-hunch), [Serendipity](#serendipity), [Folgezettel](#folgezettel), [Hub Notes](#hub-notes)

### Interstitial Journaling - Task-Transition Journaling
**Full Name**: Interstitial Journaling
**Description**: A micro-journaling practice coined by Tony Stubblebine (CEO of Medium, ~2017) involving brief journal entries written in the gaps between tasks — at the moment of transition from one activity to the next. **Rather than maintaining a separate journaling habit, the practice embeds reflection into existing workflow transitions at zero additional time cost.** Bob Doto incorporates interstitial journaling into his Zettelkasten workflow in *A System for Writing* (2024) as a tool for capturing fleeting thoughts, maintaining creative momentum, and feeding the CLOG. Entries can become fleeting notes, CLOG entries, or day log content.
**Documentation**: [Interstitial Journaling Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_interstitial_journaling.md)

### Sleeping Folder
**Full Name**: Sleeping Folder (Deferred Processing Space)
**Description**: A workflow management concept introduced by Bob Doto in *A System for Writing* (2024) for handling inbox overflow. When the inbox accumulates more items than can be processed, items that are not immediately actionable are moved to the sleeping folder rather than being discarded or left cluttering the inbox. **The sleeping folder serves as a low-pressure holding space — items "sleep" until they become relevant again through the practitioner's evolving interests or project needs.** Distinct from archives (completed items) and the inbox (active processing), the sleeping folder reduces decision fatigue and supports hidden relevance discovery through periodic review.
**Documentation**: [Sleeping Folder Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_sleeping_folder.md)

### Triangle of Creativity
**Full Name**: Triangle of Creativity (Reading-Thinking-Writing Integration)
**Description**: A framework introduced by Bob Doto in *A System for Writing* (2024) describing three interdependent activities that drive knowledge development: **reading** (input), **thinking** (processing), and **writing** (output). **The triangle emphasises that these activities must be continuously integrated — not performed in isolated phases.** A practitioner who reads without writing, writes without thinking, or thinks without reading breaks the creative cycle. The Zettelkasten serves as the medium where all three activities converge. This echoes Ahrens' insight that writing is not the outcome of thinking but the medium of thinking.
**Documentation**: [Triangle of Creativity Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_triangle_of_creativity.md)

### Verknüpfungszwang - Compulsive Linking
**Full Name**: Verknüpfungszwang (Compulsion to Connect)
**Description**: A term originating from art historian Aby Warburg's (1866-1929) approach to iconographic analysis, describing an obsessive drive to find connections between images, symbols, and cultural artifacts. Bob Doto repurposes the concept in *A System for Writing* (2024) to describe a common Zettelkasten anti-pattern: **compulsively linking notes to every possible related note, prioritising link quantity over link quality.** The result is a dense but noisy network where meaningful connections are buried under superficial ones. Often co-occurs with Fetzenwissen — shallow notes linked excessively compound both problems.

## Behavior Change, Habits & Productivity

### Habit Loop
**Full Name**: Habit Loop (Cue → Craving → Response → Reward)
**Description**: The neurological feedback cycle underlying all habitual behavior. Originally described as a three-step loop (cue → routine → reward) by Charles Duhigg in *The Power of Habit* (2012), refined by James Clear into a four-stage model (cue → craving → response → reward) in *Atomic Habits* (2018). The addition of "craving" explains *why* we act — without motivational drive, cues alone don't trigger behavior. The loop operates in the **basal ganglia**, shifting behavior from conscious (System 2) to automatic (System 1) through repetition. Clear maps one "law" to each stage: make it obvious (cue), attractive (craving), easy (response), satisfying (reward) — with inversions for breaking bad habits.
**Documentation**: [Habit Loop Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_habit_loop.md)
**Source**: Duhigg, C. (2012). *The Power of Habit*; Clear, J. (2018). *Atomic Habits*
**Related**: [System 1 and System 2](#system-1-and-system-2), [Choice Architecture](#choice-architecture), [Commitment Device](#commitment-device), [Compound Effect](#compound-effect), [Flow State](#flow-state)

### Compound Effect
**Full Name**: Compound Effect (Aggregation of Marginal Gains)
**Description**: The principle that small, consistent changes accumulate over time to produce disproportionately large results — analogous to compound interest in finance. A **1% daily improvement compounds to 37.78x over one year** (1.01^365), while 1% daily decline approaches zero (0.99^365). Popularized by Darren Hardy (2010) and James Clear (2018). The **Plateau of Latent Potential** describes the frustrating gap where effort exceeds visible results — the "Valley of Disappointment" where most people quit. Clear's ice cube analogy: heating from 25°F to 31°F shows no change, but 32°F triggers a phase transition. Applied by Sir Dave Brailsford in British Cycling: improving hundreds of components by 1% each produced Olympic dominance within 5 years.
**Documentation**: [Compound Effect Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_compound_effect.md)
**Source**: Hardy, D. (2010). *The Compound Effect*; Clear, J. (2018). *Atomic Habits*
**Related**: [Planning Fallacy](#planning-fallacy), [Habit Loop](#habit-loop), [Desirable Difficulties](#desirable-difficulties), [Deliberate Practice](#deliberate-practice), [Adjacent Possible](#adjacent-possible)

### Flow State
**Full Name**: Flow State (Optimal Experience)
**Description**: Mental state of complete absorption in an activity, characterized by energized focus, full involvement, and enjoyment of the process. Coined by psychologist **Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi** (1990) in *Flow: The Psychology of Optimal Experience*. The key condition: **task difficulty must be approximately 4% beyond current skill level** (the Goldilocks Rule). Too easy → boredom; too hard → anxiety. The narrow band between them produces maximum learning and motivation. Eight components: challenge-skill balance, merging of action and awareness, clear goals, immediate feedback, deep concentration, sense of control, loss of self-consciousness, time distortion. Flow differs from habit automaticity — habits seek efficiency (System 1), flow requires full engagement at the edge of ability.
**Documentation**: [Flow State Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_flow_state.md)
**Source**: Csikszentmihalyi, M. (1990). *Flow: The Psychology of Optimal Experience*; Clear, J. (2018). *Atomic Habits*, Ch. 19

### Trusted System
**Full Name**: Trusted System (External Brain / Cognitive Offloading System)
**Description**: Any external repository — physical, digital, or hybrid — that reliably captures, organizes, and surfaces commitments, information, and ideas at the appropriate time, enabling the mind to release its grip on those items. Coined by David Allen in *Getting Things Done* (2001). The mind will only stop tracking an open loop if it trusts that something else is tracking it reliably. Three non-negotiable properties: (1) **complete capture** — everything goes in, no exceptions; (2) **regular review** — contents stay current; (3) **reliable retrieval** — items surface when needed. Trust is binary: 95% capture still forces the mind to track everything as a safety net. Grounded in **distributed cognition** (Hutchins, 1995) and the **extended mind thesis** (Clark & Chalmers, 1998) — external artifacts that function as reliable, accessible cognitive extensions are part of the cognitive system. The Zettelkasten is a trusted system for intellectual work; GTD's organized lists are a trusted system for task management.
**Documentation**: [Trusted System Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_trusted_system.md)
**Source**: Allen, D. (2015). *Getting Things Done*; Hutchins, E. (1995). *Cognition in the Wild*; Clark, A. & Chalmers, D. (1998). "The Extended Mind"
**Related**: [Open Loops](#open-loops), [Habit Loop](#habit-loop), [Flow State](#flow-state), [Compound Effect](#compound-effect), [Choice Architecture](#choice-architecture), [BASB](#basb)

### BASB
**Full Name**: BASB (Building a Second Brain)
**Description**: Personal Knowledge Management (PKM) methodology introduced by Tiago Forte in *Building a Second Brain* (2022). Externalizes cognition by capturing ideas digitally, organizing them for actionability, distilling them to their essence, and expressing them as creative output. The primary framework is the **CODE method** (Capture, Organize, Distill, Express), supported by **PARA** (organizational system), **Progressive Summarization** (distillation technique), and **Intermediate Packets** (modular output units). Forte extends Allen's insight that "your mind is for having ideas, not holding them" from task management to *knowledge management*. The vault's C.O.D.E. skill taxonomy is directly derived from this framework.
**Documentation**: [BASB Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_basb.md)
**Source**: Forte, T. (2022). *Building a Second Brain*. Atria Books.
**Related**: [CODE Method](#code-method), [Progressive Summarization](#progressive-summarization), [Intermediate Packets](#intermediate-packets), [Trusted System](#trusted-system), [Divergence and Convergence](#divergence-and-convergence)

### CODE Method
**Full Name**: CODE Method (Capture, Organize, Distill, Express)
**Description**: Four-phase knowledge lifecycle framework from Tiago Forte's *Building a Second Brain* (2022). Transforms raw information into creative output through successive phases: **Capture** (gather what resonates, filtered by Twelve Favorite Problems), **Organize** (sort by actionability using PARA — Projects, Areas, Resources, Archives), **Distill** (compress to essence via Progressive Summarization), **Express** (produce deliverables from Intermediate Packets). Each phase maps to a dedicated technique. The vault's C.O.D.E. skill classification system (`code-stage` metadata) is directly derived from this framework. Complementary to GTD's five-step workflow: GTD processes tasks, CODE processes knowledge.
**Documentation**: [CODE Method Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_code_method.md)
**Source**: Forte, T. (2022). *Building a Second Brain*, Chapter 3
**Related**: [Progressive Summarization](#progressive-summarization), [Intermediate Packets](#intermediate-packets), [Trusted System](#trusted-system), [Open Loops](#open-loops), [Divergence and Convergence](#divergence-and-convergence), [BASB](#basb)

### Progressive Summarization
**Full Name**: Progressive Summarization
**Description**: Multi-layer distillation technique for making captured notes discoverable and useful to your future self. Four layers of compression: **Soil** (capture the original ~500 words), **Oil** (bold main points — 10–20% of Layer 1), **Gold** (highlight critical passages — 10–20% of Layer 2), **Gems** (write a 1–2 sentence executive summary in your own words). Each layer reduces information by 80–90%, creating a power-law distribution where the most valuable insights float to the surface. Deliberately incremental: layers are added "just-in-time" when revisiting a note for a specific purpose, not at capture time. Forte's counterintuitive framing: "a method for *forgetting* as much as possible."
**Documentation**: [Progressive Summarization Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_progressive_summarization.md)
**Source**: Forte, T. (2022). *Building a Second Brain*, Chapter 6
**Related**: [CODE Method](#code-method), [Intermediate Packets](#intermediate-packets), [Desirable Difficulties](#desirable-difficulties), [Elaborative Interrogation](#elaborative-interrogation), [BASB](#basb)

### Abundance Mentality
**Full Name**: Abundance Mentality (vs. Scarcity Mentality)
**Description**: A paradigm introduced by Stephen Covey in *The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People* (1989, Habit 4: Think Win/Win) describing the deep belief that there is enough success, recognition, and resources for everyone. **Contrasts with Scarcity Mentality, which views life as zero-sum** — another's gain is your loss. Abundance Mentality flows from internal security grounded in principles and character (the Private Victory of Habits 1–3), not external validation. It is one of three character traits required for Win/Win agreements (alongside integrity and maturity). Leaders with Abundance Mentality genuinely celebrate others' success, share credit and power freely, and seek solutions that expand the total value rather than dividing a fixed pie.
**Documentation**: [Abundance Mentality Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_abundance_mentality.md)
**Source**: Covey, S.R. (1989). *The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People*, Habit 4
**Related**: [Growth Mindset](#growth-mindset), [Loss Aversion](#loss-aversion), [Circle of Influence](#circle-of-influence), [Paradigm Shift](#paradigm-shift)

### Emotional Bank Account
**Full Name**: Emotional Bank Account
**Description**: A trust-building metaphor from Stephen Covey's *The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People* (1989) that quantifies relationship quality as a running balance of deposits and withdrawals. **Six major deposits**: understanding the individual, attending to little things, keeping commitments, clarifying expectations, showing personal integrity, and apologizing sincerely when wrong. Withdrawals are asymmetric — **a single betrayal can overdraw an account that took months of deposits to build** (paralleling Gottman's research that stable relationships require a 5:1 positive-to-negative ratio). High-trust relationships allow communication mistakes because the intent is understood; low-trust relationships make every word politicized.
**Documentation**: [Emotional Bank Account Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_emotional_bank_account.md)
**Source**: Covey, S.R. (1989). *The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People*; Gottman, J. (1994). *Why Marriages Succeed or Fail*
**Related**: [Reciprocity Bias](#reciprocity-bias), [Compound Effect](#compound-effect), [Active Listening](#active-listening), [Abundance Mentality](#abundance-mentality)

### Win/Win Thinking
**Full Name**: Win/Win Thinking (Six Paradigms of Human Interaction)
**Description**: An interpersonal leadership paradigm from Covey's Habit 4 (*The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People*, 1989) that seeks mutual benefit in all interactions. Covey identifies six paradigms: Win/Win (mutual benefit), Win/Lose (competition), Lose/Win (capitulation), Lose/Lose (spite), Win (self-interest), and **Win/Win or No Deal** (mutual benefit or walk away). Win/Win requires three character traits: **integrity** (keeping commitments), **maturity** (balancing courage with consideration), and **abundance mentality** (believing there is enough for everyone). It is the foundation for Habit 6 (Synergize) — without Win/Win agreements, genuine creative cooperation is impossible.
**Documentation**: [Win/Win Thinking Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_win_win_thinking.md)
**Source**: Covey, S.R. (1989). *The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People*, Habit 4
**Related**: [Abundance Mentality](#abundance-mentality), [Emotional Bank Account](#emotional-bank-account), [Game Theory](acronym_glossary_ml.md#game-theory), [Loss Aversion](#loss-aversion)

### Empathic Listening
**Full Name**: Empathic Listening (Listening Within the Other's Frame of Reference)
**Description**: A deep listening mode from Covey's Habit 5 ("Seek First to Understand, Then to Be Understood") that goes beyond active listening by seeking to understand the speaker's frame of reference, not just their words. **Most people listen autobiographically** — they evaluate, probe, advise, or interpret through their own paradigm. Empathic listening suspends this to enter the other's world. Four developmental levels: (1) mimic content, (2) rephrase content, (3) reflect feeling, (4) rephrase content + reflect feeling ("You feel ___ because ___"). Maps to Aristotle's persuasion sequence: **Ethos** (credibility) → **Pathos** (empathic alignment) → **Logos** (logical presentation). Most people jump to Logos without establishing Ethos or Pathos.
**Documentation**: [Empathic Listening Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_empathic_listening.md)
**Source**: Covey, S.R. (1989). *The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People*, Habit 5
**Related**: [Active Listening](#active-listening), [Emotional Bank Account](#emotional-bank-account), [Abundance Mentality](#abundance-mentality), [Circle of Influence](#circle-of-influence)

### Relationship Management (EQ)
**Full Name**: Relationship Management (Emotional Intelligence)
**Description**: The ability to use awareness of your own and others' emotions to manage interactions successfully — the capstone skill in the emotional intelligence framework that builds upon self-awareness, self-management, and social awareness. Defined by Travis Bradberry and Jean Greaves in *Emotional Intelligence 2.0* (2009) and rooted in Daniel Goleman's original EQ model (1995), relationship management encompasses trust-building through behavioral consistency, direct conflict engagement, feedback delivery, and collaborative influence. Unlike charisma or social dominance, EQ-based relationship management is empathy-driven: it requires genuinely reading others' emotional states before attempting to influence the interaction, and demands congruence between verbal messages and body language for credible communication.
**Documentation**: [Relationship Management](../resources/term_dictionary/term_relationship_management.md)
**Source**: Bradberry, T. & Greaves, J. (2009). *Emotional Intelligence 2.0*; Goleman, D. (1995). *Emotional Intelligence*
**Related**: [Empathic Listening](#empathic-listening), [Emotional Bank Account](#emotional-bank-account), [Win/Win Thinking](#winwin-thinking), [Abundance Mentality](#abundance-mentality)

### Maturity Continuum
**Full Name**: Maturity Continuum (Dependence → Independence → Interdependence)
**Description**: The foundational progression model from Stephen Covey's *The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People* (1989). Three stages: **Dependence** ("you" paradigm — others take care of me), **Independence** ("I" paradigm — self-reliant, self-directed), and **Interdependence** ("we" paradigm — combining strengths for results greater than any individual). Habits 1–3 achieve the **Private Victory** (dependence → independence); Habits 4–6 achieve the **Public Victory** (independence → interdependence). **Most people wrongly assume independence is the goal** — Covey argues interdependence is the higher, more mature state. Only independent people can choose interdependence; dependent people who attempt it produce co-dependence.
**Documentation**: [Maturity Continuum Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_maturity_continuum.md)
**Source**: Covey, S.R. (1989). *The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People*, Part I
**Related**: [Growth Mindset](#growth-mindset), [Abundance Mentality](#abundance-mentality), [Circle of Influence](#circle-of-influence), [Compound Effect](#compound-effect), [Grit](#grit), [Five Levels of Leadership](#five-levels-of-leadership)

### Five Levels of Leadership
**Full Name**: Five Levels of Leadership (Position → Permission → Production → People Development → Pinnacle)
**Description**: John C. Maxwell's leadership progression model describing five levels through which leaders develop influence. **Level 1 (Position)**: people follow because of title — produces compliance, not commitment. **Level 2 (Permission)**: people follow because of relationship and trust. **Level 3 (Production)**: people follow because of results and track record. **Level 4 (People Development)**: people follow because of what the leader has done for them — mentorship and reproduction. **Level 5 (Pinnacle)**: people follow because of who the leader is — earned reputation and legacy. Each level builds on the previous; a leader can operate at different levels with different people. Parallels Covey's Maturity Continuum and Collins' Level 5 Leadership.
**Documentation**: [Five Levels of Leadership](../resources/term_dictionary/term_five_levels_of_leadership.md)
**Source**: Maxwell, J.C. (2011). *The 5 Levels of Leadership*; Maxwell, J.C. (1998). *The 21 Irrefutable Laws of Leadership*
**Related**: [Maturity Continuum](#maturity-continuum), [Servant Leadership](#servant-leadership), [Leadership Lid](#leadership-lid), [Growth Mindset](#growth-mindset), [Emotional Bank Account](#emotional-bank-account)

### Servant Leadership
**Full Name**: Servant Leadership
**Description**: A leadership philosophy in which the leader's primary role is to serve others, coined by Robert K. Greenleaf in his 1970 essay "The Servant as Leader." Core test: **"Do those served grow as persons? Do they become healthier, wiser, freer, more autonomous, more likely themselves to become servants?"** Ten characteristics identified by Larry Spears: listening, empathy, healing, awareness, persuasion, conceptualization, foresight, stewardship, commitment to growth, and building community. Contrasts with command-and-control leadership by inverting the organizational pyramid. Maxwell's Law of Addition (leaders add value by serving) is built on this philosophy. Adopted by Southwest Airlines, Starbucks, and TD Industries.
**Documentation**: [Servant Leadership](../resources/term_dictionary/term_servant_leadership.md)
**Source**: Greenleaf, R.K. (1970). "The Servant as Leader"; Spears, L.C. (1995). *Reflections on Leadership*
**Related**: [Five Levels of Leadership](#five-levels-of-leadership), [Empathic Listening](#empathic-listening), [Win/Win Thinking](#winwin-thinking), [Emotional Bank Account](#emotional-bank-account), [Abundance Mentality](#abundance-mentality)

### Leadership Lid
**Full Name**: The Law of the Lid (Leadership Ability Determines Effectiveness)
**Description**: John C. Maxwell's first law in *The 21 Irrefutable Laws of Leadership* (1998). **Effectiveness = Leadership Ability × Dedication.** Even with maximum dedication (10/10), a leader with low leadership ability (4/10) produces limited effectiveness (40/100). Organizations plateau when they hit the leader's lid — the organization cannot outgrow the leadership ability of its leader. **Raising the lid has the highest ROI** of any personal investment because it multiplies the impact of all other efforts. The concept explains why technically brilliant individuals sometimes fail as managers, and why leadership development is more valuable than adding more effort to existing capabilities.
**Documentation**: [Leadership Lid](../resources/term_dictionary/term_leadership_lid.md)
**Source**: Maxwell, J.C. (1998). *The 21 Irrefutable Laws of Leadership*, Chapter 1
**Related**: [Five Levels of Leadership](#five-levels-of-leadership), [Servant Leadership](#servant-leadership), [Systems Thinking](#systems-thinking), [Pareto Principle](#pareto-principle-8020-rule), [Growth Mindset](#growth-mindset), [Circle of Influence](#circle-of-influence), [Peter Principle](#peter-principle)

### Peter Principle
**Full Name**: Peter Principle (Promotion to Incompetence)
**Description**: Management theory observation by Laurence J. Peter (1969) stating that in a hierarchy, every employee tends to rise to their level of incompetence. Employees are promoted based on current-role performance, but the skills needed at the next level are often fundamentally different — creating a systematic promotion-competence disconnect. **The principle predicts that over time, every position tends to be filled by someone unable to fulfill its duties**, with work accomplished by those who have not yet reached their level of incompetence. Empirically validated by Benson, Li, and Shue (2019), who found that top salespeople promoted to management performed significantly worse as managers.
**Documentation**: [Peter Principle](../resources/term_dictionary/term_peter_principle.md)
**Source**: Peter, L.J. & Hull, R. (1969). *The Peter Principle: Why Things Always Go Wrong*
**Related**: [Leadership Lid](#leadership-lid), [Five Levels of Leadership](#five-levels-of-leadership), [Growth Mindset](#growth-mindset), [Systems Thinking](#systems-thinking)

### Transformational Leadership
**Full Name**: Transformational Leadership
**Description**: A leadership theory originated by James MacGregor Burns (1978) and operationalized by Bernard Bass (1985) in which leaders inspire followers to transcend self-interest for collective purpose. **Built on four behavioral components (the "Four I's"): Idealized Influence, Inspirational Motivation, Intellectual Stimulation, and Individualized Consideration.** Bass's Full Range Leadership Model positions transformational leadership as the most effective style, complementing (not replacing) transactional leadership, with laissez-faire at the least effective end. Measured by the Multifactor Leadership Questionnaire (MLQ) and validated across thousands of empirical studies as one of the most researched leadership theories in organizational behavior.
**Documentation**: [Transformational Leadership](../resources/term_dictionary/term_transformational_leadership.md)
**Source**: Burns, J.M. (1978). *Leadership*; Bass, B.M. (1985). *Leadership and Performance Beyond Expectations*
**Related**: [Servant Leadership](#servant-leadership), [Five Levels of Leadership](#five-levels-of-leadership), [Leadership Lid](#leadership-lid), [Growth Mindset](#growth-mindset), [Emotional Bank Account](#emotional-bank-account), [Maturity Continuum](#maturity-continuum)

### Intermediate Packets
**Full Name**: Intermediate Packets (IPs)
**Description**: Discrete, reusable units of knowledge work that can be assembled into larger creative outputs — the modular building blocks of creative production. Five types: distilled notes, outtakes, work-in-process, final deliverables, and documents from others. Core insight: **most creative work is recombination, not invention**. Rather than starting every project from scratch (the "blank page" problem), a knowledge worker assembles pre-made packets into new deliverables. Parallels software engineering's shift from monolithic to modular architectures. Good IPs are atomic, self-contained, labeled, reusable, and composable. In the vault, term notes, paper section notes, digest notes, and skills all function as Intermediate Packets.
**Documentation**: [Intermediate Packets Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_intermediate_packets.md)
**Source**: Forte, T. (2022). *Building a Second Brain*, Chapter 7

## Algorithmic Decision Frameworks (Cognitive)

### Computational Kindness
**Full Name**: Computational Kindness (Reducing Cognitive Burden)
**Description**: The principle of reducing others' cognitive load by providing specific, constrained options rather than open-ended flexibility. Grounded in the asymmetry between **verification** (easy — "Does Tuesday at 2pm work?") and **generation** (hard — "When are you free?"). Proposing specific options is kinder than open flexibility because it transforms a hard generation problem into an easy verification problem. The same P ≠ NP principle that underpins cryptography.
**Documentation**: [Computational Kindness Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_computational_kindness.md)
**Source**: Christian, B. & Griffiths, T. (2016). *Algorithms to Live By*, Conclusion
**Related**: [Choice Architecture](#choice-architecture), [Satisficing](acronym_glossary_ml.md#satisficing), [Game Theory](acronym_glossary_ml.md#game-theory)

### Information Cascades
**Full Name**: Information Cascades (Herding / Social Proof)
**Description**: Phenomenon where individuals rationally follow observed actions of others rather than their own private information, producing **herding behavior**. A cascade forms when the accumulated public signal overwhelms each individual's private signal. Rational at the individual level but potentially catastrophic collectively — the group can converge on an incorrect outcome. Cascades are **fragile**: a single confident contrarian with strong contrary evidence can shatter them. Explains market bubbles, fads, and the outsized influence of early product reviews.
**Documentation**: [Information Cascades Term](../resources/term_dictionary/term_information_cascades.md)
**Source**: Christian, B. & Griffiths, T. (2016). *Algorithms to Live By*, Chapter 11; Bikhchandani et al. (1992)
**Related**: [Game Theory](acronym_glossary_ml.md#game-theory), [Cognitive Bias](#cognitive-bias), [WYSIATI](#wysiati)
