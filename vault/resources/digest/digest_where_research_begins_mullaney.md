# Digest: Where Research Begins — The Self-Centered Research Process and Key Questions at Each Stage

- **Book**: *Where Research Begins: Choosing a Research Project That Matters to You (and the World)*
- **Authors**: Thomas S. Mullaney (historian, Stanford University; Guggenheim Fellow), Christopher Rea (professor of Asian Studies, University of British Columbia)
- **Publisher**: University of Chicago Press, 2022 (Chicago Guides to Writing, Editing, and Publishing series)

## Overview

*Where Research Begins* argues that **the hardest part of research is not executing it but choosing what to research in the first place** — and that this choice must begin from the self. The book develops a **"self-centered research" methodology**: a structured, exercise-driven process for discovering a compelling research problem that matters to you personally before designing it to matter to the world.

The book's central insight is that most researchers fail at the beginning, not the middle or end. They either pick topics that bore them, jump to questions before understanding their own motivations, or chase problems assigned by others. Mullaney and Rea's antidote is a two-phase architecture: **introversion first** (Chapters 1-3: look inward to find your problem), then **extroversion second** (Chapters 4-6: connect your problem to the intellectual community). Each chapter includes "TRY THIS NOW" exercises and "SOUNDING BOARD" checkpoints that make the process repeatable.

This digest focuses on the **self-centered research process** as a methodology and the **key questions raised at each stage** through the book's exercises, making it a companion to the vault's existing questioning frameworks from *Make It Stick*, *A More Beautiful Question*, and *The Art of Asking*.

## Key Frameworks

### Framework 1: Self-Centered Research (The Core Philosophy)

**Definition**: Self-centered research is the practice of beginning from your own authentic interests, instincts, and curiosities rather than pursuing topics to satisfy external judges. It means maintaining close contact with your own self throughout the research journey while remaining self-critical and open to others' perspectives.

**What it is NOT**:
- Not self-absorbed or ego-driven
- Not autobiographical (the output is empirical, grounded scholarship about the world)
- Not a license to ignore the field

**The paradox**: By starting from the self, you produce research that matters more to others — because genuine passion and authentic curiosity generate the persistence required for original discovery.

### Framework 2: The Introversion → Extroversion Architecture

The book's six chapters follow a deliberate two-phase progression:

| Phase | Chapters | Orientation | Goal |
|-------|----------|-------------|------|
| **Introversion** | 1-3 | Inward | Discover your Problem; design your project from personal conviction |
| **Extroversion** | 4-6 | Outward | Connect your Problem to the intellectual community; begin writing |

**Why this order matters**: Most research training starts with the literature review (extroversion) — reading what others have done, finding a "gap," and filling it. Mullaney and Rea argue this produces derivative, passionless research. By starting with introversion, you arrive at the literature with a strong sense of what you're looking for, making the field navigable rather than overwhelming.

### Framework 3: The Topic → Question → Problem Hierarchy

This is the book's most consequential distinction:

| Concept | Definition | Example | Trap |
|---------|-----------|---------|------|
| **Topic** | A broad, abstract category (noun + modifiers) indicating area of curiosity | "20th century Chinese history" | The "Narrow-Down-Your-Topic" trap: making topics smaller doesn't make them researchable |
| **Question** | A specific, answerable inquiry about facts | "What facts do you wish to know about X?" | Jumping to one question too early — building on unstable ground |
| **Problem** | A nagging, enduring concern that generates interconnected questions — "something that follows you around" | "How do societies remember and forget?" | Mistaking a topic for a problem; mistaking a question for a problem |

**The progression**: You start with a topic, generate many questions from it, then discover the Problem that connects your questions. The Problem is upstream of both topics and questions — it's the engine that drives your research.

### Framework 4: Self-Evidence

**Self-evidence** is the written record of your thoughts, reactions, questions, and assumptions generated throughout the research process. It is NOT the evidence you find in archives or data — it is evidence about yourself as a researcher.

**Functions**:
- Creates an evolving record of your ideas
- Provides clues about your underlying concerns
- Aids memory and collaboration
- Makes writing a regular research habit

**The rule**: "Don't wait till the very end. Write your thoughts down now." Every "TRY THIS NOW" exercise produces self-evidence.

### Framework 5: The Sounding Board

A **Sounding Board** is a trusted mentor, colleague, or friend who helps you gain perspective on your research direction. The Sounding Board is NOT a judge or evaluator — they are a reflective surface.

**Key principles**:
- A true mentor asks "Is this what *you* want?" not "Is this what *I* want?"
- Well-intentioned suggestions from authority figures can inadvertently inhibit self-directed research
- The Sounding Board appears at checkpoints throughout the process, not just at the end

### Framework 6: The Problem Collective

A **Problem Collective** is the set of researchers across different fields and disciplines who share your underlying Problem, even if they use different terminology or study different topics. This differs from your "field" — it's organized around shared concerns rather than institutional structures.

**Example**: A researcher studying memory in Chinese history, a neuroscientist studying memory encoding, and a computer scientist studying database design might share the Problem of "how systems preserve and retrieve information."

## The Self-Centered Research Process: Stage-by-Stage Exercises and Key Questions

### Stage 0: The Manifesto (Introduction)

**Exercise: Write Here, Right Now**
- Write down, in plain language, what you think you want to research
- No need to impress — just capture your current state
- This becomes your first piece of self-evidence

**Key questions**:
- "What do I think I want to research, right now, before reading anything else?"
- "Why does this matter to me personally?"

### Stage 1: Generate Questions (Chapter 1)

**Core principle**: A topic is NOT a question. You must move from having a topic to having questions.

**Exercise: Search Yourself**
- Conduct a database/library search on your topic
- Allocate 80% of your mental energy to *self-observation*, not to the results
- Notice: Which results attract you? Which bore you? Which surprise you?
- Write down your reactions, including "fluke" results that caught your eye
- Sleep on it, then reflect: What do your selections reveal about your actual concerns?

**Exercise: Let Boredom Be Your Guide**
- Actively identify what about your topic *bores* you
- Boredom is a signal — it reveals areas you don't actually care about
- Use boredom to clarify what you genuinely want to research

**Exercise: Go Small or Go Home**
- Generate a minimum of 20 specific, fact-focused questions about your topic
- Use concrete prompts: "What facts do you wish to know?"
- Avoid abstract "meaning" questions — focus on verifiable details
- Quantity over quality at this stage

**Sounding Board checkpoint**: Start Building Your Research Network

**Key questions**:
- "What about this topic genuinely excites me — not what *should* excite me?"
- "What bores me? What am I avoiding? Why?"
- "If I could know one fact about this topic, what would it be?"
- "Which search result made me pause — and why?"

**Stage outcome**: "You Have Questions"

### Stage 2: Find Your Problem (Chapter 2)

**Core principle**: Don't jump to a single research question. Find the Problem that connects your questions.

**Exercise: Run a Diagnostic Test on Your Questions**
Language-level stress tests:
- Do your questions end with question marks? (Not "I plan to explore...")
- Remove sweeping adjectives ("modern," "traditional," "significant")
- Replace vague collective nouns with specific demographics
- Avoid verbs implying predetermined outcomes ("influence," "affect," "impact")
- Ensure questions are indifferent to their own answers (no leading questions)

**Exercise: Use Primary Sources to Educate Your Questions**
- Engage with primary sources not to *answer* your questions but to *refine* them
- Let sources reveal new keywords and angles you hadn't considered
- Sources should educate your questions, not resolve them

**Exercise: Make Your Assumptions Visible**
- Write down what you assume the answer to each question will be
- Identify where assumptions are doing the work of evidence
- Look for assumptions you hold so deeply you didn't notice them

**Exercise: Identify the Problem That Connects Your Questions**
- Lay out all your questions
- Look for patterns: What underlying concern generates these specific questions?
- The Problem is the engine behind the questions — it's what "follows you around"

**Sounding Board checkpoint**: Get Leads on Primary Sources

**Key questions**:
- "If I remove all jargon and adjectives from my questions, what remains?"
- "What am I *assuming* the answer will be — and what if I'm wrong?"
- "What single concern connects most of my questions?"
- "Is there something I keep coming back to across different topics?"

**Stage outcome**: "You Have a Problem (in a Good Way)"

### Stage 3: Design the Project (Chapter 3)

**Core principle**: Primary sources are complex artifacts with multiple layers of meaning. Sources cannot defend themselves — you must construct the argument.

**Exercise: Treat Your Primary Source Like a Cereal Box**
- Examine one source from every possible angle: Who created it? When? Why? For whom? What materials? What historical context? What's missing?
- This exercise demonstrates that even mundane objects contain multiple research angles

**Exercise: Envision Your Primary Sources**
- Imagine the full range of sources that could address your Problem
- Don't limit yourself to obvious or conventional sources

**Exercise: Connect the Dots Using Your Sources (in Pencil)**
- Map how your sources relate to each other and to your Problem
- Draw connections tentatively ("in pencil") — they will change
- Look for where sources converge on your Problem

**Exercise: Decision Matrix**
- Evaluate project feasibility: access to sources, time, skills, funding, institutional support
- Weight practical constraints against intellectual excitement

**Exercise: Get Money for Nothing (Prepare a Formal Research Proposal)**
- Write a proposal even if you don't need funding — the act of articulating your project to an external audience forces clarity

**Sounding Board checkpoints**: Is Your Decision Matrix Complete? / Share Your Proposal with a Trusted Mentor

**Key questions**:
- "What would I learn if I examined this source from 50 different angles?"
- "What sources exist that I haven't considered?"
- "Is my project feasible given my actual resources and constraints?"
- "Can I explain this project clearly to someone outside my field?"

**Stage outcome**: "You Have the Beginnings of a Project"

### Stage 4: Find Your Problem Collective (Chapter 4)

**Core principle**: Your Problem Collective is the cross-disciplinary set of researchers who share your Problem — not your field, not your department, not your advisor's network.

**Exercise: Change One Variable**
- Take your research problem and change one variable (time period, geography, population, method)
- Ask: Who else is studying this variant? They may be in your Problem Collective

**Exercise: Before and After**
- Look for researchers studying the same phenomenon in different eras or contexts
- Historical and comparative perspectives reveal your Collective

**Exercise: Map Out Your Collective (Secondary Source Search)**
- Search for researchers whose work shares your underlying concern
- Organize them not by field but by Problem

**Exercise: Find and Replace All "Insider Language"**
- Rewrite your proposal in plain language, removing all field-specific jargon
- If your Problem Collective spans disciplines, jargon is a barrier

**Sounding Board checkpoint**: Does the Lay Version of My Proposal Make Sense?

**Key questions**:
- "Who else in the world is kept up at night by the same concern — even in completely different fields?"
- "If I change one variable in my problem, who appears?"
- "Can I describe my research without any jargon?"

**Stage outcome**: "Welcome to Your Collective"

### Stage 5: Navigate Your Field (Chapter 5)

**Core principle**: Reimagine the literature review as problem-finding, not gap-filling. Read your field for *their* Problems, not just their findings.

**Exercise: Start Your Own "What's Your Problem?" Bookstore**
- Organize the literature in your field not by topic or chronology but by Problem
- Ask of each work: "What Problem is this researcher *really* trying to solve?"
- This reframes the literature review from passive summary to active diagnosis

**Exercise: Change Their Variables**
- Apply the "Change One Variable" technique to published work in your field
- Ask: What would happen if this researcher had studied a different population, era, or method?

**Exercise: Rewrite for Your Field**
- Translate your proposal into the language and conventions of your specific field
- After finding your Collective (cross-disciplinary), now position within your field

**Sounding Board checkpoint**: Find a Sounding Board in Your Field

**Key questions**:
- "What Problem is each researcher in my field *really* solving?"
- "How does my Problem relate to the Problems already recognized in my field?"
- "Where does my work sit in the landscape of Problems, not just topics?"

**Stage outcome**: "Welcome to Your Field"

### Stage 6: Begin Writing (Chapter 6)

**Core principle**: Writing IS research. Don't wait until you "know enough" to start writing.

**Exercise: Create "Draft 0"**
- Write a rough, incomplete, imperfect version of your project
- Draft 0 is not a rough draft — it's pre-draft: messy, fragmentary, exploratory
- The purpose is to see what you mean, not to say it well

**Exercise: Move from 0 to 1**
- Transform Draft 0 into Draft 1 by finding the argument hidden in the mess
- Draft 1 has structure, even if it's still rough

**Sounding Board checkpoint**: Talk to Yourself (use self-evidence to evaluate progress)

**Key questions**:
- "What would I write if I weren't afraid of being wrong?"
- "What argument is hiding in my Draft 0?"
- "What do I now know that I didn't know when I started?"

**Stage outcome**: "Welcome to Self-Centered Research"

## Other Topics Covered

- **"Commonly Made Mistakes"**: Each chapter highlights typical errors (e.g., narrowing topics instead of finding problems, jumping to literature review before self-reflection, confusing a topic for a problem)
- **Two Types of Plan B**: (1) Same problem, different case/sources; (2) Same topic, different project — ensures you have fallback options without abandoning your Problem
- **"Perfection Is Boring"**: The book explicitly rejects perfectionism as an enemy of research — imperfect, authentic work advances knowledge more than polished, derivative work
- **Concluding exercises**: "Find a New Problem and Start a New Project" (recursion) and "Help Someone Else" (pay it forward)

## Relevance to Knowledge Management

The frameworks in *Where Research Begins* map directly to SlipBox and questioning practices:

- **Topic → Question → Problem hierarchy** parallels the Zettelkasten principle that notes should be organized by problems and questions, not by topics or categories. The vault's entry points are organized by problem domains, not by topic trees
- **Self-evidence** is precisely what the `## Questions` section in vault notes captures — a written record of the researcher's own thinking about the material, not just the material itself. The `slipbox-generate-questions` skill operationalizes this by generating questions from note content
- **The Sounding Board** maps to the vault's feedback loop: `slipbox-update-feedback` captures corrections and refinements from reviewing answers, serving as an automated Sounding Board
- **Problem Collective** aligns with how the vault's graph structure reveals unexpected connections across domains — the `slipbox-analyze-term-relevance` skill finds notes that share a "Problem" (via co-citation and shared neighbors) across different vault categories
- **Introversion → Extroversion** mirrors the C.O.D.E. framework progression: Capture and Distill are introverted (personal knowledge construction), while Express is extroverted (sharing knowledge with others)
- **"Let Boredom Be Your Guide"** provides a questioning heuristic that complements the Three-Sprint Architecture: Sprint 1 (Socratic) stress-tests claims, but Mullaney/Rea add that noticing what *bores* you is equally diagnostic
- **Elaborative interrogation** from *Make It Stick* (asking "Why is this true?") maps to the book's diagnostic tests on questions — both demand that the researcher examine assumptions behind claims. The book adds the upstream step: first generate many questions, then stress-test them

## Questions

### Application (Taxonomic)
1. The book's Topic → Question → Problem hierarchy argues that clustering questions reveals the underlying Problem. Could the vault's `slipbox-generate-questions` skill be extended to cluster generated questions across a set of related term notes and surface latent "Problems" — shared concerns that cut across individually siloed concepts?

### Synthesis (Lateral)
2. Mullaney/Rea's "self-evidence" (writing your own reactions throughout the research process) resembles but differs from the [Zettelkasten](../term_dictionary/term_zettelkasten.md) concept of fleeting notes. Is self-evidence a subset of fleeting notes, or does it serve a fundamentally different epistemic function — capturing *researcher state* rather than *knowledge claims*?
3. The "Problem Collective" (cross-disciplinary researchers sharing an underlying Problem) maps structurally to co-citation clusters in graph analysis. How does this compare to the Tier 2 shared-neighbor analysis in [term_slipbox](../term_dictionary/term_slipbox.md)'s relevance landscape — and could vault graph traversal be used to discover "Problem Collectives" across domains by seeding PPR from a Problem statement rather than a single term?
