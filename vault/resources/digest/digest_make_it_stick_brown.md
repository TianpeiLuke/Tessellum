## Overview

*Make It Stick* argues that **effective learning is counterintuitive**: the study techniques most people rely on — rereading, highlighting, cramming — create an illusion of mastery but produce fragile, short-lived retention. The strategies that genuinely work — retrieval practice, spaced repetition, interleaving, elaborative interrogation, and generation — feel harder and less productive in the moment but produce dramatically stronger long-term retention and transfer.

The book's foundational principle is **desirable difficulty**: making learning more effortful in specific ways strengthens encoding, consolidation, and retrieval. This digest focuses on the **cognitive science-based review techniques** the book advocates and gives special attention to **elaborative interrogation** — the technique of asking "why" and "how" to deepen understanding through self-generated explanations.

## Core Thesis

> "Learning is deeper and more durable when it's effortful. Learning that's easy is like writing in sand, here today and gone tomorrow."

The book presents a three-stage model of how memory works — **encoding** (converting perceptions into memory traces), **consolidation** (reorganizing and stabilizing traces over hours/days, with sleep playing a crucial role), and **retrieval** (recalling from long-term memory, which itself strengthens the trace through reconsolidation). The central insight is that effortful retrieval at each stage produces durable learning, while passive re-exposure does not.

## Key Frameworks: Seven Evidence-Based Learning Strategies

### 1. Retrieval Practice (Testing Effect) — The Most Important Strategy

**What it is**: Actively recalling information from memory rather than re-exposing yourself to material.

**Why it works**: The act of retrieval itself changes the memory — it strengthens neural pathways and makes the information easier to retrieve again. Retrieval is not a neutral read-out; it is a **learning event**.

**Key evidence**:
- **Roediger & Karpicke (2006)**: Students who studied a passage once and were tested 3 times recalled dramatically more at 1 week than students who studied 3 times and were tested once — despite the re-study group having more exposure
- **Columbia, IL middle school study**: Low-stakes quizzing with feedback 3 times during a course produced 92% retention (A-) vs. 79% (C+) for material reviewed but not quizzed — a full grade-level improvement

**How to apply**: Close your books and recall key concepts from memory. Use flashcards for retrieval, not recognition. Explain concepts aloud without notes. Take frequent practice tests. Quiz yourself weekly on both current and prior material.

### 2. Spaced Practice (Distributed Practice)

**What it is**: Distributing study sessions over time with gaps between them, rather than concentrating study into a single intensive session (massed practice / cramming).

**The paradox**: Massed practice feels more productive — visible short-term gains create an illusion of learning. Spaced practice feels slower and more frustrating because some forgetting occurs between sessions. But that forgetting is the mechanism that makes it work.

**Why it works**: Forgetting between sessions forces more effortful retrieval, which strengthens the memory trace. "When learning is spaced, forgetting is the friend of learning."

**Key principle**: "Delaying subsequent retrieval practice is more potent for reinforcing retention than immediate practice, because delayed retrieval requires more effort."

**Spacing schedule**:
- Review new material within a day
- Revisit after several days
- Again after a week
- Monthly for well-mastered material

### 3. Interleaving (Mixed Practice)

**What it is**: Alternating between different topics, problem types, or subjects during practice, rather than completing all practice on one topic before moving to the next (blocked practice).

**Why it works**: Mixing forces learners to (a) discriminate between problem categories, (b) identify unifying characteristics within each type, and (c) select the appropriate solution strategy. This builds flexible, transferable mental models.

**Key evidence**:
- **Baseball batters**: Those who practiced hitting fastballs, curves, and change-ups in random interleaved order recorded more hits on a later randomized test than those who practiced each pitch type in blocks — despite feeling less confident during practice
- **Beanbag tossing**: Children who practiced at varied distances (2 and 4 feet) outperformed those who practiced exclusively at the test distance (3 feet) — even though they never practiced at 3 feet

### 4. Elaborative Interrogation — Deep Dive

**What it is**: A higher-order questioning strategy that involves prompting yourself to explain *why* a fact is true and *how* it connects to what you already know. Originally developed by Michael Pressley et al. (1987).

**Step-by-step methodology**:

| Step | Action | Example (for "The heart has four chambers") |
|------|--------|----------------------------------------------|
| 1 | Read or encounter a new fact | "The heart has four chambers." |
| 2 | Ask "Why is this true?" | "Because it must separate oxygenated and deoxygenated blood for efficient oxygen delivery while sending blood to lungs for re-oxygenation." |
| 3 | Ask "How does this relate to what I already know?" | "Connects to the circulatory system model — pulmonary and systemic circuits operate in parallel. Simpler organisms have fewer chambers." |
| 4 | Ask "What are the implications?" | "If one chamber fails, the entire system is compromised. Explains why valve defects are dangerous." |
| 5 | Ask "How does this compare to related concepts?" | "Unlike single-pump systems of simpler organisms, this dual-pump design enables high metabolic rates." |
| 6 | Synthesize in your own words | "The four-chamber design is two pumps in one organ — a solution to maintaining high oxygen delivery while keeping blood types separate." |

**Why it works cognitively**:
- Forces active search of existing knowledge schemas
- Creates new connections between prior and new knowledge
- Promotes deeper encoding through self-generated explanations
- Transforms passive reading into active meaning-construction
- Builds richer retrieval cues for later access

**Research evidence**: Pressley et al. (1987) showed students prompted to explain "why" recalled substantially more facts than controls. Follow-up studies (Pressley, Symons, McDaniel et al., 1988) demonstrated **72% retention** for elaborative interrogation users vs. ~36% for passive readers — nearly double. The technique is especially effective when learners possess relevant prior knowledge to connect to.

> "The more you can explain about the way your new learning relates to your prior knowledge, the stronger your grasp of the new learning will be, and the more connections you create that will help you remember it later."

### 5. Generation (Productive Struggle)

**What it is**: Attempting to answer a question or solve a problem *before* being taught the solution.

**Key principle**: "Trying to solve a problem before being taught the solution leads to better learning, even when errors are made in the attempt." Productive struggle creates cognitive "hooks" that make subsequent instruction more meaningful. The brain processes the correct answer more deeply when it has first attempted (and failed) to produce it.

### 6. Reflection (Retrieval + Elaboration Combined)

**What it is**: Deliberately reviewing what was learned, expressing it in your own words, connecting to prior knowledge, and assessing performance. Combines retrieval practice, elaboration, and calibration in a single activity.

**Reflection questions**:
- What went well? What could be improved?
- What connections can I make to other material?
- What would I do differently?
- How would I apply this in a different context?

### 7. Calibration (Accurate Self-Assessment)

**What it is**: Developing an accurate picture of what you know vs. what you don't know.

**The problem**: Students routinely experience "illusions of knowing" — rereading produces familiarity that is mistaken for mastery. The Dunning-Kruger effect compounds this: the least competent students overestimate their abilities the most.

**The solution**: Regular low-stakes testing reveals the gap between perceived and actual knowledge. Do not trust feelings of familiarity as indicators of mastery.

## Desirable Difficulties: The Unifying Principle

The concept of **desirable difficulty** (coined by Robert and Elizabeth Bjork, 1994) is the theoretical framework unifying all seven strategies above.

**Core paradox**: The conditions that make learning feel easiest (massed practice, rereading, blocked practice) produce the weakest long-term retention. The conditions that feel difficult, slow, and frustrating (spacing, interleaving, retrieval, generation) produce durable, transferable learning.

**What qualifies as "desirable"**: A difficulty is desirable only if the learner can overcome it through increased effort. Difficulty from unclear instructions, missing prerequisites, or unnecessary complexity is *undesirable*.

| Desirable | Undesirable |
|-----------|-------------|
| Spacing out practice sessions | Unclear or confusing instructions |
| Interleaving different topics | Missing prerequisite knowledge |
| Retrieving from memory instead of rereading | Unnecessarily complex presentation |
| Generating answers before being shown | Cognitive overload beyond capacity |
| Elaborating in your own words | Distracting or irrelevant challenges |

> "The more effort required to retrieve (or, in effect, relearn) something, the better you learn it."

## What Does NOT Work (Debunked Techniques)

| Technique | Why It Fails |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Rereading** | "Three strikes: time consuming, doesn't result in durable memory, and involves self-deception as growing familiarity feels like mastery." |
| **Highlighting** | Students cannot reliably identify the most important parts of a text; no benefit over simply reading |
| **Massed practice (cramming)** | "The rapid gains are evident, but the rapid forgetting is not." Creates temporary retention only |
| **Single-minded repetition** | Produces familiarity mistaken for mastery — an "illusion of knowing" |
| **Learning styles theory** | "Not confirmed by empirical research." No evidence that matching instruction to visual/auditory/kinesthetic preferences improves outcomes |

## Practical Application Guide

### For Students
1. **Self-quiz as primary study method** — replace rereading
2. **Space reviews**: within a day → several days → a week → monthly
3. **Interleave**: mix problem types and subjects within each session
4. **Elaborate**: ask "why does this work this way?" and "how does this connect to X?"
5. **Generate before reading**: attempt problems before the chapter
6. **Reflect after sessions**: what went well, what needs work, how concepts connect
7. **Calibrate with low-stakes quizzes**: don't trust feelings of familiarity
8. **Write summaries from memory** after readings, not during
9. **Explain concepts aloud** without consulting notes
10. **Use flashcards with spaced repetition**: shuffle until mastered, then revisit periodically

### For Teachers
1. Use frequent low-stakes cumulative quizzes (not just recent material)
2. Explain to students why these methods feel harder but work better
3. Design assignments requiring generation and elaboration
4. Use interleaved problem sets rather than blocked practice
5. Provide regular formative assessments with feedback

### For Knowledge Workers and Lifelong Learners
1. Practice reflection through self-questioning after every learning session
2. Teach others to solidify your own knowledge
3. Accept struggle as a sign of productive learning, not failure
4. Use mnemonic devices (imagery, acronyms, method of loci) for factual memorization
5. Develop deliberate practice habits: specific goals, immediate feedback, repetition

## Relevance to Knowledge Management

The strategies in *Make It Stick* map directly to Zettelkasten and SlipBox knowledge management practices:

- **Retrieval practice** parallels the Zettelkasten principle of writing notes in your own words from memory rather than copying — the act of reformulation is a retrieval event
- **Elaborative interrogation** ("why is this true?" / "how does this connect?") is precisely what happens when creating links between notes — each link forces an explanation of the relationship
- **Spaced repetition** aligns with periodic review of notes and the SlipBox practice of revisiting and refining notes over time
- **Interleaving** maps to the Zettelkasten practice of mixing topics within a single note-taking session rather than processing one subject exhaustively before moving to the next
- **Generation** connects to the SlipBox "Express" stage (C.O.D.E. framework) — attempting to answer questions from vault knowledge before looking up answers
- **Calibration** is supported by graph-aware search: the vault reveals what you actually know (linked, well-documented) vs. what you think you know (ghost notes, stubs, isolated concepts)
