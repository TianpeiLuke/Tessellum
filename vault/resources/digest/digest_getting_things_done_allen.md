## Overview

*Getting Things Done* presents a complete methodology for managing all "open loops" — anything that has your attention but hasn't been clarified and organized — by externalizing commitments from your mind into a trusted system. Allen's core thesis is counterintuitive: **productivity is directly proportional to the ability to relax**. When your mind is occupied holding onto unprocessed commitments ("open loops"), it cannot think clearly, creatively, or strategically. By capturing everything, clarifying what each item means, organizing outcomes into appropriate containers, reflecting regularly, and engaging with confidence, you achieve what Allen calls a "mind like water" — a state of calm responsiveness where your attention is fully available for the task at hand.

What distinguishes GTD from other productivity systems is its **bottom-up approach**. Unlike top-down goal-setting frameworks (e.g., OKRs, SMART goals), GTD argues that people cannot focus on high-level vision until they have control over the immediate noise of daily commitments. The methodology starts at "ground level" (current actions) and works upward through the six Horizons of Focus to life purpose. This is pragmatic rather than aspirational — Allen's insight is that the executive worrying about an unanswered email cannot simultaneously think strategically about organizational vision. Clear the runway first, then climb to altitude.

The 2015 revised edition updates the original 2001 text with a new chapter on GTD and cognitive science (Chapter 14), reflecting two decades of neuroscience research that validates Allen's experiential observations — particularly the Zeigarnik Effect (incomplete tasks occupy working memory) and the limited capacity of short-term memory.

## Key Frameworks

### The Five-Step Workflow

The core GTD methodology operates as a continuous cycle:

| Step | Action | Key Question | Output |
|------|--------|-------------|--------|
| **1. Capture** | Collect everything that has your attention | "What's on my mind?" | Items in an inbox (physical or digital) |
| **2. Clarify** | Process each item to determine meaning and action | "Is it actionable? What's the next action?" | Actionable items or reference/trash |
| **3. Organize** | Place items in the correct category | "Where does this belong?" | 7 organized lists/containers |
| **4. Reflect** | Review the system regularly | "Is everything current and complete?" | Updated, trusted system |
| **5. Engage** | Choose and execute actions with confidence | "What should I do right now?" | Completed actions |

### The Clarifying Flowchart

Allen's decision tree for processing each inbox item is the operational heart of GTD:

1. **What is it?** — Identify the item
2. **Is it actionable?**
   - **No** → Trash, Someday/Maybe, or Reference
   - **Yes** → What's the desired outcome (project)? What's the next action?
3. **Will the next action take less than 2 minutes?**
   - **Yes** → Do it now (Two-Minute Rule)
   - **No** → Delegate it (Waiting For) or Defer it (Next Actions list / Calendar)

### Seven Core Organization Categories

| Category | Contains | Review Frequency |
|----------|----------|-----------------|
| **Projects List** | Any outcome requiring 2+ actions | Weekly |
| **Project Support** | Plans, notes, and materials for active projects | As needed |
| **Calendar** | Time-specific actions and day-specific information only | Daily |
| **Next Actions** | Organized by context (@office, @calls, @computer, @errands) | Constantly |
| **Waiting For** | Delegated actions with dates and expected outcomes | Weekly |
| **Reference** | Non-actionable information you may need later | As needed |
| **Someday/Maybe** | Interests, ideas, and possibilities — not committed | Weekly |

**Critical distinction**: The calendar is *only* for items with a hard date/time. "Might do Tuesday" goes on Next Actions, not the calendar. This prevents calendar bloat and preserves trust in the system.

### Horizons of Focus (Six Levels)

Perspective-setting framework from ground level to life purpose:

| Horizon | Altitude | Focus | Time Frame | Example |
|---------|----------|-------|------------|---------|
| **Ground** | Runway | Current next actions | Now | "Call vendor about invoice" |
| **Horizon 1** | 10,000 ft | Current projects | 1-4 weeks | "Launch new dashboard" |
| **Horizon 2** | 20,000 ft | Areas of focus & accountability | Ongoing | "Team management", "Health" |
| **Horizon 3** | 30,000 ft | Goals & objectives | 1-2 years | "Get promoted to L7" |
| **Horizon 4** | 40,000 ft | Vision | 3-5 years | "Lead a research org" |
| **Horizon 5** | 50,000+ ft | Purpose & principles | Life | "Make knowledge accessible" |

**Key insight**: GTD works bottom-up. Most productivity systems start at Horizon 5 (purpose) and work down. Allen argues this fails because unprocessed commitments at Ground level consume the cognitive resources needed for higher-level thinking. Clear the runway first.

### Natural Planning Model (Five Phases)

How the mind naturally plans any project (contrast with "unnatural" top-down planning):

| Phase | Function | Example (Planning a Dinner Party) |
|-------|----------|-----------------------------------|
| **1. Purpose & Principles** | Why are we doing this? What are the boundaries? | "Celebrate a friend's promotion; budget under $200" |
| **2. Vision / Outcome** | What does wild success look like? | "12 people, great food, laughter, memorable evening" |
| **3. Brainstorming** | What are all possible ideas? (no judgment) | "Venue, menu, guest list, decorations, music, transportation..." |
| **4. Organizing** | What are the components, sequences, priorities? | "Book venue before invitations; menu before shopping" |
| **5. Next Actions** | What's the very next physical thing to do? | "Call Restaurant X about availability for Saturday" |

### Four-Criteria Model for Choosing Actions

When deciding what to do *right now*, filter by:

1. **Context** — Where are you? What tools are available? (@office, @phone, @home, @anywhere)
2. **Time available** — How much time before the next commitment?
3. **Energy available** — What is your current cognitive/physical energy level?
4. **Priority** — Given the above constraints, what has the highest payoff?

### The Threefold Model for Evaluating Daily Work

At any point, you are doing one of three types of work:

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Predefined work** | Tasks on your Next Actions lists | Processing email, writing a report |
| **Work as it appears** | Unplanned inputs that seem urgent | Colleague walks in with a question |
| **Defining your work** | Processing inboxes and clarifying commitments | Weekly Review, inbox processing |

The danger is spending all time on Type 2 (reactive) while neglecting Type 3 (defining). GTD's system ensures Type 3 happens regularly through the Weekly Review.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Your mind is for having ideas, not holding them.** Any commitment held in your head instead of a trusted external system occupies working memory and generates stress — even when you're not consciously thinking about it (Zeigarnik Effect).

2. **The Two-Minute Rule**: If the next action takes less than two minutes, do it immediately. The overhead of tracking it exceeds the cost of just doing it. This single rule can clear 30-50% of a typical inbox.

3. **"Next action" thinking is transformative.** Asking "What's the very next physical action?" converts vague intentions ("handle the marketing thing") into executable tasks ("Draft email to Sarah requesting Q2 budget numbers"). This is the single most powerful habit in GTD.

4. **The Weekly Review is the critical success factor.** Set aside ~2 hours weekly to: Get Clear (process all inboxes), Get Current (update all lists), Get Creative (review Someday/Maybe, generate new ideas). The system degrades without this discipline.

5. **Context-based organization > priority-based.** Organizing by context (@phone, @computer, @errands) lets you batch similar actions and choose appropriately based on where you are and what tools are available.

6. **Projects are outcomes, not tasks.** Any desired outcome requiring two or more actions is a "project" in GTD. Most people have 30-100 active projects. Maintaining a complete projects list prevents things from falling through cracks.

7. **Capture must be complete to build trust.** If even one open loop stays in your head, your mind won't trust the external system — and you lose the cognitive benefit. 100% capture is the price of admission.

8. **The calendar is sacred.** Only hard-landscape items go on the calendar — things that *must* happen at a specific time. Daily task lists on the calendar erode trust because unfinished items create a pile of broken commitments.

9. **Outcome thinking activates the reticular activating system.** Defining what "done" looks like primes your brain to notice relevant opportunities and resources — the same mechanism behind "you notice the car you're thinking of buying everywhere."

10. **Bottom-up before top-down.** You can't focus on life purpose (Horizon 5) while your inbox is overflowing and you've forgotten to call the plumber. GTD clears the lower horizons so higher-level thinking becomes possible.

11. **Reference is not action.** Separating reference material from actionable items prevents the "someday I'll get to that pile" syndrome. If it's not actionable now, file it where you can find it or put it on Someday/Maybe.

12. **Three tiers of mastery**: (1) Employing the fundamentals of managing workflow; (2) Implementing a more elevated, integrated total life management system; (3) Leveraging skills to create clear space and get things done in an ever-expanding way.

## The Weekly Review Process

The Weekly Review is GTD's maintenance ritual — the mechanism that keeps the entire system trustworthy:

| Phase | Actions | Purpose |
|-------|---------|---------|
| **Get Clear** | Process all inboxes to zero; collect loose papers, notes, receipts; empty your head with a "mind sweep" | Remove all unprocessed inputs |
| **Get Current** | Review Next Actions lists (mark completed, add new); review Calendar (upcoming week + previous week for triggers); review Waiting For (follow up on stalled items); review Projects list (ensure each has a next action) | Ensure all lists reflect reality |
| **Get Creative** | Review Someday/Maybe list; review goals and vision (Horizons 3-5); consider new projects or ideas triggered by the review | Generate forward-looking creative thinking |

**Recommended**: Friday afternoon or end-of-workweek — arrives at the weekend with a clear mind.

## Comparison with Other Productivity Approaches

| Dimension | GTD | Atomic Habits | Eisenhower Matrix | OKRs |
|-----------|-----|---------------|-------------------|------|
| **Direction** | Bottom-up (actions → purpose) | Inside-out (identity → outcomes) | Top-down (priority → action) | Top-down (objectives → results) |
| **Unit** | Next action | Atomic habit | Prioritized task | Key result |
| **Strength** | Comprehensive capture; stress reduction | Behavior change; long-term compounding | Quick prioritization | Organizational alignment |
| **Weakness** | Setup overhead; requires discipline | Less effective for one-off tasks | Oversimplifies complex workflows | Doesn't address daily execution |
| **Review cadence** | Weekly | Daily (habit tracking) | Ad hoc | Quarterly |
| **Core mechanism** | Externalize to trusted system | Identity-based behavior loops | Urgent/Important quadrant | Measurable outcomes |

## Relevance to Our Work

GTD's methodology transfers directly to knowledge management and analytical workflows:

- **The capture-clarify-organize cycle mirrors the Zettelkasten workflow** — fleeting notes (capture), literature notes (clarify), permanent notes (organize). Both systems externalize thinking into a trusted repository and use regular review to maintain integrity. The vault's inbox → processing → filing pipeline is a GTD implementation for knowledge.

- **"Next action" thinking applies to investigation workflows** — when an abuse case arrives, the investigator's instinct is often to look at the whole case. GTD's discipline of asking "What's the very next physical action?" prevents analysis paralysis and moves cases forward. This connects to SOPs that decompose investigations into sequential steps.

- **The Weekly Review parallels vault maintenance** — the `/slipbox-run-incremental-update` and periodic `/slipbox-check-broken-links` workflows function as a "Weekly Review" for the knowledge system, ensuring the vault remains trustworthy and current.

- **Horizons of Focus maps to organizational planning** — Ground (daily tasks) → H1 (current projects/sprints) → H2 (program areas like DNR, URES) → H3 (annual goals/OP1) → H4 (team vision) → H5 (organizational mission). This connects to [Planning Fallacy](../term_dictionary/term_planning_fallacy.md) — GTD's bottom-up approach counteracts the tendency to plan at H3-H5 without accounting for H0-H1 reality.

- **"Open loops" and cognitive load** connect to Kahneman's [System 1 and System 2](../term_dictionary/term_system_1_and_system_2.md) framework — unprocessed open loops consume System 2 bandwidth, degrading decision quality. GTD's externalization frees System 2 for analytical work. See also [WYSIATI](../term_dictionary/term_wysiati.md) — a cleared mind can look beyond "what's available" to consider the full picture.
