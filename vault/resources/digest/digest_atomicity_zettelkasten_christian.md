# Digest: Create Zettel from Reading Notes — The Principle of Atomicity

## Source

- **Blog Post**: [Create Zettel from Reading Notes According to the Principle of Atomicity](https://zettelkasten.de/posts/create-zettel-from-reading-notes/)
- **Author**: Christian Tietze — co-creator of zettelkasten.de, long-time Zettelkasten practitioner and software developer (creator of *The Archive* app)
- **Site**: [zettelkasten.de](https://zettelkasten.de/) — the leading English-language resource on the Zettelkasten method
- **Published**: September 3, 2013 (revised 2025)
- **Accessed**: March 14, 2026
- **Supplementary**: [The Principle of Atomicity – On the Difference Between a Principle and Its Implementation](https://zettelkasten.de/posts/principle-of-atomicity-is-not-its-implementation/) by Sascha (August 12, 2025)

## Overview

Christian Tietze introduces the **Principle of Atomicity** — the foundational Zettelkasten guideline that each note should contain one self-contained unit of knowledge. The phrase, coined by Christian in this 2013 post, borrows from software engineering's "separation of concerns": *put things which belong together in a Zettel, but try to separate concerns from one another*. The post frames this within a three-phase GTD-inspired workflow for converting reading notes into permanent Zettel.

The original post (~1,000 words) is practical and prescriptive, offering a concrete inbox-processing workflow. It was revised in 2025 with a note acknowledging that "the principle of atomicity" became a "hindrance for many" — people interpreted it as a rigid rule ("one idea per note") rather than a guiding principle. Sascha's 2025 follow-up article elaborates on this distinction, proposing the Six Knowledge Building Blocks framework as a more precise implementation guide.

This digest covers both the original post and Sascha's follow-up, with focus on the atomicity principle itself — its definition, its misinterpretation, and how the Zettelkasten community has evolved its understanding.

## Key Frameworks / Core Concepts

### The Three-Phase Workflow (GTD-Inspired)

Christian's process for converting reading notes into permanent Zettel mirrors GTD's inbox processing:

| Phase | Action | Parallel |
|-------|--------|----------|
| **1. Pull** | Collect all reading notes about a source into one place | GTD: empty inbox into processing area |
| **2. Cluster** | Group related notes by topic; draw boundaries between concerns | GTD: categorize and decide |
| **3. Write** | Create one Zettel per cluster; each becomes a self-contained note | GTD: file into trusted system |

The clustering step is where atomicity comes in — it's the moment where you decide what belongs together and what should be separated.

### The Principle of Atomicity

> "Put things which belong together in a Zettel, but try to separate concerns from one another."

Christian borrows the term "separation of concerns" from software engineering, where it means designing systems so that each module handles one thing well. Applied to Zettelkasten:

- **Each Zettel should be self-contained** — understandable without requiring other notes
- **Each Zettel should address one concern** — not two unrelated ideas crammed together
- **"Atomic" does not mean "tiny"** — an atom is the smallest *meaningful* unit, which may span several paragraphs if the concern demands it
- **The boundary question**: "Does this note try to say two separable things?" If yes, split.

### The Misinterpretation Problem (2025 Revision)

Christian's 2025 revision acknowledges the concept's unintended consequences:

1. **Rigid interpretation**: Many practitioners took "one idea per note" as an absolute rule, leading to anxiously tiny notes that lacked context
2. **Paralysis**: Writers would agonize over whether a note was "truly atomic" instead of just writing
3. **The revision**: Christian added a note directing readers to Sascha's follow-up article for a more nuanced treatment

### Principle vs. Implementation (Sascha, 2025)

Sascha's follow-up article draws a critical distinction:

| Aspect | Principle | Implementation |
|--------|-----------|----------------|
| **Nature** | Guiding idea — "make notes self-contained and focused" | Concrete rule — "one idea per note" |
| **Flexibility** | Adapts to context; the practitioner decides what counts as "one concern" | Fixed rule; no room for judgment |
| **Failure mode** | Too vague for beginners who need concrete guidance | Too rigid for intermediate practitioners; causes anxiety |

Sascha identifies the **[beetle-in-a-box](../term_dictionary/term_beetle_in_a_box.md) phenomenon** (Wittgenstein): everyone uses "atomicity" and "one idea per note," but each person defines the terms differently. The phrase has become a floating signifier in the community — seemingly shared but privately interpreted.

### Six [Knowledge Building Blocks](../term_dictionary/term_knowledge_building_blocks.md) (Sascha)

To make atomicity actionable, Sascha proposes that knowledge notes are built from these six types of content:

| Building Block | Description | Example |
|----------------|-------------|---------|
| **Premises** | Claims or facts that support a conclusion | "Luhmann wrote 90,000 notes over 40 years" |
| **Logical Form** | The reasoning structure connecting premises to conclusions | "If X and Y, then Z" |
| **Conclusions** | The claim being argued for | "Zettelkasten enables prolific output" |
| **Definitions** | Precise meanings of terms used | "Atomicity means..." |
| **Distinctions** | Boundaries between related concepts | "Fleeting vs. permanent notes" |
| **Heuristics** | Rules of thumb for practice | "If a note tries to say two things, split it" |

An **argument** — the fundamental knowledge atom — consists of exactly three elements: premises, logical form, and conclusion. This gives practitioners a structural test: "Does my note contain one complete argument?"

### Connection to the Zettelkasten Iceberg

Sascha explicitly connects atomicity to his Zettelkasten Iceberg framework (Level 3: Knowledge Building). Atomicity operates at the level where raw notes are refined into knowledge structures. The Six Building Blocks map to:

- **Surface level** (collection): Reading notes, highlights — not yet atomic
- **Middle level** (processing): Clustering, separating concerns — applying the principle
- **Deep level** (building): Complete arguments with premises + logic + conclusion — atomic knowledge

## Key Takeaways

1. **Atomicity is a principle, not a rule** — "make notes self-contained and focused" is the spirit; "one idea per note" is one (often misapplied) implementation
2. **The software engineering parallel holds** — separation of concerns means each module (note) has one job, but that job can be complex
3. **The three-phase workflow works** — Pull reading notes → Cluster by topic → Write one Zettel per cluster
4. **The boundary test is practical** — "Does this note try to say two separable things?" is the key question
5. **Anxiety about atomicity is a sign of misunderstanding** — if you're paralyzed about whether a note is "truly atomic," you've mistaken a principle for a rule
6. **Arguments are the natural atoms** — premises + logical form + conclusion form the structural unit of knowledge
7. **The beetle-in-a-box problem is real** — community terms like "atomicity" and "one idea" need explicit definitions to be useful
8. **Input-first atomicity is counterproductive** — trying to decide atomicity while reading (before processing) leads to fragmented, underdeveloped notes

## Relevance to Our Work

This digest connects directly to several vault themes:

1. **Note processing pipeline**: Our vault uses a similar workflow — raw source material is processed through reading logs and digest notes before becoming structured term/area notes. The three-phase workflow (Pull → Cluster → Write) maps to our digest → term note → linked vault structure.

2. **Knowledge atomicity in practice**: Our [term notes](../term_dictionary/term_permanent_notes.md) are themselves atomic — each term note addresses one concept with definition, properties, and links. The Principle of Atomicity validates this approach.

3. **The Zettelkasten Iceberg connection**: Sascha's framework (digested in [digest_zettelkasten_iceberg_sascha](digest_zettelkasten_iceberg_sascha.md)) explicitly references atomicity as a Level 3 operation. This digest provides the original source.
