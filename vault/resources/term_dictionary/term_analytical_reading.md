# Term: Analytical Reading

## Definition

**Analytical reading** is the third and central level of Mortimer J. Adler's four-level reading framework, defined as the sustained, systematic effort to achieve full comprehension and critical evaluation of a demanding text. It is "thorough reading, complete reading, or good reading — the best reading you can do" (Adler & Van Doren, 1972). Where [inspectional reading](term_inspectional_reading.md) asks "What is this book about?", analytical reading demands "What does this book mean in detail, and is it true?"

Analytical reading is operationalized through **15 explicit rules** organized into three sequential stages — structural, interpretive, and critical. The stages must be followed in order: you must understand what the author is saying (structural + interpretive) before you are entitled to evaluate whether it is true (critical). This constraint — **understanding must precede judgment** — is the most violated and most important principle in the framework.

The 15 rules constitute the closest thing to a systematic algorithm for extracting understanding from a complex text. They apply to any demanding reading — research papers, technical documentation, philosophical arguments, policy proposals — not just books.

## Full Name

Also known as:
- **Close reading** (literary criticism term for detailed textual analysis)
- **Deep reading** (Maryanne Wolf's term for sustained, immersive comprehension)
- **Level 3 reading** (Adler's positional designation within the four levels)

Related but distinct:
- [Inspectional Reading](term_inspectional_reading.md) — Level 2; quick structural grasp; a prerequisite for analytical reading
- [Syntopical Reading](term_syntopical_reading.md) — Level 4; comparative reading across multiple sources; builds on analytical reading
- [Elaborative Interrogation](term_elaborative_interrogation.md) — self-directed "why" questioning; overlaps with the interpretive stage
- [Socratic Questioning](term_socratic_questioning.md) — guided inquiry that parallels the critical stage

## The 15 Rules of Analytical Reading

### Stage 1: Structural Understanding — "What is the book about?"

| Rule | Action | Technique |
|------|--------|-----------|
| **1** | **Classify** the work by kind and subject | Is it practical or theoretical? Fiction or expository? What domain? |
| **2** | **State the unity** in a single sentence | The "elevator pitch" — what ties everything together? |
| **3** | **Enumerate the major parts** in order and relation | Outline the work; show how parts connect to the whole |
| **4** | **Define the problems** the author is solving | What questions does the author set out to answer? |

### Stage 2: Interpretive Understanding — "What is being said in detail?"

| Rule | Action | Technique |
|------|--------|-----------|
| **5** | **Come to terms** with the author | Locate key words; determine precisely how the author uses them |
| **6** | **Grasp the leading propositions** | Find the most important sentences; extract the claims |
| **7** | **Know the author's arguments** | Reconstruct argument sequences; connect scattered passages |
| **8** | **Determine which problems are solved, which unsolved** | Distinguish resolved questions from remaining open issues |

### Stage 3: Critical Evaluation — "Is it true? What of it?"

**General maxims (intellectual etiquette):**

| Rule | Principle |
|------|-----------|
| **9** | **Complete understanding before criticism** — say "I understand" before "I agree/disagree" |
| **10** | **Disagree respectfully** — challenge ideas, not the person; avoid contentious debate |
| **11** | **Support disagreement with reasons** — distinguish knowledge from mere opinion |

**Specific criteria for disagreement:**

| Rule | Critique | Question |
|------|----------|----------|
| **12** | **Uninformed** | Is the author missing knowledge that would change the conclusion? |
| **13** | **Misinformed** | Does the author assert something factually incorrect? |
| **14** | **Illogical** | Does the reasoning contain non sequiturs, contradictions, or unsupported leaps? |
| **15** | **Incomplete** | Has the author failed to solve all stated problems, or oversimplified? |

**Critical constraint**: Unless you can show the author is uninformed (12), misinformed (13), illogical (14), or incomplete (15), you are obligated to agree — or at least suspend judgment.

## The Four Reader Questions

Analytical reading is guided by four universal questions (applicable at every reading level):

| # | Question | Stage | What It Addresses |
|---|----------|-------|-------------------|
| 1 | What is the book about as a whole? | Structural | Central theme and development |
| 2 | What is being said in detail, and how? | Interpretive | Ideas, assertions, arguments |
| 3 | Is it true, in whole or in part? | Critical | Validity — only after understanding |
| 4 | What of it? | Evaluative | Significance, implications, actions |
