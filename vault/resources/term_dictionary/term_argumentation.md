# Argumentation

## Definition

**Argumentation** is the interdisciplinary study and practice of constructing, presenting, analyzing, and evaluating arguments — structured sequences of reasoning in which claims are advanced and supported by evidence, reasons, and warrants. Unlike formal deductive logic, which deals with validity in abstract symbol systems, argumentation theory addresses how people actually reason and resolve disagreements in natural language contexts: courtrooms, academic papers, policy debates, everyday conversations, and — increasingly — AI-assisted decision systems. The field draws on logic, rhetoric, linguistics, philosophy, psychology, and communication studies.

The roots of argumentation lie in **Aristotle's** complementary treatises — the *Topics* (dialectical reasoning through question-and-answer), the *Rhetoric* (persuasion of audiences through ethos, pathos, and logos), and the *Prior Analytics* (formal syllogistic logic). Aristotle's key insight was that argumentation operates across all three domains simultaneously: a sound argument must be logically valid (logos), delivered credibly (ethos), and presented in a way that engages the audience (pathos). This tripartite foundation persists in modern argumentation theory, though each tradition has developed its own emphasis.

Modern argumentation theory crystallized in the mid-20th century through three landmark works published within a year of each other: **Stephen Toulmin's** *The Uses of Argument* (1958), which proposed a practical model of argument structure (claim, grounds, warrant, backing, qualifier, rebuttal) as an alternative to formal logic; **Chaim Perelman and Lucie Olbrechts-Tyteca's** *The New Rhetoric* (1958), which recentered argumentation on audience adherence rather than formal validity; and the subsequent development of **informal logic** as a field by scholars such as Ralph Johnson and J. Anthony Blair. These three streams — structural, rhetorical, and logical — continue to define the major approaches to argumentation today.

## Historical Context

The history of argumentation can be traced through five major periods:

1. **Classical Antiquity (5th c. BCE -- 4th c. BCE)**: The Sophists pioneered rhetorical argumentation as teachable skill. Plato critiqued rhetoric as manipulative in the *Gorgias* but acknowledged its proper use in the *Phaedrus*. Aristotle synthesized the field by treating dialectic (cooperative truth-seeking) and rhetoric (audience-directed persuasion) as complementary counterparts, both governed by logical structure.

2. **Medieval and Renaissance (5th -- 17th c.)**: Boethius subordinated rhetoric to dialectic. Scholastic philosophers (Aquinas, Ockham) refined syllogistic reasoning. The trivium (grammar, logic, rhetoric) institutionalized argumentation as a core educational discipline.

3. **Modern Logic and the Marginalization of Rhetoric (17th -- mid-20th c.)**: The rise of mathematical logic (Frege, Russell) pushed informal reasoning to the periphery. Argumentation was treated as a deficient form of formal proof rather than a legitimate domain of study.

4. **The Argumentation Renaissance (1958 -- 1990s)**: Toulmin, Perelman, and Hamblin independently challenged formal logic's monopoly on reasoning. Van Eemeren and Grootendorst developed pragma-dialectics at the University of Amsterdam. Johnson and Blair established informal logic as a discipline.

5. **Contemporary Developments (1990s -- present)**: Computational argumentation, argumentation in AI (argument mining, automated reasoning), and integration with cognitive science. The study of argumentation now spans abstract argumentation frameworks (Dung, 1995), dialogue systems, and applied fields such as law, medicine, and policy analysis.

## Taxonomy

| Approach | Key Scholars | Core Idea | Evaluation Criterion |
|----------|-------------|-----------|---------------------|
| **Classical Rhetoric** | Aristotle, Cicero, Quintilian | Argumentation as audience-directed persuasion through ethos, pathos, logos | Effectiveness — does the argument persuade the intended audience? |
| **Toulmin Model** | Stephen Toulmin | Argument as a six-part structure: claim, grounds, warrant, backing, qualifier, rebuttal | Soundness — are all components present, warranted, and qualified? |
| **Pragma-Dialectics** | van Eemeren, Grootendorst | Argumentation as a procedure for resolving disagreements through critical discussion | Reasonableness — does the argument follow the ten rules of critical discussion? |
| **Informal Logic** | Johnson, Blair, Walton | Argumentation as everyday reasoning evaluated by relevance, sufficiency, and acceptability (RSA) | Cogency — are premises relevant, sufficient, and acceptable? |
| **New Rhetoric** | Perelman, Olbrechts-Tyteca | Argumentation as securing adherence of a universal audience through quasi-logical, reality-based, and dissociative techniques | Adherence — would a universal audience accept the argument? |
| **Formal Dialectics** | Hamblin, Barth, Krabbe | Argumentation as rule-governed dialogue games with obligations and commitments | Correctness — are the dialogue rules followed? |

## Key Properties

- **Distinct from formal logic**: Argumentation deals with defeasible, context-dependent reasoning in natural language — not truth-preserving derivations in formal systems. Arguments can be strong without being formally valid.
- **Audience-relative**: The same argument succeeds or fails depending on the audience's shared warrants, values, and background knowledge. This is Perelman's central insight and the practical lesson of Booth's warrant analysis.
- **Normative and descriptive**: Argumentation theory is both prescriptive (what makes a good argument?) and descriptive (how do people actually argue?). Pragma-dialectics explicitly bridges these through its ideal model of critical discussion.
- **Defeasible**: Unlike deductive proofs, arguments can be defeated by new evidence, stronger counter-arguments, or exposed warrant failures — argumentation is inherently revisable.
- **Dialogical**: Most argumentation theories treat arguments not as monologues but as exchanges between a proponent and an opponent (or an author and a skeptical reader). Even solo writing implicitly addresses an imagined critic.
- **Fallacy-connected**: Each major argumentation theory generates its own account of fallacies — violations of the standards that theory sets for good argument. The pragma-dialectical rules, Toulmin's component failures, and informal logic's RSA criteria all produce different but overlapping fallacy taxonomies.
- **Multi-modal**: Arguments combine logos (logical structure), ethos (credibility), and pathos (emotional engagement). Effective argumentation calibrates all three modes to the audience and context.
- **Transferable across domains**: The same argumentation structures apply in academic research, legal reasoning, policy debate, business cases, and everyday disagreements — differing only in domain-specific warrants and evidence standards.

## Related Terms

### Core Components
- **[Research Argument](term_research_argument.md)**: Booth's five-component model (claim, reasons, evidence, warrants, acknowledgments) is a practical adaptation of Toulmin's model for academic writing — the most directly applied argumentation framework in this vault
- **[Logos](term_logos.md)**: The logical structure of an argument — one of Aristotle's three modes of persuasion and the dimension most directly addressed by argumentation theory
- **[Ethos](term_ethos.md)**: Speaker credibility — argumentation theory shows that logical structure alone is insufficient; the source must be trustworthy
- **[Pathos](term_pathos.md)**: Emotional engagement — argumentation theory shows that logical structure alone is insufficient; the audience must also care about the outcome
- **[Persuasion](term_persuasion.md)**: The broader process of changing minds through communication; argumentation provides the logical backbone (logos) while persuasion adds delivery (ethos + pathos)

### Argumentation Practices
- **[Steelmanning](term_steelmanning.md)**: Constructing the strongest version of an opponent's argument — a practice that embodies the pragma-dialectical ideal of engaging charitably with opposing positions
- **[Socratic Questioning](term_socratic_questioning.md)**: A dialectical method that probes the components of an argument through systematic questioning — testing claims, reasons, evidence, and warrants
- **[Active Listening](term_active_listening.md)**: A prerequisite for effective argumentation — understanding the opponent's actual position before constructing a response

### Fallacies (Argumentation Failures)
- **[Logical Fallacies](term_logical_fallacies.md)**: Systematic violations of argumentation standards — each fallacy type maps to a failure in a specific argument component
- **[Straw Man](term_straw_man.md)**: Distorting an opponent's argument to make it easier to attack — a violation of the pragma-dialectical rule requiring faithful representation
- **[Ad Hominem](term_ad_hominem.md)**: Attacking the arguer rather than the argument — a violation of the rule separating argument quality from arguer characteristics
- **[Circular Reasoning](term_circular_reasoning.md)**: Using the conclusion as a premise — a structural failure where the warrant is identical to the claim
- **[Critical Thinking](term_critical_thinking.md)**: The parent discipline of evaluative reasoning; argumentation provides the structures critical thinking evaluates

### Computational Argumentation
- **[QBAF](term_qbaf.md)**: Quantitative Bipolar Argumentation Framework — the modern formal-graph counterpart to Toulmin's structural model, with weighted arguments and bipolar attack/support edges
- **[DF-QuAD](term_df_quad.md)**: Discontinuity-free gradual semantics that aggregates a QBAF into a deterministic dialectical strength — the algorithmic counterpart to argument evaluation in pragma-dialectics
- **[Contestability](term_contestability.md)**: Formal AI-system property — the engineering analogue of the argumentation-theory principle that every claim must remain open to challenge
