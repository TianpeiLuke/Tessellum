# Term: Question Storming

## Definition

**Question storming** (also called QStorming or question brainstorming) is an ideation technique in which participants generate only questions -- not answers -- about a challenge, opportunity, or topic within a defined time period. By suspending the drive to solve and instead focusing on how a problem is framed, question storming opens new lines of inquiry that traditional brainstorming (which generates answers and solutions) systematically misses. The technique rests on a fundamental insight: the quality of solutions is bounded by the quality of questions that precede them; reframing a problem through better questions often matters more than generating more answers to a poorly framed problem.

The technique has been developed independently by several practitioners. **Hal Gregersen**, executive director of the MIT Leadership Center, formalized it as the **"Question Burst"** method, described in his book *Questions Are the Answer* (2018). Warren Berger popularized it in *A More Beautiful Question* (2014) as a core technique for innovative organizations. Dan Rothstein and Luz Santana of the **Right Question Institute** developed the **Question Formulation Technique (QFT)** as a structured classroom method for teaching students to generate, improve, and prioritize their own questions. Terry Fadem, in *The Art of Asking* (2009), positioned question-focused inquiry as a management skill distinct from answer-focused inquiry.

What unites these approaches is a shared structural insight: when people are asked to generate answers, social pressure to appear competent causes self-censorship and convergent thinking; when people are asked to generate questions, the lower perceived risk of "just asking" produces more diverse, creative, and surprising lines of inquiry. Questions are judged less harshly than answers, so question storming reduces the inhibition that makes traditional brainstorming less effective in practice than in theory.

## Core Methodology: The Question Burst Process

Hal Gregersen's Question Burst is the most rigorously documented version, based on over 200 interviews with creative leaders and tested at organizations including Chanel, Disney-Pixar, Genentech, Patagonia, Salesforce, and Zappos.

### Step 1: Set Up (2 minutes)

- One person presents a challenge they are genuinely stuck on -- briefly (1-2 sentences)
- The presenter does NOT explain context, background, or what they have already tried
- Minimal context forces questions to come from fresh perspectives

### Step 2: Generate Questions (4 minutes)

- All participants generate questions about the challenge as rapidly as possible
- **Two strict rules**:
  1. **No answers allowed** -- only questions
  2. **No explanations** -- do not justify why you are asking a particular question
- The presenter records every question verbatim (acts as scribe, not participant)
- Goal: generate at least 15-20 questions in four minutes
- An ideal session generates approximately 50 questions

### Step 3: Select and Commit (2-5 minutes)

- Review all questions
- Identify 3-5 "catalytic questions" -- questions that feel most surprising, uncomfortable, or perspective-shifting
- The presenter commits to pursuing at least one new pathway opened by these questions

### Key Design Principles

| Principle | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Time-boxed** | Urgency prevents overthinking and self-censorship |
| **Questions only** | Suspends judgment; removes performance pressure |
| **No explanation** | Prevents justification that narrows the inquiry |
| **Verbatim recording** | Preserves exact phrasing; subtle wording differences matter |
| **Fresh perspectives** | Minimal context forces outsider viewpoints |

## The Question Formulation Technique (QFT)

Developed by Dan Rothstein and Luz Santana of the Right Question Institute, the QFT is a structured six-step process used primarily in education:

1. **Design a Question Focus (QFocus)**: Present a statement, image, or prompt -- NOT a question -- as the stimulus
2. **Produce questions**: Generate as many questions as possible following four rules:
   - Ask as many questions as you can
   - Do not stop to discuss, judge, or answer
   - Write down every question exactly as stated
   - Change any statements into questions
3. **Categorize questions**: Sort into open-ended and closed-ended
4. **Prioritize questions**: Select the three most important questions
5. **Plan next steps**: Determine how to use the priority questions
6. **Reflect**: What did you learn? How can you use this going forward?

## Psychological Mechanisms

### Why Question Storming Works

- **Reduced evaluation apprehension**: Questions feel lower-risk than proposed solutions; participants fear judgment less
- **Reframing effect**: Questions force consideration of alternative problem framings, not just alternative solutions
- **Perspective-taking**: Each question implicitly adopts a different viewpoint, surfacing blind spots
- **Suspension of expertise**: Experts naturally gravitate toward answers; forcing question-only mode temporarily disables expertise bias

### Three Typical Outcomes (Gregersen)

After participating in a question burst, people usually experience one of three outcomes:

1. **New energy**: A more positive state of mind about their challenge, leading to new and valuable ideas
2. **Expanded scope**: A realization that the problem is much bigger (or different) than initially understood
3. **Self-implication**: A recognition that they themselves are part of the problem

## Key Research & Evidence

### Origins and Key Practitioners
- **Hal Gregersen (2018)**: *Questions Are the Answer: A Breakthrough Approach to Your Most Vexing Problems at Work and in Life* (Harper Business). Based on 200+ interviews with innovators including Elon Musk and Jeff Bezos. Nautilus Award winner. Gregersen is executive director of the MIT Leadership Center and a senior lecturer at MIT Sloan.
- **Warren Berger (2014)**: *A More Beautiful Question* -- documented question storming as a core technique used by innovative companies. Showed how generating questions before answers leads to more breakthrough innovations.
- **Dan Rothstein & Luz Santana (2011)**: *Make Just One Change: Teach Students to Ask Their Own Questions* -- developed the Question Formulation Technique at the Right Question Institute. Demonstrated that students who learn to formulate their own questions show greater engagement and deeper learning.
- **Terry Fadem (2009)**: *The Art of Asking* -- distinguished question-focused inquiry from answer-focused inquiry in management contexts. Positioned "What If" questions as the hallmark of creative organizations.

### Organizational Adoption
Question storming / Question Burst has been used at: Chanel, Daimler, Disney-Pixar, Ernst & Young, Fidelity, Genentech, Microsoft, NASA, Patagonia, PepsiCo, Salesforce, Starbucks, U.S. Army, and Zappos.

## Practical Applications

### In Innovation and Product Development
- Use question storming at the beginning of a product cycle to ensure the team is solving the right problem, not just efficiently solving the wrong one
- Replace the first 15 minutes of a brainstorming session with question generation to improve the quality of subsequent ideation

### In Knowledge Management and Analytical Decision-Making
- Question storming is applicable to vault exploration: instead of searching for answers, generate questions about a domain and let the knowledge graph reveal unexpected connections
- In abuse prevention, question storming applied to a case review can surface investigative angles that standard checklists miss -- e.g., "Why does this account's return pattern change after contact with customer service?" rather than simply checking whether the return rate exceeds a threshold

### In Education
- The QFT has been adopted in thousands of classrooms; students who formulate their own questions demonstrate higher engagement and deeper comprehension than those who only answer teacher-generated questions

### Connection to Design Thinking
- Question storming is particularly effective in the Define and Ideate phases of design thinking, where reframing the problem through better questions directly improves the quality of generated solutions

## Criticisms & Limitations

- **Requires psychological safety**: In hierarchical organizations, junior members may still self-censor even when "only" generating questions, if questions that challenge leadership assumptions feel risky
- **Quality variance**: Not all questions are equal; without follow-up prioritization and analysis, a list of 50 questions can feel overwhelming rather than illuminating
- **Limited for well-defined problems**: When the problem is already clearly defined and decomposed (e.g., a known engineering constraint), question storming adds less value than direct analytical approaches
- **Facilitation dependency**: The technique's effectiveness depends heavily on skilled facilitation -- maintaining the "no answers" rule, encouraging quantity, and guiding the selection phase
- **Novelty effect**: Some evidence suggests that question storming's benefits may diminish with repeated use in the same group if it becomes routinized
