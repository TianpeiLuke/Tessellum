# Self-Management (Emotional Intelligence)

## Definition

**Self-management** is the ability to use awareness of your emotions to stay flexible and positively direct your behavior (Bradberry & Greaves, 2009). It is the second of four core emotional intelligence competencies in both Goleman's (1995) and Bradberry's (2009) frameworks, and it depends directly on **self-awareness** — you cannot manage what you do not notice. Self-management is not the suppression of emotions but the conscious regulation of emotional reactions so that they serve rather than sabotage your goals.

Goleman's original model (1995) used the term **self-regulation** and identified five sub-competencies: emotional self-control, transparency, adaptability, achievement orientation, and optimism. Bradberry and Greaves reframed the concept as "self-management" and provided 17 actionable strategies, emphasizing that the skill is trainable and measurable through the EQ appraisal. The neurological basis involves the prefrontal cortex overriding impulsive signals from the amygdala — a process that is physically limited by fatigue, stress, and sleep deprivation.

## Key Properties

- **Builds on self-awareness**: Self-management is impossible without first recognizing your emotional state; self-awareness provides the raw data, self-management provides the response strategy
- **Includes impulse control**: The capacity to pause between stimulus and response — resisting the urge to react immediately, especially under stress or provocation — is the foundational behavioral expression of self-management
- **Involves self-talk management**: The internal narrative you maintain directly shapes emotional intensity; replacing automatic negative self-talk with rational, evidence-based self-talk is a primary self-management lever (Bradberry & Greaves, 2009)
- **Strengthened by sleep and physical health**: Self-management is a physiologically constrained resource — sleep deprivation impairs prefrontal cortex function, reducing impulse control and emotional regulation capacity; exercise, nutrition, and caffeine management directly affect EQ performance
- **Key strategies include counting to 10 and breathing techniques**: Simple physiological interventions — counting to 10 before responding, deep diaphragmatic breathing, and the "sleep on it" strategy — create temporal and cognitive distance from the triggering stimulus, allowing the prefrontal cortex to reassert control over the amygdala
- **Distinguishes reaction from response**: Reactions are automatic and emotion-driven (System 1); responses are deliberate and goal-aligned (System 2) — self-management is the practice of converting reactions into responses

## Related Terms

- **[Emotional Intelligence](term_emotional_intelligence.md)**: The overarching framework comprising self-awareness, self-management, social awareness, and relationship management; self-management is the second competency and the bridge between personal awareness and social effectiveness
- **[Self-Awareness](term_self_awareness.md)**: The prerequisite competency — accurate recognition of your emotions as they occur; without self-awareness, self-management degrades to suppression or avoidance
- **[Grit](term_grit.md)**: Perseverance and passion for long-term goals; grit provides the sustained motivation that self-management channels productively by preventing emotional derailment
- **[Habit Loop](term_habit_loop.md)**: The cue-craving-response-reward cycle; self-management strategies create new habit loops that replace impulsive reactions with deliberate responses
- **[Amygdala Hijack](term_amygdala_hijack.md)**: The phenomenon where the amygdala overrides rational processing during perceived threats; self-management techniques are specifically designed to prevent or recover from amygdala hijacks
