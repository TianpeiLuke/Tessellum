# Term: Design Thinking

## Definition

**Design thinking** is a human-centered, iterative problem-solving methodology that draws on methods from the design profession -- empathy with users, creative ideation, and rapid prototyping -- to tackle complex, ill-defined ("wicked") problems. Formalized by IDEO and the Stanford d.school (Hasso Plattner Institute of Design), design thinking structures innovation into five phases: **Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, and Test**. Unlike traditional analytical approaches that begin with a well-defined problem and proceed linearly to a solution, design thinking begins with deep understanding of human needs, reframes the problem based on that understanding, generates a broad range of possible solutions, and then iterates rapidly through prototyping and testing.

The methodology's intellectual roots extend to Herbert Simon's *The Sciences of the Artificial* (1969), which argued that design is a way of thinking applicable to any domain -- not just industrial or graphic design. In the 1990s and 2000s, David Kelley (founder of IDEO and the Stanford d.school) and Tim Brown (CEO of IDEO from 2000) rebranded and popularized the approach for the business world. In a pivotal 2003 conversation, Kelley and Brown decided to stop calling IDEO's approach "design" and start calling it "design thinking" -- making explicit that the methodology was about a cognitive approach to problem-solving, not about aesthetics or artifacts.

Design thinking occupies a distinctive position in the problem-solving landscape: it complements analytical frameworks like MECE decomposition (which assumes the problem can be cleanly structured) and systems thinking (which examines feedback loops and emergent behavior) by foregrounding the human experience as the starting point. As Warren Berger observes in *A More Beautiful Question*, design thinking is fundamentally driven by questioning -- the "Why / What If / How" cycle maps directly onto the design thinking phases, with Empathize + Define corresponding to "Why?", Ideate to "What If?", and Prototype + Test to "How?"

## Full Name

**Design Thinking** (also: Human-Centered Design, HCD)

Related frameworks:
- **Double Diamond** (UK Design Council) -- Discover, Define, Develop, Deliver
- **Design Sprint** (Google Ventures) -- compressed 5-day design thinking cycle
- **Lean UX** -- design thinking applied to agile software development
- **Service Design** -- design thinking applied to service ecosystems

## The Five Phases

### Phase 1: Empathize -- Understand the User

**Purpose**: Gain deep, empathetic understanding of the people for whom you are designing.

**Core activities**:
- Conduct interviews, observations, and immersion experiences
- Practice "beginner's mind" -- set aside assumptions about what users need
- Identify latent needs that users themselves may not articulate
- Build empathy maps documenting what users say, do, think, and feel

**Key principle**: The designer's job is not to impose solutions but to discover real human needs. This phase operationalizes Berger's "Why?" stage -- stepping back from routine to observe with fresh eyes and practice "vuja de."

### Phase 2: Define -- Frame the Problem

**Purpose**: Synthesize empathy findings into a clear, actionable problem statement (Point of View).

**Core activities**:
- Synthesize observations into insights
- Create a Point of View (POV) statement: [User] needs [need] because [insight]
- Reframe the challenge as a "How Might We...?" question
- Ensure the problem definition is human-centered, not technology-centered

**Key principle**: A well-framed problem is half-solved. The "How Might We" (HMW) format -- pioneered by IDEO -- uses the word "might" deliberately to open possibility without demanding commitment.

### Phase 3: Ideate -- Generate Solutions

**Purpose**: Produce a broad range of creative solutions through divergent thinking.

**Core activities**:
- Brainstorming (and question storming) with deferred judgment
- Mind mapping, sketching, and analogical thinking
- "Long-distance connections" between distant concepts (Berger)
- Quantity over quality in initial generation; evaluation comes later

**Key principle**: The goal is to explore the widest possible solution space before converging. This phase corresponds to Berger's "What If?" stage.

### Phase 4: Prototype -- Build to Think

**Purpose**: Create quick, low-fidelity representations of ideas to learn through making.

**Core activities**:
- Build physical or digital prototypes rapidly and cheaply
- Treat prototypes as "questions embodied" (Berger) -- each one asks "Will this work?"
- Use prototypes to generate feedback, not to demonstrate finished solutions
- Embrace failure as informational feedback

**Key principle**: Prototyping is a form of thinking, not a finishing step. Building something tangible surfaces assumptions that purely abstract reasoning misses.

### Phase 5: Test -- Learn and Iterate

**Purpose**: Put prototypes in front of real users and learn from their reactions.

**Core activities**:
- Observe how users interact with prototypes
- Gather feedback on what works, what confuses, what delights
- Iterate: refine prototypes, redefine the problem, or return to earlier phases
- Accept that the first solution is rarely the final solution

**Key principle**: Testing is not validation -- it is learning. The design thinking cycle is explicitly non-linear; testing frequently sends teams back to Empathize or Define.

## Key Research & Evidence

### Intellectual Origins
- **John E. Arnold (1950s)**: MIT/Stanford engineering professor; one of the first to write about "design thinking" as a creative cognitive approach to engineering problems.
- **Herbert Simon (1969)**: Published *The Sciences of the Artificial*, arguing that design is a general mode of reasoning applicable to any domain. Introduced the concept of "satisficing" -- seeking solutions that are "good enough" rather than optimal.
- **Robert McKim (1973)**: Published *Experiences in Visual Thinking* at Stanford; emphasized visual and spatial reasoning in design.
- **Peter Rowe (1987)**: Published *Design Thinking*, the first book with that exact title, analyzing how architects and urban planners approach problems.

### Formalization at IDEO and Stanford
- **David Kelley (1991-present)**: Founded IDEO; later founded the Stanford d.school (2004). Recognized as the person who brought design thinking from a professional practice to a teachable, cross-disciplinary methodology.
- **Tim Brown (2000-present)**: As CEO of IDEO, published *Change by Design* (2009), the definitive business articulation of design thinking. Argued that design thinking could tackle "wicked" problems in healthcare, education, and social innovation.
- **Hasso Plattner Institute of Design (d.school)**: Established at Stanford in 2004; developed the five-phase model taught worldwide.

### Empirical Validation
- Design thinking has been adopted by organizations including Apple, Google, IBM, SAP, P&G, Kaiser Permanente, and the Gates Foundation. IBM's Enterprise Design Thinking program trained over 100,000 employees. However, rigorous controlled studies measuring design thinking's effectiveness compared to alternative methodologies remain limited.

## Practical Applications

### In Product and Service Innovation
- Design thinking is the standard methodology at design consultancies (IDEO, frog, Continuum) and increasingly at technology companies for product development.

### In Knowledge Management and Analytical Decision-Making
- The Empathize phase maps to the Zettelkasten practice of capturing literature notes from diverse sources before synthesizing; the Define phase parallels formulating entry point questions for the vault.
- In abuse prevention, design thinking's emphasis on understanding the user (buyer, seller, or bad actor) prevents the common pitfall of designing enforcement systems based on internal assumptions rather than actual behavioral patterns.

### In Education
- Stanford's d.school, MIT's Media Lab, and hundreds of universities worldwide teach design thinking as a cross-disciplinary approach to complex problems.

### In Healthcare
- Doug Dietz (GE Healthcare) used design thinking to redesign the MRI experience for children -- reframing the intimidating machine as an adventure. Sedation needs dropped and patient satisfaction rose by 90%.

## Criticisms & Limitations

- **Overreliance on empathy without analytical rigor**: Critics argue that "empathize" can become performative -- brief user interviews mistaken for deep understanding.
- **Process fetishism**: Organizations may follow the five phases mechanically without the underlying mindset shift, reducing design thinking to a checklist.
- **Weak on systemic and structural problems**: Design thinking excels at user-facing innovation but may underweight structural, political, or systemic constraints that require systems thinking.
- **Limited empirical evidence**: Despite widespread adoption, controlled studies comparing design thinking to alternative problem-solving approaches are scarce (Auernhammer & Roth, 2021).
- **Exclusion concerns**: The emphasis on rapid prototyping and "fail fast" may disadvantage participants from cultures or organizations where failure carries significant social or professional cost.
- **Scope inflation**: The term has been applied so broadly (from product design to social policy to personal life) that it risks becoming meaningless.
