# Term: Natural Planning Model

## Definition

The **Natural Planning Model** is a five-phase framework for project planning described by David Allen in *Getting Things Done* (2001, revised 2015). It captures how the human mind naturally approaches any task requiring more than one action — from planning a dinner party to launching a product — and formalizes it into a repeatable process. Allen's key insight is that the brain already follows this sequence unconsciously for trivial tasks (e.g., deciding where to eat lunch), but most people abandon this natural process for complex projects, defaulting instead to "reactive planning" (gathering data, forming ideas, and responding to crises without a clear purpose or vision).

The five phases are: (1) **Define purpose and principles** — why are we doing this and what are the boundaries? (2) **Envision the outcome** — what does wild success look like? (3) **Brainstorm** — what are all possible ideas, unconstrained? (4) **Organize** — what are the components, sequences, and priorities? (5) **Identify next actions** — what is the very next physical thing to do? The model is explicitly **top-down then bottom-up**: it starts with purpose (the highest abstraction) and ends with a concrete physical action (the lowest abstraction). This bridges the gap between vision and execution that plagues most planning systems.

The Natural Planning Model contrasts with what Allen calls "unnatural planning" — the typical organizational default where a manager announces "We need a plan" and the team jumps to Phase 4 (organizing) without first establishing purpose, vision, or brainstorming. This produces plans that are technically structured but disconnected from intent, leading to misalignment, scope creep, and the need for frequent rework.

## The Five Phases

### Phase 1: Define Purpose and Principles

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Core question** | "Why are we doing this?" |
| **Output** | Clear statement of purpose + operating constraints |
| **Function** | Sets the success criteria and decision-making boundaries |

The purpose answers "why" and provides the motivation and decision filter for everything that follows. Principles define the boundaries of acceptable behavior ("We won't compromise quality for speed," "Budget must stay under $50K"). Without a stated purpose, teams solve problems that don't matter; without principles, they solve them in ways that create new problems.

### Phase 2: Envision the Outcome

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Core question** | "What does wild success look like?" |
| **Output** | A vivid mental image of the completed project |
| **Function** | Activates the reticular activating system (RAS); focuses attention |

Outcome visioning leverages the brain's **reticular activating system** — the neural network that filters sensory input based on current priorities. When you clearly define what "done" looks like, your brain begins noticing relevant resources, connections, and opportunities in the environment that were previously invisible. This is the same mechanism behind noticing a car model everywhere after you decide to buy one.

### Phase 3: Brainstorm

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Core question** | "What are all the things that come to mind about this?" |
| **Output** | Unconstrained list of ideas, associations, and possibilities |
| **Function** | Generates the raw material for organizing |

Brainstorming follows visioning because the mind naturally generates ideas to close the gap between "where we are" and "where we want to be." Allen emphasizes three rules: (1) don't judge, challenge, evaluate, or criticize during brainstorming; (2) go for quantity over quality; (3) put everything in view (mind maps, whiteboards, lists). The creative tension between current reality and the envisioned outcome drives ideation.

### Phase 4: Organize

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Core question** | "What are the components, sequences, and priorities?" |
| **Output** | Structured plan with milestones, sequences, and dependencies |
| **Function** | Imposes order on brainstormed material |

Organization occurs naturally after brainstorming — the mind identifies relationships, clusters ideas into categories, assigns sequences, and spots gaps. This is where traditional project planning tools (Gantt charts, work breakdown structures, MECE decomposition) become useful. The key is that organizing comes *after* unconstrained ideation, not before it.

### Phase 5: Identify Next Actions

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Core question** | "What's the very next physical thing to do?" |
| **Output** | A concrete, executable action for each open component |
| **Function** | Bridges planning to execution; creates momentum |

The most critical phase — and the one most often skipped. Without a concrete next action, projects stall in the "I should work on that sometime" limbo. Allen's discipline: the next action must be a **physical, visible activity** ("Draft the email to Sarah requesting budget numbers"), not a vague intention ("Handle the budget situation"). Every active project component must have a defined next action at all times.

## Natural vs. Unnatural Planning

| Dimension | Natural Planning | Unnatural (Reactive) Planning |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------------------------|
| **Starting point** | Purpose ("Why?") | Organization ("Let's plan") |
| **Sequence** | Purpose → Vision → Brainstorm → Organize → Next Actions | Jump to organizing; backfill purpose later |
| **Ideation** | Diverge first (brainstorm), then converge (organize) | Skip brainstorming; start with existing ideas |
| **Result** | Plans aligned with intent; next actions defined | Plans technically structured but disconnected from purpose |
| **Failure mode** | Over-planning (spending too long in Phase 2-3) | Misalignment, scope creep, "busy but not productive" |

## Applications

### Research and Paper Digestion

The paper digestion pipeline mirrors the Natural Planning Model:
1. **Purpose**: Why are we reading this paper? (relevance to current research questions)
2. **Vision**: What would a useful digest look like? (structured notes with vault connections)
3. **Brainstorm**: What are all the key ideas, methods, and findings? (reading and annotation)
4. **Organize**: How do these map to section notes? (intro, contrib, algo, exp_design, exp_result)
5. **Next Actions**: What's the first section to write? (usually the lit note index card)

### Investigation Workflow Design

SOPs for abuse investigation implicitly follow the Natural Planning Model:
1. **Purpose**: Determine whether abuse occurred (not "process the case")
2. **Vision**: What does a complete investigation look like? (all signals checked, decision documented)
3. **Brainstorm**: What signals, data sources, and patterns could be relevant?
4. **Organize**: Sequence the checks (highest-signal first, then corroborating evidence)
5. **Next Actions**: Pull the customer's order history

### Comparison with Other Planning Frameworks

| Framework | Focus | Relationship to NPM |
|-----------|-------|---------------------|
| **Design Thinking** | Empathize → Define → Ideate → Prototype → Test | Similar diverge-then-converge structure; DT adds user empathy and prototyping |
| **MECE Decomposition** | Mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive breakdown | Phase 4 (Organize) — MECE ensures no gaps in the breakdown |
| **OKR Planning** | Objectives → Key Results → Initiatives | Starts at Phase 2 (Vision/Outcome) — skips explicit purpose and brainstorming phases |
| **Agile Sprint Planning** | Backlog → Sprint Goal → Tasks | Phase 4-5 (Organize + Next Actions) — assumes purpose and vision are already defined |
| **Systems Thinking** | Interconnections, feedback loops, leverage points | Enriches Phase 3-4 — brainstorming connections and organizing with feedback loops in mind |

## Related Terms

- [Systems Thinking](term_systems_thinking.md) — enriches Phase 3-4 of the Natural Planning Model by surfacing interconnections and feedback loops that sequential planning misses
- [MECE](term_mece.md) — the decomposition discipline applied in Phase 4 (Organize) to ensure the plan has no gaps and no overlaps
- [Design Thinking](term_design_thinking.md) — parallel structure (Empathize → Define → Ideate → Prototype → Test); NPM adds explicit purpose/principles and next-action discipline
- [Socratic Questioning](term_socratic_questioning.md) — Phase 1 (Purpose) is Socratic: "Why are we doing this?" probes assumptions before committing to action
- [Open Loops](term_open_loops.md) — projects without defined next actions (Phase 5) are open loops that consume cognitive resources
- [Trusted System](term_trusted_system.md) — the Natural Planning Model's outputs must be captured in a trusted system to close open loops
- [Planning Fallacy](term_planning_fallacy.md) — Phase 2 (Vision) and Phase 5 (Next Actions) counteract the planning fallacy: vision grounds expectations, next actions ground execution
- [Flow State](term_flow_state.md) — clear purpose (Phase 1) and concrete next actions (Phase 5) create the goal clarity and immediate feedback conditions for flow
- [Question Storming](term_question_storming.md) — Phase 3 (Brainstorm) can be enhanced with question storming: generating questions about the project rather than solutions
- [Deliberate Practice](term_deliberate_practice.md) — applying the Natural Planning Model to skill development: define the purpose (what skill?), envision the target performance, brainstorm practice strategies
- [Cognitive Bias](term_cognitive_bias.md) — Phase 3's "no judgment" rule counteracts anchoring and confirmation bias during ideation
- [Intermediate Packets](term_intermediate_packets.md) — NPM Phase 4 (Organize) and Phase 5 (Next Actions) map to assembling IPs into project plans
- [CODE Method](term_code_method.md) — Allen's five-phase planning model complements CODE: NPM plans projects, CODE processes knowledge
- [Divergence and Convergence](term_divergence_and_convergence.md) — NPM Phase 3 (Brainstorm) is divergent; Phases 4-5 (Organize, Next Actions) are convergent
