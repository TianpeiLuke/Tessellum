# Term: Active Reading

## Definition

**Active reading** is the foundational principle of Mortimer J. Adler's reading methodology: reading is an **active skill** that can be systematically developed, not a passive reception of information. "The more active the reading, the better" (Adler & Van Doren, 1972). Most people plateau at elementary reading and never develop the skill further, yet the demands on their reading — technical papers, complex arguments, competing sources — escalate continuously.

Adler operationalizes active reading through **four universal questions** that the reader must ask of every text, at every reading level. The depth of the answers increases with each level (elementary → inspectional → analytical → syntopical), but the questions remain the same. A reader who consistently asks and answers these questions is a "demanding reader" — one who reads for understanding rather than mere information.

## Full Name

Also known as:
- **Demanding reading** (Adler's characterization of the active reader's mindset)
- **Reading for understanding** (contrasted with reading for information or entertainment)
- **Engaged reading** (educational psychology term)

Related but distinct:
- **Active learning** (ML) — machine learning paradigm for strategic sample selection; unrelated to reading methodology
- [Elaborative Interrogation](term_elaborative_interrogation.md) — specific "why" questioning technique; active reading is the broader principle
- [Retrieval Practice](term_retrieval_practice.md) — active recall from memory; active reading is active engagement with a text

## The Four Questions of Active Reading

Every active reader must ask these four questions of every text:

| # | Question | What It Addresses | Level Where Deepest |
|---|----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| 1 | **What is the book about as a whole?** | Central theme and structure | [Analytical](term_analytical_reading.md) Stage 1 |
| 2 | **What is being said in detail, and how?** | Ideas, claims, arguments | [Analytical](term_analytical_reading.md) Stage 2 |
| 3 | **Is it true, in whole or in part?** | Validity — only after understanding | [Analytical](term_analytical_reading.md) Stage 3 |
| 4 | **What of it?** | Significance, implications, actions | [Syntopical](term_syntopical_reading.md) |

**Critical ordering rule**: Questions 1 and 2 must be answered before attempting Question 3. Understanding precedes judgment.

## Reading for Information vs. Understanding

| Dimension | Reading for Information | Reading for Understanding |
|-----------|----------------------|--------------------------|
| **Prerequisite** | Text at or below your level | Text above your current level |
| **Outcome** | Know *that* something is true | Know *why* it's true, its connections |
| **Effort** | Low — adds facts to existing framework | High — expands your conceptual framework |
| **Growth** | None | Significant — you become a different reader |

Active reading, in Adler's sense, primarily means reading for understanding — engaging with texts that challenge and exceed your current comprehension.

## Connection to Learning Science

Active reading's effectiveness is explained by the same cognitive science principles that underpin [desirable difficulties](term_desirable_difficulties.md):

| Learning Principle | How Active Reading Implements It |
|-------------------|--------------------------------|
| [Retrieval Practice](term_retrieval_practice.md) | The four questions force active recall during reading |
| [Elaborative Interrogation](term_elaborative_interrogation.md) | "Why is this true?" parallels Rules 6-7 of analytical reading |
| [Desirable Difficulties](term_desirable_difficulties.md) | Applying the 15 rules is effortful but produces deeper encoding |
| [Interleaving](term_interleaving.md) | Syntopical reading interleaves perspectives across sources |
| [Spaced Repetition](term_spaced_repetition.md) | Great books "repay rereading" — spacing deepens understanding |
