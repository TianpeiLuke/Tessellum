# Term: Mental Model

## Definition

A **mental model** is an internal cognitive representation of external reality that the mind constructs to understand, explain, predict, and reason about the world. Mental models are not static snapshots but dynamic, simplified simulations -- the mind's working hypotheses about how systems, processes, and relationships function. They allow a person to mentally "run" scenarios, anticipate consequences, and evaluate alternatives before acting in the real world.

The concept was introduced by Scottish psychologist **Kenneth Craik** in his 1943 book *The Nature of Explanation*, where he proposed that the mind constructs "small-scale models" of reality and uses them to anticipate events, reason about possibilities, and guide action. Craik identified a three-step reasoning process: (1) **translation** of external processes into internal symbols, (2) **derivation** of new symbols through inference, and (3) **re-translation** of those symbols back into actions or predictions that can be compared against external events.

Mental models are necessarily incomplete and imperfect -- they simplify reality to make it tractable. As Donella Meadows observed, "our models fall far short of representing the world fully: that is why we make mistakes and why we are regularly surprised." The practical power of mental models lies not in their accuracy alone but in their ability to be **examined, tested, revised, and discarded** when evidence no longer supports them.

## Historical Context

| Year | Contributor | Contribution |
|------|------------|-------------|
| 1943 | Kenneth Craik | Coined "mental model" in *The Nature of Explanation*; proposed the mind builds "small-scale models" of reality |
| 1948 | Edward Tolman | Introduced "cognitive maps" -- spatial mental models in rats navigating mazes |
| 1983 | Philip Johnson-Laird | Published *Mental Models: Towards a Cognitive Science of Language, Inference, and Consciousness*; formalized mental model theory for deductive reasoning |
| 1990 | Peter Senge | Made "mental models" one of five disciplines in *The Fifth Discipline*; extended the concept to organizational learning |
| 2008 | Donella Meadows | Emphasized exposing and testing mental models in *Thinking in Systems: A Primer* |

Craik died in a cycling accident in 1945 at age 31, but his ideas profoundly influenced the development of cognitive science. Warren McCulloch reported that Einstein considered *The Nature of Explanation* a great book. The concept was later formalized by **Johnson-Laird**, who argued that having a "working model" of something *is* understanding it, and that explanation is essentially conveying "a blueprint for constructing a working model."

## Key Properties

- **Simplified representations**: Mental models reduce complex systems to manageable internal simulations; they need not be perfectly accurate to be useful
- **Dynamic and mutable**: Mental models are "ever changing and unstable" -- they update as new information is encountered, though revision is often resisted
- **Predictive function**: They enable testing alternatives mentally before acting, allowing anticipation of consequences without real-world experimentation
- **Selective focus**: Models "represent only the most relevant" aspects of a situation, filtering complexity through the lens of purpose and prior experience
- **Tacit by default**: Most mental models operate below conscious awareness; surfacing them requires deliberate effort (Senge's key insight)
- **Socially shared**: Organizations develop collective mental models that shape strategy, culture, and decision-making -- often without explicit acknowledgment
- **Falsifiable**: Effective use of mental models requires treating them as hypotheses to be tested, not truths to be defended (Meadows' emphasis)

## Related Concepts

Mental models are related to but distinct from several adjacent concepts:

| Concept | Relationship to Mental Model |
|---------|------------------------------|
| **Schema** (Bartlett, Piaget) | A schema is a categorical knowledge structure; mental models are dynamic simulations that can generate predictions |
| **Cognitive map** (Tolman) | A cognitive map is a spatial mental model; mental models are the broader category encompassing all domains |
| **Mental map** | An externalized drawing of a cognitive map; the explicit manifestation of an implicit mental model |
| **Heuristic** | A decision shortcut; mental models provide the underlying world-representation that heuristics operate upon |
| **Paradigm** (Kuhn) | A paradigm is a community-level mental model that defines what questions are legitimate and what methods are valid |

## Applications

### In Organizational Learning (Senge)

Senge identified mental models as one of five disciplines essential for building a learning organization. He defined them as "deeply ingrained assumptions, generalizations, or even pictures or images that influence how we understand the world and how we take action." The discipline of mental models involves **bringing these assumptions to the surface and scrutinizing them** -- a practice that most organizations systematically avoid. Without this discipline, new strategies and insights fail because they conflict with unexamined mental models that silently constrain what people consider possible.

### In Systems Thinking (Meadows)

Meadows advocated for exposing mental models "to the light of day," making them as rigorous as possible, testing them against evidence, and discarding them when they are no longer supported. She emphasized: "Get your model out there where it can be shot at. Invite others to challenge your assumptions and add their own." This is nothing more than the scientific method applied to everyday reasoning -- something "done too seldom even in science, and hardly at all in social science or management or government or everyday life."

### In Decision Making

Mental models determine which information a decision-maker notices (filtering), how they interpret it (sense-making), and what actions they consider (option generation). Poor decisions often result not from poor reasoning but from the wrong mental model being applied to a situation -- drawing logical conclusions from inaccurate assumptions, or illogical conclusions from accurate ones.

## Challenges and Limitations

- **Inertia**: People resist updating mental models even when confronted with disconfirming evidence, particularly when models are tied to identity or professional expertise
- **Invisibility**: The most powerful mental models are the ones we do not know we hold; surfacing them requires structured techniques (dialogue, inquiry, devil's advocacy)
- **Oversimplification**: All models simplify, but some simplify away the critical dynamics -- and the modeler may not realize what has been lost
- **Confirmation loop**: Mental models guide attention toward confirming evidence and away from disconfirming evidence, creating a self-reinforcing cycle
- **Shared blindness**: When an entire organization shares the same flawed mental model, there is no internal corrective mechanism

## Related Terms

- **[Term: Systems Thinking](term_systems_thinking.md)**: systems thinking provides the analytical framework; mental models are the cognitive substrate it aims to improve
- **[Term: Cognitive Bias](term_cognitive_bias.md)**: cognitive biases arise from and reinforce flawed mental models; biases are the systematic errors that mental models produce
- **[Term: System 1 and System 2](term_system_1_and_system_2.md)**: System 1 operates on implicit mental models; System 2 can examine and revise them
- **[Term: WYSIATI](term_wysiati.md)**: "What You See Is All There Is" describes the failure to recognize that one's mental model is incomplete
- **[Term: Confirmation Bias](term_confirmation_bias.md)**: the tendency to seek evidence confirming existing mental models while ignoring disconfirming evidence
- **[Term: Framing Effect](term_framing_effect.md)**: different frames activate different mental models, producing different decisions about identical situations
- **[Term: Planning Fallacy](term_planning_fallacy.md)**: over-optimistic project estimates result from an inside-view mental model that ignores base rates
- **[Term: Conceptual Retrofitting](term_conceptual_retrofitting.md)**: the process of revising existing mental models to incorporate new theoretical frameworks
