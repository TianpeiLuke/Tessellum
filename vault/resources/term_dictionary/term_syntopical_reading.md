# Term: Syntopical Reading

## Definition

**Syntopical reading** (also called comparative reading) is the fourth and highest level of Mortimer J. Adler's reading framework — the most demanding and most rewarding form of reading. It involves reading multiple works on the same subject, constructing a neutral terminology that transcends any individual author, framing questions that all authors address (whether explicitly or not), and synthesizing insights that no single source provides. The reader, not the author, drives the inquiry.

The key innovation is **dialectical objectivity**: examining all sides of a question without bias, even when the reader has a personal view. The syntopical reader must "refuse to take sides" and instead map the intellectual landscape — where authors agree, where they disagree, and why. The output is not a summary of each book but a **new understanding of the subject** that emerges from the structured comparison.

Adler considered syntopical reading his most original contribution. It was the methodology behind the *Syntopicon* (1952), a two-volume index to the "Great Ideas" across Western literature, which organized 102 fundamental ideas by mapping how thousands of authors addressed each one.

## Full Name

Also known as:
- **Comparative reading** (Adler's alternate term — emphasizing cross-source comparison)
- **Level 4 reading** (positional designation within the four levels)
- **Synoptic reading** (variant spelling; less common)

Related but distinct:
- Analytical Reading — Level 3; deep reading of a single text; syntopical reading builds on analytical reading skills
- **Literature review** (academic) — surveys existing work but typically categorizes rather than dialectically synthesizes
- **Meta-analysis** (statistics) — quantitative synthesis across studies; syntopical reading is the qualitative analog

## The Five Steps of Syntopical Reading

Two preparatory steps establish the reading list, then five core steps perform the synthesis:

### Preparation

| Step | Action | Purpose |
|------|--------|---------|
| P1 | **Create a tentative bibliography** | Identify works potentially relevant to your subject |
| P2 | **Inspect all candidates** | Use inspectional reading to filter; eliminate irrelevant works; refine the subject |

### Core Steps

| Step | Action | Description |
|------|--------|-------------|
| 1 | **Find relevant passages** | You are the master, not the disciple. Focus on finding material useful to *your* question, not on understanding each book fully. |
| 2 | **Bring authors to terms** | Construct a *neutral terminology* — a common vocabulary that all authors can be interpreted as using, whether they use those words or not. This is the hardest step. |
| 3 | **Get questions clear** | Frame neutral questions that all authors can be seen as addressing, even if they don't ask those questions explicitly. |
| 4 | **Define issues** | Arrange opposing answers from different authors. Create pro-and-con divisions. Identify where disagreements are real vs. merely verbal. |
| 5 | **Analyze the discussion** | Order questions from general to specific. Show relationships between issues. The goal is dialectical objectivity. |

### The Syntopical Paradox

You need to know which books to read to read syntopically, but you can't know which to read without reading syntopically. **Solution**: start with a tentative bibliography, use inspectional reading to refine it, then iterate. The bibliography is a living document.
