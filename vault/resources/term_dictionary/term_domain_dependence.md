# Domain Dependence

## Definition

**Domain dependence** is the inability to transfer knowledge, insight, or reasoning from one domain (where it was originally learned) to another domain where it is equally applicable. The term was introduced by **Nassim Nicholas Taleb** in *Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder* (2012) to describe how people who clearly understand a concept in one context systematically fail to recognize the identical structure in a different context.

The mechanism is rooted in the brain's **modular architecture**: we process problems using context-specific mental modules, and these modules are "encapsulated" -- we activate different cognitive routines depending on the framing of a problem, not its underlying logical structure. A doctor who fluently applies Bayesian reasoning in diagnostic medicine may completely fail to apply the same reasoning to her personal investment decisions, not because she lacks the knowledge, but because the investment context does not trigger the medical-statistics module where that knowledge resides.

## Key Properties

- **Context-dependent activation**: Knowledge is not stored as abstract, universally accessible principles but as context-bound routines. The same person can "know" something in one setting and act as if ignorant in another.
- **Expertise paradox**: Domain dependence worsens with increasing expertise. The deeper one's knowledge in a specific domain, the more tightly that knowledge is bound to the domain's particular framing, vocabulary, and conventions, making cross-domain recognition harder, not easier.
- **The classroom problem**: Formal education often produces domain-dependent knowledge. Students learn to solve problems as presented in textbooks but fail to recognize the same problems when encountered in real-world settings with different surface features. They learn to get grades, not to think.
- **Modularity and encapsulation**: Following research by Tversky and Kahneman, Taleb notes that even mathematical psychologists -- textbook authors on statistical inference -- committed egregious inferential errors outside the classroom context, putting too much confidence in small samples despite possessing the knowledge to know better.
- **Framing sensitivity**: The same logical problem produces different answers depending on how it is framed. A question about market direction in casual conversation is treated differently from the same question presented as a formal statistical exercise.
- **Asymmetric transfer**: Some domains export knowledge more easily than others. Abstract, formal domains (mathematics, logic) should in principle transfer widely, but in practice the transfer rate is remarkably low without deliberate effort.

## Related Terms
- **[Ludic Fallacy](term_ludic_fallacy.md)**: A sibling concept from Taleb; the ludic fallacy applies game-like models to unstructured reality, while domain dependence prevents recognizing that the same principle applies across contexts
- **[Narrative Fallacy](term_narrative_fallacy.md)**: Domain dependence and the narrative fallacy both involve failures of abstraction -- the narrative fallacy binds understanding to a particular story, domain dependence binds it to a particular context
- **[Cognitive Bias](term_cognitive_bias.md)**: Domain dependence belongs to the broader family of systematic errors in human judgment, particularly those involving failures of transfer and generalization
- **[Antifragility](term_antifragility.md)**: Taleb's constructive framework; domain dependence blinds us to the universal applicability of antifragile principles across systems
- **[System 1 and System 2](term_system_1_and_system_2.md)**: Domain-dependent reasoning often reflects System 1's reliance on contextual cues rather than System 2's capacity for abstract rule application
- **[Framing Effect](term_framing_effect.md)**: The framing effect is one mechanism through which domain dependence operates -- identical information presented in different frames triggers different cognitive modules
- **[Confirmation Bias](term_confirmation_bias.md)**: Within a domain, confirmation bias reinforces domain-specific heuristics; across domains, it prevents recognizing that a familiar principle applies in an unfamiliar setting
