# Term: Tu Quoque

## Definition

**Tu quoque** ("you too" or "you also") is a logical fallacy that deflects criticism by pointing out that the critic engages in the same behavior being criticized. "You can't criticize our testing practices — your team ships bugs too" shifts the focus from the validity of the criticism to the critic's own behavior. The fallacy is a specific type of **ad hominem** and **red herring**: it attacks the critic's standing to make the argument rather than addressing the argument itself, and it changes the subject from the original claim to the critic's conduct.

Tu quoque exploits the psychological power of **perceived hypocrisy** — we intuitively feel that hypocrites have no right to criticize, even though the truth of a criticism is logically independent of whether the critic practices what they preach. A doctor who smokes is still correct that smoking causes cancer. The fallacy conflates **moral authority** (the right to criticize) with **epistemic validity** (the truth of the criticism).

The fallacy is extremely common in organizational settings because most teams and processes have known imperfections, making "you do it too" an always-available deflection. In its modern geopolitical form — **whataboutism** — it was a signature rhetorical strategy of Soviet propaganda and remains pervasive in political discourse.

## Full Name

**Tu Quoque** (also known as: You Too Fallacy, Appeal to Hypocrisy, Whataboutism, Two Wrongs Make a Right, The Pot Calling the Kettle Black, Hypocrisy Defense)

## Logical Structure

| Step | Content |
|------|---------|
| Premise | Person A criticizes B for doing X |
| Response | Person B responds: "But you do X too" |
| Implied conclusion | Therefore A's criticism is invalid |
| **Why invalid** | A's hypocrisy does not make the criticism false; the truth of the claim is independent of the claimant's behavior |

## Examples

| Context | Fallacious Argument | Why It Is Fallacious |
|---------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Meeting** | "You say we need better documentation, but your team's wiki is outdated too" | The critic's documentation practices do not affect whether the team needs better documentation |
| **Data analysis** | "You're questioning our methodology? Your last report had statistical errors too" | Past errors by the critic do not invalidate current methodological concerns |
| **Policy debate** | "You can't advocate for stricter enforcement — your department had compliance issues last year" | The department's past compliance issues do not make the policy argument wrong |
| **Code review** | "You're flagging my code style? I've seen worse in your PRs" | The reviewer's own code quality does not change whether the flagged issue is real |

## Detection and Countermeasures

| Strategy | How It Works |
|----------|-------------|
| **Acknowledge and refocus** | "That may be true about us, but does it change the validity of the point?" — separates messenger from message |
| **Track whether the original criticism was addressed** | Tu quoque succeeds when the audience forgets the original point; explicitly return to it |
| **Separate moral authority from epistemic validity** | A hypocrite can still be right; evaluate the claim on its own merits |
| **Name the deflection** | "That sounds like a 'you too' response — can we address the original concern first?" — making the pattern explicit reduces its effectiveness |

## Relationship to Cognitive Biases

| Bias | How It Enables Tu Quoque |
|------|-------------------------|
| **Fairness heuristic** | Hypocrisy feels unfair, so the brain treats the criticism as invalid — conflating fairness with truth |
| **Attention redirection** | Focus shifts from the argument to the critic's behavior; the original claim is lost |
| **Emotional satisfaction** | Tu quoque equalizes perceived status — pointing out the critic's flaws feels like a valid defense because it restores psychological balance |
| **Fundamental attribution error** | We attribute the critic's behavior to character ("they're a hypocrite") rather than circumstances, making the deflection feel more damning |
