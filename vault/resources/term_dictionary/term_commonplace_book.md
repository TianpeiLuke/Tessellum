# Term: Commonplace Book

## Definition

A **commonplace book** (Latin: *loci communes*; also: **commonplace**, **commonplacing**) is a historical knowledge management practice in which a thinker maintains a personal notebook for recording **quotes, observations, ideas, reading excerpts, arguments, and half-formed thoughts** drawn from diverse sources, organized by **theme or topic** rather than chronologically. The practice dates to classical antiquity (Aristotle's *Topica* discussed *topoi* or "commonplaces" as categories for organizing arguments) and was systematized during the Renaissance by **Erasmus** in *De Copia* (1512), which taught students to maintain organized notebooks of noteworthy passages, and by **John Locke** in "A New Method of a Common-Place-Book" (1706), which introduced an alphabetical indexing system for retrieval.

The commonplace book served as the primary technology for personal knowledge management from the Renaissance through the nineteenth century. Users periodically re-read their collections and recombined entries, enabling **unexpected connections between ideas recorded at different times and from different sources**. The practice was central to the intellectual workflows of many of the most productive thinkers in Western history, including Francis Bacon, John Milton, Isaac Newton, John Locke, Erasmus, Ralph Waldo Emerson, Virginia Woolf, and Charles Darwin -- whose notebooks played a critical role in the development of the theory of natural selection.

Steven Johnson positions the commonplace book as the **original technology for cultivating slow hunches, enabling serendipity, and maintaining a personal liquid network of ideas** in *Where Good Ideas Come From* (2010). By recording ideas from diverse readings in a single place organized by theme, the commonplace book creates conditions for the collision of previously unrelated concepts -- precisely the mechanism Johnson identifies as the engine of innovation. The practice is a direct historical precursor to the **Zettelkasten** method developed by Niklas Luhmann in the twentieth century, which refined the commonplace book's principles by introducing strict atomicity (one idea per note) and explicit bidirectional linking.

## Full Name

**Commonplace Book** (also: commonplace, commonplacing, loci communes, silva rerum)

Historical variants:
- **Loci communes** (Latin) -- "common places" or "common topics"; the original term from classical rhetoric
- **Silva rerum** (Latin) -- "forest of things"; a Polish-Lithuanian variant
- **Zibaldone** (Italian) -- a miscellaneous notebook tradition; Leopardi's *Zibaldone* (1817-1832) is the most famous example
- **Florilegia** (Latin) -- "gathering of flowers"; medieval compilations of excerpts, a precursor to the Renaissance commonplace book

## Core Concepts

### The Practice: Recording, Indexing, Re-reading, Recombining

The commonplace book workflow consists of four essential activities:

| Activity | Description | Purpose |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| **Recording** | While reading, conversing, or thinking, the practitioner copies noteworthy passages, observations, and ideas into the commonplace book | Captures raw material from diverse sources |
| **Indexing** | Entries are organized under thematic headings (Erasmus) or indexed alphabetically (Locke) for retrieval | Enables finding entries by topic rather than by date or source |
| **Re-reading** | The practitioner periodically browses the commonplace book, revisiting old entries | Resurfaces forgotten ideas; triggers recognition of new connections |
| **Recombining** | Ideas from different entries, recorded at different times from different sources, are juxtaposed to generate new insights | Produces emergent understanding that neither original source contained alone |

### Distinction from Diary or Journal

The commonplace book is frequently confused with a diary or journal, but the two practices differ fundamentally:

| Feature | Commonplace Book | Diary/Journal |
|---------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Organization** | Thematic (by topic or category) | Chronological (by date) |
| **Content source** | Primarily external (readings, conversations, observed facts) | Primarily internal (personal experiences, feelings, reflections) |
| **Purpose** | Knowledge accumulation and recombination | Personal record and self-reflection |
| **Re-reading pattern** | Browsed by topic; old entries regularly revisited | Read sequentially; older entries rarely revisited |
| **Emphasis** | Ideas and arguments | Events and emotions |

This distinction is critical: the commonplace book's power comes from its thematic organization, which places ideas from different sources and time periods next to each other, enabling the cross-pollination that chronological organization prevents.

### Historical Origins and Systematization

#### Classical Roots
- **Aristotle's *Topica*** (c. 350 BCE): Discussed *topoi* (commonplaces) as general categories of argument that could be applied across different subjects -- the rhetorical ancestor of the commonplace book's thematic organization.
- **Roman rhetoric**: Cicero and Quintilian taught students to maintain collections of exemplary passages organized by rhetorical category.

#### Renaissance Systematization
- **Erasmus, *De Copia* (1512)**: The most influential Renaissance treatise on the practice. Erasmus instructed students to maintain notebooks with thematic headings and to copy noteworthy passages under the appropriate heading. He emphasized that the value lay not in the individual passages but in their accumulation and juxtaposition.
- **Philip Melanchthon**: Luther's collaborator who developed commonplacing into a systematic pedagogical method for Protestant education.

#### Locke's Indexing Innovation
- **John Locke, "A New Method of a Common-Place-Book" (1706)**: Published in French in 1686 and in English in 1706, Locke's method introduced a systematic alphabetical indexing system. Each entry was indexed by its first letter and the first vowel in its keyword, creating a compact index that allowed rapid retrieval across a large notebook. This was a significant advance over Erasmus's approach, which relied on pre-established thematic headings and could not easily accommodate new categories.

### Key Practitioners

| Practitioner | Period | Notable Use |
|-------------|--------|------------|
| **Erasmus** | 1466-1536 | Systematized the practice in *De Copia*; modeled commonplacing as the foundation of humanist education |
| **Francis Bacon** | 1561-1626 | Maintained commonplace books as part of his empirical method; his *Promus of Formularies and Elegancies* is a surviving example |
| **John Milton** | 1608-1674 | His *Commonplace Book* (published posthumously) recorded extracts organized under political, ethical, and theological headings |
| **Isaac Newton** | 1643-1727 | Maintained extensive notebooks blending reading notes, experimental observations, and theoretical speculations |
| **John Locke** | 1632-1704 | Invented a systematic indexing method; his own commonplace books survive in the Bodleian Library |
| **Charles Darwin** | 1809-1882 | His "Red Notebook," "Transmutation Notebooks" (B-E), and other notebooks functioned as commonplace books in which the theory of natural selection gradually crystallized |
| **Ralph Waldo Emerson** | 1803-1882 | Maintained extensive "journals and miscellaneous notebooks" (published in 16 volumes) from which he drew material for his essays |
| **Virginia Woolf** | 1882-1941 | Maintained reading notebooks organized by theme alongside her diary |

### Evolution into Modern Forms

The commonplace book's principles -- recording, indexing, re-reading, recombining -- have been carried forward and refined in modern knowledge management systems:

| Historical Practice | Modern Descendant | Key Innovation |
|-------------------|-------------------|----------------|
| **Commonplace book** (Renaissance) | **Zettelkasten** (20th century) | Strict atomicity (one idea per note) and explicit bidirectional linking between notes |
| **Locke's indexing** | **Digital tagging and search** | Computational indexing eliminates the need for manual index maintenance |
| **Thematic organization** | **Wiki link structure** | Hyperlinks enable non-linear, thematic navigation across a large knowledge base |
| **Periodic re-reading** | **Spaced repetition / graph traversal** | Algorithmic resurfacing replaces manual browsing |
| **Physical notebook** | **Digital note apps** (Obsidian, Roam, Notion) | Digital tools enable search, linking, and visualization at scale |

### Johnson's Argument: Commonplace Books as Innovation Infrastructure

Johnson argues in *Where Good Ideas Come From* that the commonplace book is not merely a study aid but a foundational technology for innovation. His key claims:

1. **Slow-hunch preservation**: The commonplace book keeps partial ideas alive across months, years, or decades, allowing them to find their complementary pieces
2. **Serendipity engine**: Thematic re-reading creates unexpected collisions between ideas from different sources and time periods
3. **Personal liquid network**: The commonplace book creates a dense, reconfigurable network of ideas within a single mind, analogous to the liquid networks that produce innovation at the social level
4. **Adjacent possible expansion**: Each new entry expands the set of possible recombinations, mirroring how each new technology expands the adjacent possible

### Supporting Evidence
- **Intellectual history research**: Scholars including Ann Blair (*Too Much to Know*, 2010), Richard Yeo (*Notebooks, English Virtuosi, and Early Modern Science*, 2014), and Earle Havens (*Commonplace Books: A History of Manuscripts and Printed Books from Antiquity to the Twentieth Century*, 2001) have documented the pervasiveness and intellectual importance of commonplace books across several centuries.
- **Darwin's notebooks**: Howard Gruber's *Darwin on Man* (1974) and subsequent scholarship have demonstrated through meticulous archival analysis that Darwin's notebooks functioned as commonplace books in which the theory of natural selection developed gradually -- the paradigmatic case of a slow hunch cultivated through commonplacing.

## Practical Applications

### In Personal Knowledge Management
- The commonplace book tradition provides the historical foundation and intellectual justification for modern note-taking systems like Zettelkasten, Obsidian, and Roam. Practitioners maintaining a slip-box or digital knowledge base are continuing a practice with a 500-year pedigree.

### In Education
- Commonplacing was the standard pedagogy for developing analytical and rhetorical skills from the Renaissance through the nineteenth century. Modern educational reformers who advocate for reflective journals, reading logs, and synthesis notebooks are reviving the practice in different form.

### In Research and Writing
- The commonplace book's workflow -- record, index, re-read, recombine -- maps directly onto the research-to-writing pipeline: gather sources, organize by theme, identify patterns and gaps, synthesize into original arguments.

### In Knowledge Management at Scale
- Organizational wikis, internal knowledge bases, and shared note systems (Confluence, Notion, internal wikis) are institutional commonplace books -- shared repositories of knowledge organized by topic that enable collective re-reading and recombination.

### In Abuse Prevention
- The Abuse slipbox project is a direct descendant of the commonplace book tradition, applying its principles (recording, thematic organization, linking, recombination) to the domain of the domain. Customer contacts, risk evaluations, and policy documentation are "commonplaced" into a unified, interconnected knowledge graph.

## Criticisms and Limitations

- **Out-of-context quotation**: Commonplace books extract passages from their original context, which can distort meaning. A quote removed from its surrounding argument may be misinterpreted or misapplied -- a concern raised by critics from the Renaissance onward.
- **Passive accumulation risk**: Without the re-reading and recombination steps, a commonplace book degenerates into a "collector's fallacy" -- the illusion of knowledge through accumulation without processing. Soenke Ahrens makes this distinction central to *How to Take Smart Notes*.
- **Indexing overhead**: Before digital search, maintaining a useful commonplace book required significant manual indexing effort. Locke's method reduced but did not eliminate this burden.
- **Lack of atomicity**: Unlike the Zettelkasten, traditional commonplace books do not enforce one-idea-per-entry. Entries can be long, mixing multiple ideas, which makes retrieval and recombination less precise.
- **Individual-scale limitation**: Commonplace books are inherently personal; they do not scale naturally to teams or organizations without additional infrastructure (which wikis and knowledge graphs provide).
- **Selection bias in historical examples**: The practitioners cited (Darwin, Newton, Locke) are among history's most exceptional thinkers. The commonplace book may have been a necessary but not sufficient condition for their achievements, and millions of less exceptional commonplace books have been forgotten.
