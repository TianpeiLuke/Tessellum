# Confirmation Bias

## Definition

Confirmation bias is the tendency to search for, interpret, favor, and recall information in a way that confirms or supports one's prior beliefs or values, while giving disproportionately less consideration to alternative possibilities. It is one of the most extensively studied and pervasive cognitive biases in the psychological literature, affecting scientific reasoning, medical diagnosis, legal proceedings, political beliefs, and everyday judgment.

The bias operates through multiple mechanisms simultaneously. *Selective search* means we preferentially seek evidence that supports what we already believe rather than evidence that could disprove it. *Biased interpretation* means we interpret ambiguous evidence as confirming our prior view. *Selective recall* means we more easily remember instances that align with our beliefs. Together, these mechanisms create a self-reinforcing cycle: our beliefs shape what evidence we encounter, how we interpret it, and what we remember, which in turn strengthens the original beliefs.

Confirmation bias is especially dangerous because it affects experts as much as laypeople, operates largely unconsciously, and is resistant to most debiasing techniques. In the age of algorithmic content curation, social media echo chambers amplify the bias by presenting users with information that matches their existing preferences, creating epistemic bubbles where disconfirming evidence is systematically filtered out.

## Historical Context

The concept has ancient roots --- Francis Bacon wrote in 1620 that "the human understanding when it has once adopted an opinion draws all things else to support and agree with it" --- but it was first experimentally demonstrated by Peter Wason in 1960 through his "2-4-6 task." Participants were told that the sequence 2-4-6 fit a rule and asked to discover the rule by proposing test sequences. Most participants hypothesized "increasing by two" and tested only confirming sequences (4-6-8, 10-12-14) rather than disconfirming ones. The actual rule was simply "any ascending sequence," but participants rarely discovered it because they never tested sequences that would *disprove* their hypothesis.

Wason's work launched decades of research. Raymond Nickerson's comprehensive 1998 review, "Confirmation Bias: A Ubiquitous Phenomenon in Many Guises," documented the bias across dozens of domains. Kahneman and Tversky's broader heuristics-and-biases program provided the cognitive framework, linking confirmation bias to System 1's tendency toward coherence-seeking and Kahneman's concept of WYSIATI ("What You See Is All There Is").

More recently, Hugo Mercier and Dan Sperber's "argumentative theory of reasoning" (2011) proposed that confirmation bias is not a flaw but a feature: human reasoning evolved not to find truth but to win arguments, making one-sided evidence gathering adaptive in social contexts. This reframing does not reduce the bias's impact on individual judgment but suggests it may be mitigated by adversarial debate structures where each side is incentivized to find disconfirming evidence for the other's claims.

## Key Properties

- **Operates through three distinct channels**: selective search (seeking confirming evidence), biased interpretation (reading ambiguous data as confirming), and selective recall (better memory for confirming instances)
- **Largely unconscious**: people are typically unaware they are doing it, making it exceptionally difficult to counteract through willpower alone
- **Affects experts as well as novices**: physicians, judges, scientists, and intelligence analysts all demonstrate confirmation bias in their professional domains
- **Resistant to standard debiasing**: simply warning people about confirmation bias has limited effect; structured countermeasures are needed
- **Amplified by emotional investment**: the more personally important a belief, the stronger the confirmation bias protecting it (motivated reasoning)
- **Drives belief polarization**: when two groups with opposing views examine the same mixed evidence, both become more confident in their original positions
- **Undermines scientific method**: the entire structure of hypothesis testing (falsification) exists as an institutional counterweight to this natural human tendency
- **Compounded by social media algorithms**: recommendation systems serve content matching user preferences, creating filter bubbles that reduce exposure to disconfirming evidence
- **Self-sealing**: the bias makes it difficult to recognize the bias itself, since evidence of one's own bias is disconfirming evidence that the bias filters out
- **Primary antidote is active falsification**: deliberately asking "what evidence would change my mind?" and then seeking that specific evidence

## Applications

| Domain | How It Manifests | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Medical Diagnosis | Anchoring on initial diagnosis and selectively attending to confirming symptoms | A physician who suspects pneumonia may underweight symptoms suggesting heart failure |
| Criminal Investigation | Police focus on evidence supporting their prime suspect while neglecting exculpatory leads | Tunnel vision in wrongful conviction cases documented by the Innocence Project |
| Scientific Research | Researchers design experiments and interpret results in ways that favor their hypothesis | Publication bias toward positive results; p-hacking to reach significance |
| Political Beliefs | Consuming news from ideologically aligned sources and dismissing contrary reporting | Partisan media consumption patterns, echo chambers on social media platforms |
| Investing | Seeking analyst reports that support an existing position while ignoring bearish signals | Holding a stock and reading only bullish analysis, dismissing warning signs |
| Hiring | Interviewers who form early impressions selectively attend to confirming information | Structured interviews outperform unstructured ones because they reduce confirmation bias |

## Related Terms

- **[Cognitive Bias](term_cognitive_bias.md)**: parent concept; confirmation bias is arguably the most fundamental and pervasive cognitive bias
- **[WYSIATI](term_wysiati.md)**: Kahneman's principle that we draw conclusions from available information without considering what is missing, which confirmation bias exploits
- **[Narrative Fallacy](term_narrative_fallacy.md)**: confirmation bias feeds the construction of coherent but misleading stories from selectively gathered evidence
- **[Availability Heuristic](term_availability_heuristic.md)**: confirming instances are more available to memory, reinforcing the bias
- **[Critical Thinking](term_critical_thinking.md)**: the discipline of actively seeking disconfirming evidence as the primary counterweight to confirmation bias
- **[Logical Fallacies](term_logical_fallacies.md)**: confirmation bias underlies several formal and informal fallacies, including cherry-picking and hasty generalization
- **[Survivorship Bias](term_survivorship_bias.md)**: a specific form where disconfirming evidence (failures) is invisible, feeding confirmation of success narratives
- **[Hindsight Bias](term_hindsight_bias.md)**: post-hoc, selectively remembering evidence that confirmed the known outcome
- **[Overconfidence Effect](term_overconfidence_effect.md)**: selectively processed confirming evidence inflates subjective confidence in one's judgments
- **[Anchoring Effect](term_anchoring_effect.md)**: initial information serves as an anchor that confirmation bias then protects from revision
