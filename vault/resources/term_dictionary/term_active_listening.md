# Active Listening

## Definition

**Active listening** is a communication technique in which the listener fully concentrates on, comprehends, and responds to a speaker — going beyond passive hearing to demonstrate engagement and ensure accurate understanding. Developed by psychologists **Carl Rogers** and **Richard Farson** in the 1950s as part of Rogers' person-centered therapy, active listening requires the listener to attend to the speaker's words, tone, and body language; reflect back what was heard through paraphrasing or summarizing; ask clarifying questions; and withhold judgment until the speaker has finished.

The technique serves two simultaneous purposes: it ensures the listener **accurately understands** the speaker's message (reducing miscommunication), and it makes the speaker **feel heard and respected** (building trust and rapport). In argumentation contexts, active listening is a powerful persuasion tool: by genuinely understanding the other person's position before responding, the listener gains both the information needed to construct effective counterarguments and the relational goodwill that makes the other person receptive to influence.

## Key Properties

- **Full attention**: The listener gives undivided attention — no multitasking, no formulating rebuttals while the other person speaks
- **Reflective paraphrasing**: The listener restates the speaker's message in their own words ("So what you're saying is...") to confirm accurate understanding
- **Clarifying questions**: The listener asks open-ended questions to deepen understanding rather than to challenge or cross-examine
- **Nonverbal engagement**: Eye contact, nodding, and open body posture signal attention and respect
- **Withholding judgment**: The listener defers evaluation until they have fully understood the speaker's complete position
- **Emotional validation**: Acknowledging the speaker's feelings ("I can see why that would be frustrating") without necessarily agreeing with their conclusion
- **Dual benefit**: Simultaneously improves comprehension accuracy and builds interpersonal trust

## Applications

| Context | Application |
|---------|-------------|
| **Argumentation** | Understanding the opponent's position fully before responding; de-escalation through demonstrated respect |
| **Management** | Building trust with reports; eliciting honest feedback; Fadem's questioning framework depends on listening to answers |
| **Research interviews** | Accurately capturing subjects' perspectives without interviewer bias |
| **Code reviews** | Understanding the author's design intent before critiquing implementation choices |
| **Conflict resolution** | Reducing defensiveness by making each party feel heard before seeking compromise |

- **[Logical Fallacies](term_logical_fallacies.md)**: Active listening helps identify fallacies in others' reasoning by ensuring accurate comprehension before analysis
- **[Cognitive Bias](term_cognitive_bias.md)**: Active listening counteracts confirmation bias by forcing attention to the speaker's actual words rather than the listener's preconceptions
- **[Straw Man](term_straw_man.md)**: Active listening prevents straw-manning by ensuring the listener represents the speaker's argument accurately
- **[Argumentation](term_argumentation.md)**: Active listening is a prerequisite for effective argumentation — understanding the opponent's actual position before constructing a response
- **[Critical Thinking](term_critical_thinking.md)**: Active listening supports the interpretation and analysis skills of critical thinking — accurate comprehension before evaluation
