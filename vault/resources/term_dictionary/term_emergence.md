# Emergence

## Definition

**Emergence** refers to the existence or formation of collective properties, behaviors, or structures that arise from the interactions among a system's components but that are not present in, nor predictable from, those components in isolation. An emergent property belongs to the whole system rather than to any individual part.

The concept is central to complexity science, philosophy of mind, and systems thinking. It is often summarized as "the whole is more than the sum of its parts" -- a principle traceable to Aristotle's *Metaphysics*. Emergence stands in direct contrast to **reductionism**, which holds that all higher-level phenomena can be fully explained by the properties and laws governing their constituent parts.

## Types: Weak vs. Strong Emergence

| Type | Can be simulated? | Relation to physics | Key advocate | Examples |
|------|-------------------|---------------------|-------------|----------|
| **Weak emergence** | Yes (in principle) | Compatible with physicalism; reducible given complete micro-knowledge | Mark Bedau (1997) | Traffic jams, flocking, Game of Life gliders, weather patterns |
| **Strong emergence** | No (in principle) | Irreducible; implies novel causal powers and possibly new fundamental laws | David Chalmers (2006) | Consciousness (debated), qualia |

**Weak emergence**: The emergent property can, in principle, be derived from micro-level rules through simulation or exhaustive computation, but it is practically unpredictable due to nonlinear interactions and combinatorial complexity. Most scientific examples of emergence are weak.

**Strong emergence**: The emergent property is ontologically irreducible -- it cannot be deduced even in principle from complete knowledge of micro-level components and laws. If strong emergence exists, it requires new fundamental laws beyond physics. This remains philosophically controversial.

## Key Properties

- **Novelty**: Emergent properties are qualitatively distinct from the properties of individual components
- **Irreducibility** (strong) or **computational irreducibility** (weak): Cannot be derived by simple aggregation
- **Downward causation**: Higher-level emergent structures can constrain or influence lower-level component behavior
- **Supervenience**: Emergent properties depend on and covary with the micro-level configuration
- **Non-additivity**: The system's behavior is not a linear sum of component behaviors
- **Context-dependence**: Emergence often requires specific environmental or relational conditions (functional emergence)

## Historical Context

The modern concept originated with the **British Emergentists**: John Stuart Mill (*A System of Logic*, 1843) distinguished "homopathic" from "heteropathic" effects; C. Lloyd Morgan (*Emergent Evolution*, 1923) coined the term "emergent"; C.D. Broad (*The Mind and Its Place in Nature*, 1925) gave the first systematic philosophical treatment. The tradition was largely eclipsed by reductionist successes in mid-20th-century physics and chemistry, but revived in the 1970s through Philip Anderson's "More Is Different" (1972) and the rise of complexity science at the Santa Fe Institute in the 1980s-90s.
