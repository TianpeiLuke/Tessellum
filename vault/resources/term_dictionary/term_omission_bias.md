# Omission Bias

## Definition

**Omission bias** is the systematic tendency to judge harmful actions (commissions) as morally worse than equally harmful inactions (omissions), even when the outcomes are identical or the omission produces greater harm. A person who actively poisons a rival is judged more harshly than one who merely fails to warn the rival about a poisoned dish — despite both leading to the same result. The asymmetry is not about outcomes; it is about the perceived causal and moral weight of doing versus not doing.

The bias reflects a deep intuition that acting to cause harm requires intent, whereas failing to prevent harm can be attributed to ignorance, oversight, or competing priorities. This intuition is often wrong — deliberate omissions can be just as intentional as commissions — but the moral framing persists. The result is that harmful inaction is systematically undertreated, underregulated, and underpunished relative to harmful action.

Omission bias has profound practical consequences. Parents who refuse vaccines for their children are committing a harmful omission (exposing the child to disease risk) but perceive themselves as avoiding a harmful action (the potential side effects of vaccination). The asymmetry is not rational — the expected harm from omission typically exceeds the expected harm from commission — but the psychological framing makes inaction feel safer because it does not involve "doing something" that could be blamed.

## Historical Context

The formal experimental study of omission bias was initiated by Mark Spranca, Elisa Minsk, and Jonathan Baron in their 1991 paper "Omission and Commission in Judgment and Choice," published in the *Journal of Experimental Social Psychology*. Their experiments presented participants with scenarios in which a protagonist could either actively cause harm or passively allow harm to occur. Subjects consistently rated the active harm as more immoral, even when outcomes were held constant. In one paradigm, actively stealing $100 was judged as worse than failing to point out a $100 billing error in one's favor — despite identical financial consequences.

Ilana Ritov and Jonathan Baron (1992) extended this work, connecting omission bias to status quo bias and demonstrating that the effect persisted in consequential decision contexts such as vaccination decisions. Their research showed that people preferred the risk of harm from inaction (not vaccinating) over an equivalent or smaller risk of harm from action (vaccinating), because the action-caused harm felt more blameworthy. This line of research has since been applied to medical ethics, legal liability, environmental policy, and regulatory design.

## Key Properties

- **Moral asymmetry**: Actions that cause harm are judged as more immoral than omissions that cause equivalent or greater harm — the moral weight of "doing" exceeds the moral weight of "not doing"
- **Complementary to action bias**: Action bias drives people to act even when inaction is optimal; omission bias makes people judge those who act and fail more harshly — together they create a paradox where we feel compelled to act but are punished more for acting badly
- **Vaccination decisions**: Parents judge vaccine side effects (commission harm) as worse than disease risk from non-vaccination (omission harm), even when the expected harm from non-vaccination is far greater
- **Legal asymmetry**: Most legal systems punish commissions more severely than omissions — murder carries heavier sentences than failure to rescue, even when the failure is deliberate
- **Causal perception**: Actions are perceived as more directly causal than omissions, even when both are necessary conditions for the outcome — "he did it" feels more explanatory than "he let it happen"
- **Intent attribution**: Actions are assumed to involve greater intent and deliberation than omissions, even when omissions are fully deliberate
- **Emotional asymmetry**: Commissions generate stronger negative emotions (guilt, anger, disgust) than omissions, amplifying the perceived moral difference
- **Exacerbated by identifiability**: When the victim is identifiable and the harm is vivid, commission is judged even more harshly relative to omission
- **Countered by framing**: Reframing an omission as an action ("choosing not to act" rather than "not acting") reduces the asymmetry by making the decision quality of the omission explicit
- **Cultural variation**: Some cultures show weaker omission bias, particularly those with stronger collectivist norms where duty to act is more heavily emphasized
- **Interacts with diffusion of responsibility**: In group settings, omission is further enabled because no single individual feels personally responsible for the inaction
- **Stronger for unfamiliar domains**: When people lack expertise, they are more likely to prefer inaction because action in an unfamiliar domain feels riskier than doing nothing

## Applications

| Domain | Application | Mechanism |
|--------|-------------|-----------|
| **Public Health** | Vaccine hesitancy persists partly because parents judge side effects from vaccination (commission) as worse than disease from non-vaccination (omission) | Omission bias makes inaction feel safer even when it carries greater expected harm |
| **Medical Ethics** | Withdrawing treatment is judged as morally worse than withholding treatment, even when the patient outcome is identical | The act of stopping (commission) feels more causal than the act of never starting (omission) |
| **Legal Systems** | Criminal law punishes acts of commission more severely than acts of omission; Good Samaritan laws are rare and weak | Legislative frameworks mirror the cognitive bias, treating inaction as less blameworthy |
| **Environmental Policy** | Regulators are more willing to ban pollutants (preventing commission) than to mandate cleanup (requiring action against omission) | The political cost of action-caused harm exceeds the political cost of inaction-caused harm |
| **Fraud Prevention** | Allowing a fraudulent claim to process (omission) feels less culpable than actively approving it (commission), even when the omission is deliberate | Agents may avoid flagging suspicious activity because "not acting" feels less risky than "acting wrongly" |

## Related Terms

- **[Cognitive Bias](term_cognitive_bias.md)**: parent concept — omission bias is a fundamental asymmetry in moral and causal judgment
- **[Action Bias](term_action_bias.md)**: motivational complement — action bias drives people to act; omission bias punishes them more when action goes wrong
- **[Status Quo Bias](term_status_quo_bias.md)**: reinforcing bias — preference for the current state favors omission over commission
- **[Loss Aversion](term_loss_aversion.md)**: underlying mechanism — commission-caused losses feel more painful than omission-caused losses
- **[Moral Licensing](term_moral_licensing.md)**: related moral judgment pattern — past good behavior licenses subsequent omissions
- **[Trolley Problem](term_trolley_problem.md)**: philosophical framework — the distinction between actively diverting harm versus passively allowing harm
- **[Sunk Cost Fallacy](term_sunk_cost_fallacy.md)**: compounding effect — once a course of inaction has been maintained, switching to action feels like abandoning the prior (non-)commitment
- **[Regret Aversion](term_regret_aversion.md)**: driving mechanism — anticipated regret from action exceeds anticipated regret from inaction
