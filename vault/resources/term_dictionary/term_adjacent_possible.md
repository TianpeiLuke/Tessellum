# Term: Adjacent Possible

## Definition

**The adjacent possible** is a concept describing the set of all first-order possibilities available at any given moment -- the configurations, innovations, and ideas that are exactly one step away from current reality. Originated by theoretical biologist **Stuart Kauffman** in his research on the origins of life and the self-organization of complex systems, the adjacent possible was popularized and extended to innovation history by **Steven Johnson** in *Where Good Ideas Come From: The Natural History of Innovation* (2010).

In Kauffman's original formulation from chemical biology, the adjacent possible describes the set of novel molecules that can be formed by recombining the molecules that already exist in a system. Starting from a small set of simple molecules, single-step reactions can produce a modestly larger set of new molecules; those new molecules in turn expand the set of possible single-step reactions, and so on. The adjacent possible thus grows as it is explored -- each new molecule opens doors to further molecules that were previously inaccessible. This creates an expanding frontier of possibility that is both constrained (only certain next steps are reachable) and generative (each step expands the frontier).

Johnson's key extension is to apply this framework to the history of human innovation. Ideas, technologies, and cultural breakthroughs are constrained by the adjacent possible of their era: they require precursor technologies, knowledge, infrastructure, and materials to already be in place. Innovations that arrive before the adjacent possible can support them -- what Johnson calls ideas "ahead of their time" -- reliably fail, no matter how brilliant. Conversely, when the adjacent possible is ready, multiple independent inventors frequently discover the same innovation simultaneously (the phenomenon of "multiples"), because the adjacent possible makes the discovery accessible to anyone exploring its boundaries.

## Full Name

**The Adjacent Possible** (sometimes: "the space of the adjacent possible")

Origin and key contributors:
- **Stuart Kauffman** -- theoretical biologist; originated the concept in *The Origins of Order: Self-Organization and Selection in Evolution* (1993) and *Investigations* (2000), studying how chemical autocatalytic sets explore possibility space
- **Steven Johnson** -- science writer; popularized and extended the concept to innovation history in *Where Good Ideas Come From* (2010)

## Core Concepts

### The Expanding Boundary Property

The most distinctive feature of the adjacent possible is that **its boundaries grow as you explore them**. Each door you open reveals new doors that were previously invisible. In chemistry, each new molecule enables new reactions; in technology, each new invention enables new combinations; in knowledge, each new insight reframes what questions become askable.

| Property | Description | Example |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| **Constraint** | Only first-order steps are reachable; you cannot leap to distant possibilities | Babbage's Analytical Engine (1837) could not be built because vacuum tubes, transistors, and programming languages did not yet exist |
| **Expansion** | Each exploration expands the frontier | The invention of the transistor (1947) opened the adjacent possible for integrated circuits (1958), which opened the possible for microprocessors (1971) |
| **Simultaneity** | When the adjacent possible is ready, multiple discoverers converge | Calculus (Newton & Leibniz, 1680s); natural selection (Darwin & Wallace, 1858); telephone (Bell & Gray, 1876) |

### Origin in Kauffman's Origin-of-Life Research

Kauffman's work on **chemical autocatalytic sets** -- self-sustaining networks of chemical reactions -- demonstrated that sufficiently diverse molecular systems spontaneously generate catalytic closure, where the products of some reactions catalyze other reactions in the network. The adjacent possible in this context is the set of molecules reachable by one additional reaction step. Kauffman argued that life itself emerged not through an improbable single event but through the systematic exploration of an expanding adjacent possible in prebiotic chemistry.

### Johnson's Extension to Innovation History

Johnson maps Kauffman's framework onto centuries of human innovation, arguing that the adjacent possible explains:

- **Why some ideas fail despite brilliance**: Charles Babbage designed a general-purpose computer in the 1830s, but the adjacent possible of Victorian-era manufacturing could not support it. The components (vacuum tubes, transistors, programming languages) were decades or centuries away.
- **Why multiples occur**: When the adjacent possible is ready, the innovation becomes almost inevitable. Johnson documents dozens of cases where the same invention was independently discovered by multiple people within months or years.
- **Why innovation accelerates**: Each generation of innovations expands the adjacent possible for the next generation, creating an accelerating cascade of possibility.

### Historical Examples

| Innovation | Adjacent Possible Context | Outcome |
|-----------|--------------------------|---------|
| **Babbage's Analytical Engine (1837)** | Required precision manufacturing, electronics, and software concepts that did not yet exist | Failed -- too far ahead of its adjacent possible |
| **YouTube (2005)** | Required broadband internet, cheap storage, Flash video, digital cameras -- all of which had recently arrived | Succeeded -- the adjacent possible was ready |
| **Darwin's Natural Selection (1858)** | Required Malthus's population theory, Lyell's deep time, biogeography data from global exploration | Emerged simultaneously with Wallace because the intellectual adjacent possible was mature |
| **GPS Navigation (1990s-2000s)** | Required satellites (Cold War), atomic clocks, microprocessors, mobile computing | Each precursor opened the next door |

### Relationship to Combinatorial Innovation

The adjacent possible is fundamentally a **combinatorial** concept: new possibilities arise from recombining existing elements. This connects to Johnson's broader argument that innovation is primarily a process of connecting and recombining existing ideas, technologies, and components rather than creating entirely new elements from nothing. The adjacent possible defines which combinations are currently reachable.

### Empirical Support

- **Simultaneous discovery research**: Sociologists of science (Robert K. Merton, 1961) documented hundreds of cases of "multiples" -- independent simultaneous discoveries -- which the adjacent possible framework explains as the natural consequence of a shared innovation frontier.
- **Technology forecasting**: The adjacent possible framework has been adopted in technology strategy and foresight, where analysts map which innovations are "one step away" given current infrastructure and knowledge.
- **Evolutionary biology**: The concept has been validated in studies of evolutionary innovation, where new phenotypic traits emerge from recombinations of existing developmental pathways (Wagner, 2011, *The Origins of Evolutionary Innovations*).

## Practical Applications

### In Innovation Strategy
- Organizations can map their adjacent possible by systematically inventorying the technologies, knowledge, and capabilities currently available and asking what first-order recombinations are feasible. This prevents both over-ambitious "moonshot" projects that outrun the adjacent possible and under-ambitious incrementalism that fails to explore the frontier.

### In Knowledge Management
- The Zettelkasten and slip-box methodology operationalize the adjacent possible for ideas: by accumulating atomic notes and linking them, a knowledge worker expands the intellectual adjacent possible available for recombination. Each new note opens connections to other notes that were previously invisible, mirroring the expanding boundary property.

### In Organizational Design
- Liquid networks (open offices, cross-functional teams, internal platforms) expand the organizational adjacent possible by increasing the surface area for idea collision. Rigid silos, by contrast, constrain the adjacent possible to within-team recombinations.

### In Abuse Prevention and Fraud Detection
- New fraud patterns emerge from the adjacent possible of existing attack vectors: when a new platform feature or policy is introduced, it expands the adjacent possible for both legitimate and abusive uses. Mapping the adjacent possible of abuse vectors allows proactive identification of emerging threats before they materialize at scale.

## Criticisms and Limitations

- **Retrospective clarity, prospective ambiguity**: The adjacent possible is much easier to identify in retrospect than in prospect. After an innovation succeeds, it is straightforward to show that the precursors were in place; before it succeeds, the relevant precursors may be difficult to identify among many candidate components.
- **Boundary definition**: The concept does not provide precise criteria for determining what counts as "one step away." Different analysts may draw the boundary of the adjacent possible differently depending on their perspective and domain expertise.
- **Determinism risk**: The framework can imply that innovations are inevitable once the adjacent possible is ready, which understates the role of individual agency, effort, luck, and contingency in translating possibility into reality.
- **Selection problem**: The adjacent possible at any moment contains vastly more possibilities than will ever be explored. The framework explains what *could* happen but not what *will* happen -- it is a necessary but not sufficient condition for innovation.
- **Oversimplification of failure**: Attributing the failure of "ahead-of-their-time" innovations solely to the adjacent possible may neglect other failure factors such as lack of funding, market readiness, or poor execution.
