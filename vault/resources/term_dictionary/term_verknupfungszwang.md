# Verknüpfungszwang

## Definition

**Verknüpfungszwang** (German: "compulsion to connect" or "linking compulsion") is a term originating from art historian Aby Warburg's approach to iconographic analysis, describing an obsessive drive to find connections between images, symbols, and cultural artifacts. In the Zettelkasten context, Bob Doto repurposes the concept in *A System for Writing* (2024) to describe a common note-taking anti-pattern: compulsively linking notes to every possible related note, prioritising link quantity over link quality. The result is a dense but noisy network where meaningful connections are buried under superficial ones.

## Historical Context

Aby Warburg (1866-1929) coined the term to describe his own methodology — and perhaps pathology — of tracing visual connections across cultures and centuries in his *Mnemosyne Atlas* project. Warburg saw connections everywhere, sometimes productively, sometimes obsessively. Bob Doto adopted the term in 2024 to name a failure mode he observed in Zettelkasten practitioners who treat linking as an end in itself rather than a tool for developing thinking.

## Key Properties

- Manifests as creating links between notes at every possible opportunity, regardless of connection strength
- Degrades signal-to-noise ratio in the note network — important connections become indistinguishable from trivial ones
- Often co-occurs with Fetzenwissen (fragmentary notes + excessive links compound each other)
- Creates cognitive overhead during retrieval — too many links to evaluate
- The antidote is intentional, meaningful linking where each connection serves a specific intellectual purpose
- Related to Sascha's Sound Communication principle — links should serve retrieval, not demonstrate thoroughness
