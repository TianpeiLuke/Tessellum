# System Archetypes

## Definition

**System archetypes** are recurring structural patterns of interacting reinforcing and balancing feedback loops that generate characteristic, predictable behavior over time across diverse systems. They serve as diagnostic templates: once a practitioner recognizes which archetype is operating, the underlying causal structure -- and its likely trajectory -- becomes visible, along with high-leverage intervention points.

The concept was developed in the system dynamics tradition at MIT. **Jay Forrester** laid the groundwork with his modeling of industrial and urban systems in the 1960s. **Peter Senge**, in *The Fifth Discipline* (1990), coined the term "system archetype" and, together with Michael Goodman, Jennifer Kemeny, and colleagues, codified the first widely adopted set of archetypes for organizational diagnosis. **Donella Meadows** independently framed many of the same patterns as "system traps" in *Thinking in Systems: A Primer* (2008), emphasizing that each trap has a corresponding "opportunity" -- a leverage point for restructuring the system.

System archetypes are depicted through **causal loop diagrams (CLDs)**, which show how variables connect via reinforcing (R) and balancing (B) loops with delays. The power of archetypes lies in their cross-domain transferability: the same "Escalation" structure appears in arms races, price wars, and bureaucratic turf battles.

## Taxonomy: The Core Archetypes

The following table synthesizes the two major framings -- Senge's organizational archetypes and Meadows' system traps -- into a unified reference. All eight archetypes requested are covered, plus two additional Senge archetypes commonly referenced in the literature.

| # | Archetype | Alternate Names | Structure | Behavior Pattern |
|---|-----------|----------------|-----------|-----------------|
| 1 | **Policy Resistance** | Fixes that Backfire, Balancing Loop Resistance | Multiple actors pull toward conflicting goals; balancing loops counteract each actor's efforts | System resists change; interventions are absorbed or neutralized, and conditions revert to baseline |
| 2 | **Tragedy of the Commons** | Environmental Erosion, Resource Depletion | Multiple agents exploit a shared resource driven by individual reinforcing loops; no balancing governance loop | Each actor intensifies use for personal gain until the shared resource degrades or collapses |
| 3 | **Drift to Low Performance** | Eroding Goals, Drifting Goals | A balancing loop adjusts goals downward to match declining performance rather than improving performance to meet goals | Standards erode incrementally; "good enough" ratchets down over time (the boiling-frog effect) |
| 4 | **Escalation** | Arms Race, Accidental Adversaries | Two reinforcing loops interlock: each party's action triggers a stronger response from the other | Competitive spiraling -- arms races, price wars, retaliatory regulation -- until one party collapses or external intervention occurs |
| 5 | **Success to the Successful** | Competitive Exclusion, Winner-Take-All | A reinforcing loop allocates more resources to whichever actor is currently performing best | Initial advantage compounds; the rich get richer while the disadvantaged lose access to resources |
| 6 | **Shifting the Burden** | Addiction, Shifting the Burden to the Intervenor | A symptomatic solution (quick fix) weakens the capacity for fundamental solution through a reinforcing side-effect loop | Dependency on the quick fix grows; the system's ability to self-correct atrophies over time |
| 7 | **Rule Beating** | Impression Management, Gaming the System | Agents find ways to satisfy the letter of a rule while violating its intent; the rule becomes the target rather than the goal it was meant to serve | Compliance metrics look satisfied but the underlying goal is not achieved; perverse incentives distort behavior |
| 8 | **Seeking the Wrong Goal** | Measurement Fixation, What Gets Measured Gets Manipulated | The system efficiently pursues a poorly defined or proxy goal that diverges from the actual desired outcome | The system optimizes for the wrong metric (e.g., GNP instead of well-being); the harder the system works, the further it drifts from its true purpose |
| 9 | **Limits to Growth** | Limits to Success | A reinforcing growth loop encounters a balancing constraint loop that slows or halts growth | Exponential early growth gradually flattens as limiting factors (resource depletion, capacity, market saturation) dominate |
| 10 | **Growth and Underinvestment** | Limits to Growth with Underinvestment | Growth requires capacity investment; underinvestment triggers a balancing loop that constrains growth, seemingly validating the original non-investment decision | Performance degrades, confirming the perception that investment would be wasted -- a self-fulfilling prophecy |

## Key Properties

- **Composed of feedback loops**: Every archetype is built from specific configurations of reinforcing (R) and balancing (B) feedback loops, often with delays
- **Cross-domain transferability**: The same structural pattern recurs in organizations, ecosystems, economies, and social systems -- only the variable names change
- **Predictable trajectories**: Once the archetype is identified, the likely behavior over time (growth-then-plateau, oscillation, collapse, escalation) is knowable in advance
- **Diagnostic, not prescriptive**: Archetypes explain *why* a problematic behavior persists; the intervention must be tailored to the specific context
- **Leverage points are structural**: Effective intervention targets the structure (loop gains, delays, information flows, goals) rather than symptoms
- **Delays amplify problems**: Most archetypes involve time delays between action and consequence, which cause decision-makers to overshoot or miss the window for intervention
- **Multiple archetypes co-occur**: Real-world systems often exhibit several archetypes simultaneously (e.g., Shifting the Burden combined with Drift to Low Performance)
- **Causal loop diagrams (CLDs)** are the standard visualization tool, showing polarity (+/-) and delays on each link

## Applications

| Domain | Archetype in Action | Example |
|--------|--------------------|---------|
| **Organizational management** | Shifting the Burden | Relying on overtime instead of hiring; relying on concessions instead of fixing root causes of abuse |
| **Environmental policy** | Tragedy of the Commons | Overfishing; groundwater depletion; carbon emissions without governance |
| **Competitive strategy** | Escalation | Price wars between rivals; feature wars in product development |
| **Resource allocation** | Success to the Successful | Internal budgets flow to already-successful teams, starving innovation |
| **Abuse prevention** | Policy Resistance | Tightening return policies shifts abuse to concession requests through customer service |
| **Public health** | Fixes that Fail (Shifting Burden) | Treating chronic disease symptoms without addressing lifestyle root causes |
| **Software engineering** | Growth and Underinvestment | Deferring infrastructure investment until technical debt constrains feature delivery |
| **Education** | Drift to Low Performance | Grade inflation: lowering standards to match declining performance rather than improving instruction |

## Challenges and Limitations

- **Recognition difficulty**: Identifying which archetype is operating requires systems thinking skill and often extensive causal mapping; practitioners may misdiagnose the pattern
- **Oversimplification risk**: Mapping a complex situation to a single archetype may omit critical causal factors unique to the context
- **Narrative seduction**: Causal loop diagrams create compelling stories that feel explanatory but may not survive rigorous empirical validation
- **Static vs. dynamic**: CLDs show structure but do not quantify magnitudes, delays, or nonlinearities -- full system dynamics simulation is needed for precise prediction
- **Intervention complexity**: Knowing the archetype does not guarantee that a feasible, politically acceptable intervention exists
- **Boundary sensitivity**: The archetype identified depends on where the analyst draws the system boundary

## Related Terms

- **[Systems Thinking](term_systems_thinking.md)**: The parent discipline; system archetypes are one of its core diagnostic tools
- **[MECE](term_mece.md)**: Decomposition principle that complements archetypes -- MECE structures the parts, archetypes reveal how the parts interact
- **[Five Whys](term_five_whys.md)**: Linear root-cause analysis; archetypes reveal the feedback loops that Five Whys' linear chains may miss
- **[Critical Thinking](term_critical_thinking.md)**: Archetypes are a systems-level extension of critical thinking -- they diagnose structural reasoning failures
- **[Groupthink](term_groupthink.md)**: Groupthink is itself a system archetype: conformity reinforcing loops suppress dissent
- **[Compound Effect](term_compound_effect.md)**: The compound effect is the reinforcing loop that drives many archetypes (Success to the Successful, Escalation)
- **[Habit Loop](term_habit_loop.md)**: The habit loop is a micro-level reinforcing loop; archetypes describe macro-level loop configurations
- **[Sunk Cost Fallacy](term_sunk_cost_fallacy.md)**: Sunk cost reasoning can drive Drift to Low Performance by anchoring to past investment rather than current standards
