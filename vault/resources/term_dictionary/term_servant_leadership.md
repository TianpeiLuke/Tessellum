# Servant Leadership

## Definition

**Servant Leadership** is a leadership philosophy and set of practices in which the leader's primary role is to serve others — employees, customers, community, and stakeholders — rather than to accumulate power or pursue personal ambition. The term was coined by **Robert K. Greenleaf** in his 1970 essay *The Servant as Leader*, where he articulated the foundational distinction: **"The servant-leader is servant first... It begins with the natural feeling that one wants to serve, to serve first. Then conscious choice brings one to aspire to lead."** This reversal of the traditional leader-first model is the defining characteristic of the philosophy.

Greenleaf proposed a practical test for servant leadership: **"Do those served grow as persons? Do they, while being served, become healthier, wiser, freer, more autonomous, more likely themselves to become servants?"** This test shifts evaluation from organizational output metrics to the growth and well-being of the people being led. A servant leader shares power, puts employees' needs first, and helps people develop and perform as highly as possible, creating a social exchange relationship in which employees feel valued and reciprocate through commitment, creativity, and organizational citizenship behavior.

The philosophy contrasts sharply with traditional **command-and-control leadership**, where authority flows top-down and followers exist to serve the leader's vision. In servant leadership, the hierarchy is conceptually inverted: the leader exists to remove obstacles, provide resources, and create conditions for others to succeed. Research has shown that servant leadership positively influences employee engagement, organizational performance, prosocial behavior, and team creativity.

## Historical Context

Robert K. Greenleaf (1904-1990) spent 40 years at AT&T, retiring in 1964 as Director of Management Research. That same year he founded **The Center for Applied Ethics**, later renamed **The Greenleaf Center for Servant-Leadership**. His inspiration came from reading Hermann Hesse's 1932 novel *Journey to the East* in 1958, in which the central character Leo accompanies a group as a servant who does menial chores but also sustains them with his spirit and song. When Leo disappears, the group falls apart — revealing that Leo was in fact the titular head of the Order, its guiding spirit, and a great leader all along.

This story crystallized Greenleaf's insight that **the best leaders are servants first**, and he published *The Servant as Leader* in 1970, followed by *The Institution as Servant* (1972) and *Trustees as Servants* (1975). The essay was later expanded into a book that became one of the most influential management texts of the 20th century.

## Taxonomy

Larry Spears, who served as CEO of the Greenleaf Center for Servant-Leadership, extracted from Greenleaf's writings a set of **ten characteristics** he viewed as central to the development of servant-leaders (Spears, 1992/2010):

| # | Characteristic | Description |
|---|---------------|-------------|
| 1 | **Listening** | Deep commitment to listening intently to others; identifying the will of the group and helping clarify that will; listening to what is said and unsaid |
| 2 | **Empathy** | Striving to understand and empathize with others; accepting and recognizing people for their unique spirits; assuming good intentions |
| 3 | **Healing** | Recognizing the potential for healing oneself and one's relationship to others; making broken spirits whole as a powerful force for transformation |
| 4 | **Awareness** | General awareness and especially self-awareness; understanding issues involving ethics, power, and values; viewing situations from an integrated, holistic position |
| 5 | **Persuasion** | Relying on persuasion rather than positional authority; convincing rather than coercing; building consensus within groups |
| 6 | **Conceptualization** | Nurturing the ability to dream great dreams; thinking beyond day-to-day realities to see the bigger picture; balancing operational focus with conceptual vision |
| 7 | **Foresight** | Understanding lessons from the past, realities of the present, and likely consequences of decisions for the future; deeply rooted in intuitive understanding |
| 8 | **Stewardship** | Holding institutions in trust for the greater good of society; commitment to serving the needs of others; emphasis on openness and persuasion over control |
| 9 | **Commitment to Growth** | Deep commitment to the growth of every individual; providing opportunities for personal, professional, and spiritual development |
| 10 | **Building Community** | Seeking to build genuine community among those who work within institutions; counteracting the shift from local communities to impersonal large institutions |

### Related Frameworks

Several scholars have proposed alternative or complementary models:

| Framework | Author(s) | Key Dimensions |
|-----------|-----------|----------------|
| **Spears' Ten Characteristics** | Larry Spears (1992) | Listening, empathy, healing, awareness, persuasion, conceptualization, foresight, stewardship, growth, community |
| **Patterson's Servant Leadership Model** | Kathleen Patterson (2003) | Agapao love, humility, altruism, vision, trust, empowerment, service |
| **Laub's Servant OLA Model** | Jim Laub (1999) | Values people, develops people, builds community, displays authenticity, provides leadership, shares leadership |
| **Sendjaya & Sarros Model** | Sendjaya & Sarros (2002) | Voluntary subordination, authentic self, covenantal relationship, responsible morality, transcendental spirituality, transforming influence |

## Key Properties

- **Service-first orientation**: The desire to serve precedes and motivates the aspiration to lead; leadership is a means of service, not the other way around
- **Inverted hierarchy**: Conceptually inverts the traditional organizational pyramid — the leader is at the bottom, supporting and lifting everyone above
- **Follower development as primary outcome**: Success is measured by the growth, autonomy, and well-being of those served, not by organizational metrics alone
- **Power through influence, not authority**: Servant leaders rely on persuasion, example, and moral authority rather than positional power or coercion
- **Ethical foundation**: Grounded in moral responsibility and stewardship; the leader holds power in trust for the benefit of others
- **Holistic concern**: Attends to the whole person — professional development, emotional well-being, personal growth, and community belonging
- **Self-awareness as prerequisite**: Awareness of one's own strengths, limitations, values, and impact is foundational to authentic service
- **Community building**: Actively works to create genuine community and belonging within institutions, counteracting alienation and isolation

## Notable Systems / Implementations

| Organization | Leader(s) | Servant Leadership Practices |
|-------------|-----------|------------------------------|
| **Southwest Airlines** | Herb Kelleher, Colleen Barrett | Employees first philosophy; empowered frontline workers to make decisions; invested deeply in employee development; Kelleher mentored Barrett from legal secretary to CEO |
| **Starbucks** | Howard Schultz | Healthcare benefits and stock options ("Bean Stock") for part-time employees; culture of belonging and inclusion; open communication encouraged at all levels |
| **TD Industries** | Jack Lowe Sr., Jack Lowe Jr. | One of the earliest adopters; built company culture explicitly around Greenleaf's principles; consistently named among Fortune's best companies to work for |
| **The Container Store** | Kip Tindell | "1 great person = 3 good people" hiring philosophy; Foundation Principles emphasizing employee well-being; industry-leading wages and training investment |
| **Synovus Financial** | James Blanchard | Culture of the Heart program; servant leadership embedded in corporate governance and strategic planning |

## Challenges and Limitations

- **Perceived softness**: Critics argue the approach may be too passive for high-stakes, fast-paced environments requiring decisive unilateral action; the term "servant" can be misinterpreted as weakness or submissiveness
- **Crisis decision-making tension**: The emphasis on consensus and collaboration may slow decision-making during crises when rapid, authoritative action is needed
- **Burnout risk**: Leaders who prioritize everyone else's needs may neglect their own well-being, leading to exhaustion and unsustainable leadership patterns
- **Exploitation vulnerability**: Unscrupulous followers may take advantage of leaders who consistently prioritize others' needs; unclear boundaries can be misconstrued as permissiveness
- **Dependency creation**: Excessive healing and empathy may create a disabling environment where employees become dependent on the leader rather than developing their own agency
- **Cultural fit dependency**: Effectiveness varies significantly across organizational cultures; servant leadership thrives in human-oriented cultures but may struggle in highly hierarchical or competitive environments
- **Measurement difficulty**: Core concepts are abstract and difficult to operationalize for rigorous empirical research, though validated instruments (e.g., Liden et al., 2008; Ehrhart, 2004) have improved measurement
- **Scope limitation**: In their focus on serving subordinates, servant leaders may underserve their own chain of command, peers, or broader stakeholders

## Related Terms

- **[Circle of Influence](term_circle_of_influence.md)**: Servant leaders focus energy on their Circle of Influence — the people they can directly serve and develop — rather than seeking to expand positional authority within the Circle of Concern
- **[Emotional Bank Account](term_emotional_bank_account.md)**: Servant leadership systematically builds Emotional Bank Account deposits (understanding individuals, keeping commitments, showing integrity) that create reserves of organizational trust
- **[Empathic Listening](term_empathic_listening.md)**: One of Spears' ten characteristics; empathic listening is both a core servant leadership practice and the behavioral mechanism through which servant leaders understand and serve followers' needs
- **[Win-Win Thinking](term_win_win_thinking.md)**: Servant leadership's emphasis on mutual benefit and follower development aligns with Win-Win Thinking — the leader seeks outcomes where both the organization and its people thrive
- **[Emotional Intelligence](term_emotional_intelligence.md)**: Self-awareness, empathy, and relationship management — core emotional intelligence competencies — are prerequisites for effective servant leadership practice
- **[Abundance Mentality](term_abundance_mentality.md)**: Servant leaders operate from an abundance mentality — believing there is enough success, recognition, and power for everyone — which enables genuine empowerment without feeling threatened
