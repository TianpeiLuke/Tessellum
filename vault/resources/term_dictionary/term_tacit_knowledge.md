# Tacit Knowledge

## Definition

Tacit knowledge is knowledge that is difficult to articulate, formalize, or transfer through written or verbal communication. Coined by Michael Polanyi (1958, 1966) with the phrase "we can know more than we can tell," tacit knowledge encompasses skills, intuitions, mental models, and experiential understanding that individuals possess but cannot easily express. It contrasts with explicit knowledge, which can be codified in documents, databases, and formal languages.

In organizational contexts, tacit knowledge includes expertise gained through years of practice, intuitive judgment in complex situations, cultural norms understood but unwritten, and relational knowledge about how to navigate organizational dynamics.

## Historical Context

| Year | Development |
|------|------------|

## Key Properties

- **Personal and context-dependent**: Rooted in individual experience, hard to separate from the knower
- **Difficult to codify**: Cannot be fully captured in rules, procedures, or documents
- **Transferred through practice**: Best shared through apprenticeship, observation, and shared experience
- **Often unconscious**: The knower may not be aware of what they know (e.g., expert intuition)
- **High organizational value**: Often the most valuable knowledge in an organization, yet the most vulnerable to loss through turnover
- **Complementary to explicit**: Tacit and explicit knowledge exist on a spectrum, not as a binary
