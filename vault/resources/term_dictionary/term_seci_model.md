# SECI Model (Nonaka-Takeuchi Model)

## Definition

The SECI model of knowledge dimensions, developed by Ikujiro Nonaka (1990) and refined with Hirotaka Takeuchi (1995), explains how tacit and explicit knowledge are converted into organizational knowledge through four modes forming a continuous spiral: Socialization (tacit→tacit), Externalization (tacit→explicit), Combination (explicit→explicit), and Internalization (explicit→tacit). The model has achieved paradigmatic status in knowledge management research.

The concept of "Ba" (roughly "place") was later added — a shared context (physical, virtual, or mental) in which knowledge is created and utilized.

## Historical Context

| Year | Development |
|------|------------|
| 1990 | Nonaka introduces the model in Japanese |
| 1991 | "The Knowledge Creating Company" in Harvard Business Review |
| 1995 | Nonaka & Takeuchi publish the foundational book |
| 1998 | Nonaka & Konno add the concept of "Ba" |
| 2000 | Nonaka, Toyama & Konno unify SECI, Ba, and leadership |
| 2006 | Gourlay critiques the model's empirical foundations |

## Taxonomy

| Mode | Knowledge Flow | Mechanism | Example |
|------|---------------|-----------|---------|
| **Socialization** | Tacit → Tacit | Observation, imitation, shared experience | Apprenticeship, pair programming |
| **Externalization** | Tacit → Explicit | Articulation, metaphor, analogy | Writing documentation, creating diagrams |
| **Combination** | Explicit → Explicit | Organizing, integrating, synthesizing | Database merging, report compilation |
| **Internalization** | Explicit → Tacit | Learning-by-doing, practice | Reading manuals then applying knowledge |

## Key Properties

- **Spiral dynamics**: The four modes form a continuous spiral — knowledge created in one mode feeds into the next
- **Bidirectional**: Knowledge flows both ways between tacit and explicit
- **Social process**: Knowledge creation is fundamentally social, not individual
- **Ba-dependent**: Each mode requires a different type of shared context (Ba)
- **Organizational scope**: Designed for organizational knowledge, not individual learning

## Challenges and Limitations

- **Japanese-organization bias**: Based on study of Japanese companies with lifetime employment — may not generalize
- **Linearity assumption**: The spiral implies a fixed sequence; knowledge conversion may skip or reverse steps
- **Socialization starting point**: Gourlay (2006) questions why the spiral must begin with Socialization
- **No mechanism for new ideas**: The model describes knowledge conversion but not how genuinely new ideas emerge (Bereiter, 2002)
- **Difficult to operationalize**: The modes are conceptually clear but hard to measure or implement in practice
