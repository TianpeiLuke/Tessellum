# Term: Circular Reasoning

## Definition

**Circular reasoning** (also: begging the question, petitio principii) is a logical fallacy in which the conclusion of an argument is assumed in one of its premises. The argument appears to prove something, but the conclusion merely restates the starting assumption in different words. "This is the right approach because it's the best way to do it" is circular — "right" and "best" are synonyms, and no independent evidence is provided. The circularity is often obscured by **verbose restatement** — using enough words and complexity that the repetition is not immediately obvious.

In its classic form (petitio principii), the premise that requires proof is smuggled in as a given. Circular reasoning is particularly common in policy justifications ("We must continue this program because it's an essential program") and self-referential definitions ("An abuse case is a case that involves abuse"). Unlike other fallacies which introduce irrelevant elements, circular reasoning fails because it introduces **no new information** — the conclusion adds nothing beyond what was already assumed.

The fallacy is a member of the **fallacies of presumption** category: the argument presupposes what it claims to prove. Detection requires stripping away jargon and paraphrase to check whether the premise and conclusion are logically equivalent statements.

## Full Name

**Circular Reasoning** (also known as: Begging the Question, Petitio Principii, Circular Argument, Circular Logic, Tautological Argument, Assuming the Conclusion, Question-Begging)

## Logical Structure

| Step | Content |
|------|---------|
| Premise | A is true because B |
| Hidden dependency | B is true because A |
| Conclusion | Therefore A is true |
| **Why invalid** | No independent evidence is introduced; the argument is entirely self-supporting |

## Examples

| Context | Fallacious Argument | Why It Is Circular |
|---------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Meeting** | "We should prioritize this initiative because it's our top priority" | "Should prioritize" and "top priority" are the same claim restated |
| **Data analysis** | "The model is accurate because its predictions match what we expect" | If expectations define accuracy, the model is never testable |
| **Policy debate** | "This regulation is necessary because without it we'd have an unregulated situation" | The necessity of regulation is assumed, not demonstrated |
| **Code review** | "This is the correct design pattern because it's the right way to structure this code" | "Correct" and "right way" are synonyms; no independent design rationale is given |

## Detection and Countermeasures

| Strategy | How It Works |
|----------|-------------|
| **Ask for independent evidence** | "What evidence supports this claim that doesn't assume the conclusion?" — forces the arguer to break out of the circle |
| **Paraphrase stripping** | Restate the argument in its simplest form — if the premise and conclusion say the same thing, the argument is circular |
| **Socratic questioning (Type 3)** | Probe reasons and evidence: "What led you to that conclusion?" exposes the absence of external justification |
| **Definitional check** | Ask whether key terms are being defined in terms of each other — self-referential definitions are a common vehicle for circularity |

## Relationship to Cognitive Biases

| Bias | How It Enables Circular Reasoning |
|------|----------------------------------|
| **Coherence heuristic** | Circular arguments feel internally consistent because they are — they just don't prove anything |
| **WYSIATI** | The circularity is not flagged as missing information; System 1 accepts the apparent completeness |
| **Mere exposure effect** | Repeated assertions feel more true; circular arguments re-expose the listener to the same claim |
| **Fluency heuristic** | Verbose restatement makes the argument feel sophisticated and therefore more credible |
