# Growth Mindset

## Definition

A **growth mindset** is the belief that human abilities — including intelligence, talent, and personality — are not fixed traits but can be developed through effort, effective strategies, and support from others. The concept was developed by Stanford psychologist **Carol S. Dweck** and formally introduced as part of her **implicit theories of intelligence** framework (Dweck & Leggett, 1988). A growth mindset contrasts with a **fixed mindset**, the belief that abilities are innate, stable, and unchangeable.

In Dweck's original terminology, these were called **incremental theory** (growth mindset) and **entity theory** (fixed mindset). The more accessible terms were adopted with the publication of her popular book *Mindset: The New Psychology of Success* (2006). The core insight is that mindsets function as **meaning systems** — organizing frameworks that shape goals, effort beliefs, attributions, and responses to setbacks. Students with a growth mindset tend to adopt learning goals, persist through difficulty, and treat failure as informational feedback rather than evidence of inability.

## Historical Context

| Period | Figure(s) | Contribution |
|--------|-----------|-------------|
| 1970s | **Carol Dweck**, Reppucci | Discovered that children with equal ability showed dramatically different responses to failure — some "wilted" while others "relished the challenge" |
| 1983 | **Dweck**, Mary Bandura | Critical insight during Bandura's dissertation: "the ability people wish to *prove* felt different from the ability people wish to *improve*" |
| 1988 | **Dweck & Leggett** | Formalized implicit theories in *Psychological Review* — entity vs. incremental theories as organizing meaning systems for goals, attributions, and persistence |
| 1998 | **Mueller & Dweck** | Six studies showed person-focused praise ("You're smart") produces fixed mindsets; process praise ("You worked hard") fosters growth mindsets |
| 2006 | **Carol Dweck** | Published *Mindset: The New Psychology of Success* — popularized the concept beyond academia |
| 2007 | **Blackwell, Trzesniewski, Dweck** | Longitudinal study: growth mindset intervention improved grades through junior high school transition |
| 2015 | **Dweck** | Introduced the concept of "false growth mindset" — superficial implementation that misses the theory's nuances |
| 2018 | **Sisk et al.** | Two meta-analyses (273 studies, 365,915 participants) found weak overall relationship between mindset and achievement, sparking replication debate |
| 2019 | **Yeager et al.** | National Study of Learning Mindsets — large-scale RCT showed brief online intervention improved grades for lower-achieving 9th graders |

## Taxonomy

### Mindset Types

| Mindset | Core Belief | Goal Orientation | Response to Failure | Effort Belief |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|---------------------|---------------|
| **Fixed (Entity)** | Abilities are innate and stable | Performance goals — prove ability | Attribute failure to lack of ability; reduce effort | High effort signals low ability |
| **Growth (Incremental)** | Abilities can be developed | Learning goals — develop ability | Attribute failure to strategy/effort; persist | Effort is the path to mastery |
| **False Growth** | Claims growth mindset but practices fixed | Superficial — praises effort without strategy | Accepts incompetence; blames students for not trying | Effort alone is sufficient (ignores strategy) |

### Domain Specificity

Mindsets are not necessarily global — a person can hold a growth mindset about athletic ability while holding a fixed mindset about mathematical ability. Research has extended beyond intelligence to:

| Domain | Extension | Key Finding |
|--------|-----------|-------------|
| **Personality** | Belief that social traits are malleable | Reduced aggression and increased empathy in adolescents (Yeager et al., 2013) |
| **Groups** | Belief that group characteristics can change | Increased willingness for peace concessions in Israeli-Palestinian conflict (Halperin et al., 2011) |
| **Mental health** | Belief that emotional states are not fixed | Reduced depression and stress symptoms in adolescents |
| **Prejudice** | Belief that prejudice can be changed | Reduced intergroup anxiety and increased diversity engagement |

## Key Properties

- **Meaning system**: Mindsets are not isolated beliefs but organizing frameworks that systematize goals, attributions, effort beliefs, and behavioral responses into coherent patterns
- **Malleability**: Mindsets themselves can be changed through targeted interventions — the core premise of mindset education
- **Praise sensitivity**: The type of feedback shapes mindset formation — person praise ("You're smart") fosters fixed mindsets; process praise ("Your strategy worked") fosters growth mindsets
- **Parental transmission pathway**: Parents' mindsets show little direct correlation with children's mindsets; instead, parents' *observable reactions to failure* predict children's belief systems (Haimovitz & Dweck)
- **Challenge-seeking**: Growth mindset predicts willingness to take on difficult tasks — students choose remedial courses or harder curriculum when they believe ability is developable
- **Neuroplasticity foundation**: Growth mindset interventions often teach that "the brain is like a muscle that gets stronger with exercise" — grounding the psychological belief in neuroscience
- **Context dependence**: Interventions are most effective for at-risk students (lower baseline grades), stigmatized populations, and in environments that support growth-oriented practices
- **Environmental interaction**: Student behaviors correlate more strongly with *instructor's* mindset than with students' own mindsets — fixed-mindset environments can negate individual growth mindsets
- **Dual-process connection**: Fixed mindset responses are often automatic (System 1); growth mindset requires deliberate reframing (System 2) until it becomes habitual

## Notable Systems / Implementations

| System | Mechanism | Domain |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| **Brainology** (Dweck, 2008) | Interactive computer program teaching neuroplasticity and growth mindset to middle schoolers | K-12 education |
| **National Study of Learning Mindsets** (Yeager et al., 2019) | Brief online intervention (~25 minutes) teaching growth mindset to 9th graders; pre-registered, nationally representative | Secondary education |
| **Saying-is-Believing Technique** (Aronson et al., 2002) | Participants write mentoring letters to younger students explaining growth mindset, reinforcing the belief through active generation | Higher education |
| **PERTS Mindset Kit** (Stanford) | Free, research-based resources for educators to implement growth mindset practices in classrooms | Teacher professional development |
| **Scalable Online Interventions** (Yeager & colleagues) | A/B tested, rapid-prototyped online modules with pre-registered evaluations; effect size ~0.10 grade points for lower-achieving students | Large-scale educational policy |

## Applications

| Domain | Application | Evidence |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| **K-12 education** | Teaching neuroplasticity to improve academic motivation and grades | Blackwell et al. (2007): improved math grades through junior high transition |
| **Higher education** | Supporting first-generation and underrepresented students | Aronson et al. (2002): improved grades for African American college students |
| **Workplace** | Organizational culture — companies with growth mindset cultures show more innovation and collaboration | Dweck et al.: growth-mindset organizations have more trusting, committed employees |
| **Sports coaching** | Coaching athletes to view setbacks as learning opportunities | Growth mindset predicts resilience and improvement after losses |
| **Clinical psychology** | Interventions for adolescent depression and social anxiety | Personality mindset interventions reduce aggression, increase empathy |
| **Conflict resolution** | Teaching that groups can change to promote peace | Halperin et al. (2011): effects persisted over six-month follow-up in Israeli-Palestinian context |
| **Parenting** | Process praise and failure-embracing feedback | Mueller & Dweck (1998): praise type causally influences children's mindset formation |

## Challenges and Limitations

### Empirical Challenges

- **Effect size debate**: Sisk et al. (2018) meta-analyses found overall weak relationship between mindset and achievement (r ≈ .10); however, the analysis gave equal weight to small convenience samples and large generalizable studies
- **Replication concerns**: Some mindset intervention studies have failed to replicate, particularly when implemented without fidelity to the original design
- **Heterogeneous results**: Effect sizes vary dramatically across studies, populations, and contexts — no single "mindset effect" exists
- **Measurement limitations**: Self-report mindset scales may capture what people *say* they believe rather than their *operative* beliefs under challenge

### Conceptual Challenges

- **False growth mindset**: Many practitioners claim growth mindset implementation while actually praising effort without strategy, accepting poor outcomes, or ignoring systemic barriers (Dweck, 2015)
- **Effort-only misconception**: The most common error is equating growth mindset with "just try harder" — genuine growth mindset includes strategy, mentorship, resources, and environmental support
- **Individual vs. structural**: Critics argue that growth mindset rhetoric can function as victim-blaming, placing responsibility on individuals while ignoring systemic barriers (socioeconomic disadvantage, discrimination)
- **Naturalism bias interaction**: Growth mindset directly challenges the widespread "naturalness bias" — the tendency to attribute success to innate talent rather than sustained effort (Duckworth)

### Ongoing Debates

- **Sisk vs. Dweck**: Whether the meta-analytic evidence supports or undermines mindset theory — methodological choices in weighting studies drive different conclusions
- **Scalability**: Whether brief online interventions can produce lasting effects without complementary environmental changes
- **Generalizability**: Whether interventions developed in US educational contexts transfer to other cultural settings
- **Mechanism clarity**: Whether mindset interventions work through belief change, belonging cues, stereotype threat reduction, or multiple pathways

## Related Terms

- **[Desirable Difficulties](term_desirable_difficulties.md)**: Learning science concept — effective learning requires productive struggle; growth mindset provides the motivational framework that makes desirable difficulties tolerable
- **[Deliberate Practice](term_deliberate_practice.md)**: The structured, effortful practice that growth mindset predicts willingness to engage in; growth mindset is the *belief* that deliberate practice works
- **[Flow State](term_flow_state.md)**: Optimal experience during skilled performance; growth mindset supports the challenge-seeking behavior that creates flow conditions
- **[Elaborative Interrogation](term_elaborative_interrogation.md)**: A learning strategy ("Why is this true?") that operationalizes growth mindset — treating knowledge as something to be actively constructed
- **[Retrieval Practice](term_retrieval_practice.md)**: A learning technique that produces desirable difficulties; growth mindset learners are more willing to engage in effortful retrieval
- **[Compound Effect](term_compound_effect.md)**: The principle that small, consistent actions compound over time; growth mindset provides the belief system that sustains compound-effect behaviors
- **[Self-Serving Bias](term_self_serving_bias.md)**: The tendency to attribute success to ability and failure to external factors; fixed mindset intensifies this pattern
- **[Fundamental Attribution Error](term_fundamental_attribution_error.md)**: Fixed mindset strengthens the tendency to infer stable traits from single behaviors; growth mindset reduces dispositional attribution
- **[Cognitive Bias](term_cognitive_bias.md)**: Fixed mindset interacts with multiple cognitive biases (confirmation bias, anchoring) by making initial self-assessments feel permanent
- **[Heuristic](term_heuristic.md)**: Mindset functions as a cognitive heuristic — a mental shortcut for interpreting success, failure, and effort that shapes subsequent behavior
- **[Critical Thinking](term_critical_thinking.md)**: Growth mindset is a prerequisite for genuine critical thinking — the willingness to question one's own assumptions requires believing those assumptions can change
