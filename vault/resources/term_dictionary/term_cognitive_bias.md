# Term: Cognitive Bias

## Definition

A **cognitive bias** is a systematic, predictable pattern of deviation from normative standards of rationality in judgment and decision-making. Unlike random errors, cognitive biases are directional: they push judgments and choices in particular directions that can be catalogued, measured, and (to some degree) anticipated. The concept was introduced by Amos Tversky and Daniel Kahneman in the early 1970s as part of their heuristics-and-biases research program, which demonstrated that people rely on mental shortcuts (heuristics) when making judgments under uncertainty, and that these shortcuts produce systematic errors.

Cognitive biases arise because the human brain evolved to make fast, good-enough decisions in ancestral environments rather than slow, optimal ones. In Kahneman's dual-process framework, most biases are products of System 1 -- the fast, automatic, intuitive mode of processing -- that System 2 (the slow, deliberate mode) fails to detect or correct. Biases are not signs of stupidity; they afflict experts and novices alike, and awareness of a bias does not by itself eliminate it. The practical implication is that debiasing strategies must operate at the process or environmental level (checklists, algorithms, choice architecture) rather than relying on individual vigilance.

As of 2016, psychologists have catalogued approximately 188 distinct cognitive biases, organized by Buster Benson into the Cognitive Bias Codex -- a visual taxonomy that groups biases by the four adaptive problems they evolved to solve: filtering information overload, constructing meaning from ambiguity, acting quickly under time pressure, and compressing memories for efficient storage.

## Full Name

Also known as:
- **Systematic bias** (in statistical/judgment contexts)
- **Judgment bias** (emphasis on judgment rather than perception)
- **Decision bias** (emphasis on choice behavior)
- **Thinking error** (informal)

Contrasted with:
- **Random error / noise** -- unsystematic variation in judgments (see Kahneman, Sibony & Sunstein, *Noise*, 2021)
- **Logical fallacy** -- errors in formal reasoning structure rather than intuitive judgment
- **Motivated reasoning** -- bias driven by emotional goals rather than cognitive architecture

## Origins and Intellectual History

### The Heuristics-and-Biases Program (1971-1979)

The systematic study of cognitive biases began with Tversky and Kahneman's landmark research:

| Year | Publication | Contribution |
|------|------------|--------------|
| 1971 | "Belief in the Law of Small Numbers" | Showed that researchers (not just laypeople) overestimate the reliability of small samples |
| 1972 | "Subjective probability: A judgment of representativeness" | Introduced representativeness heuristic |
| 1973 | "Availability: A heuristic for judging frequency and probability" | Introduced availability heuristic |
| 1974 | "Judgment under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases" (*Science*) | The landmark paper; introduced anchoring, availability, and representativeness as three core heuristics; gained worldwide attention |
| 1979 | "Prospect Theory: An Analysis of Decision under Risk" (*Econometrica*) | Formal model incorporating loss aversion, reference dependence, and probability weighting |

### Later Developments

- **Gigerenzer and colleagues (1990s)**: Argued that heuristics are not merely sources of error but can be "fast and frugal" strategies that outperform optimization in uncertain environments ("ecological rationality")
- **Buster Benson (2016)**: Organized ~188 known biases into the Cognitive Bias Codex, grouping them into four functional categories
- **Kahneman, Sibony & Sunstein (2021)**: *Noise* distinguished bias (systematic error) from noise (random variability), arguing that noise in human judgment is at least as costly as bias but receives far less attention

## Major Categories of Cognitive Biases

### Category 1: Too Much Information -- Filtering Biases

We notice things already primed in memory or repeated often:

| Bias | Mechanism | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Availability heuristic** | Ease of recall substitutes for frequency | Overestimating plane crash risk after news coverage |
| **Attentional bias** | Attending to emotionally salient stimuli | Threat-related information captures attention |
| **Confirmation bias** | Seeking evidence that supports existing beliefs | Interpreting ambiguous evidence as confirmatory |
| **Anchoring** | Initial numbers bias subsequent estimates | Salary negotiations anchored by first offer |

### Category 2: Not Enough Meaning -- Gap-Filling Biases

We find stories and patterns even in sparse or random data:

| Bias | Mechanism | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Representativeness** | Similarity to stereotype replaces probability | "Linda the feminist bank teller" conjunction fallacy |
| **Narrative fallacy** | Constructing coherent stories from random events | Hindsight bias -- "I knew it all along" |
| **Clustering illusion** | Seeing patterns in random sequences | "Hot hand" in basketball |
| **WYSIATI** | Drawing conclusions from available info only | Ignoring what you do not know |

### Category 3: Need to Act Fast -- Decision Biases

We favor the immediate, relatable, and default options:

| Bias | Mechanism | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Loss aversion** | Losses weighted ~2x more than gains | Refusing a fair gamble (50/50 for $110/$100) |
| **Status quo bias** | Preference for the current state | Low organ donation rates under opt-in defaults |
| **Optimism bias** | Overestimating positive outcomes | Entrepreneurs believing their venture will succeed |
| **Framing effect** | Presentation changes preference | "90% survival rate" vs. "10% mortality rate" |

### Category 4: Memory Constraints -- Storage Biases

We edit and reinforce some memories after the fact:

| Bias | Mechanism | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Peak-end rule** | Judging experiences by peak + end | Colonoscopy remembered as less painful if pain reduced at end |
| **Duration neglect** | Ignoring length of experience | A 30-minute painful procedure rated same as 60-minute one with identical peak/end |
| **Misinformation effect** | Post-event information alters memory | Leading questions change eyewitness testimony |
| **Rosy retrospection** | Rating past events more positively | Vacations remembered as better than experienced |

## Key Research and Evidence

- **"Judgment under Uncertainty" (Tversky & Kahneman, 1974, *Science*)**: The founding paper of the heuristics-and-biases program; demonstrated anchoring, availability, and representativeness with experimental evidence; one of the most cited papers in social science history
- **Pronin, Lin & Ross (2002) -- "The bias blind spot"**: People readily identify biases in others but believe themselves to be less biased, creating a "bias about biases"
- **Heuristics-and-Biases Inventory (Bertola et al., 2023, *Frontiers in Psychology*)**: An open-source instrument measuring individual differences across multiple heuristics and biases, confirming that biases vary in strength across individuals but remain systematic at the population level
- **Replication projects (2015-2022)**: Large-scale replication efforts have confirmed the robustness of core biases (anchoring, framing, loss aversion) while finding weaker-than-expected effects for some phenomena (ego depletion, certain priming effects)

## Practical Applications

### Organizational Decision-Making
- **Structured interviews**: Replace unstructured (System 1-driven) interviews with standardized criteria to reduce representativeness and halo effects
- **Reference class forecasting**: Use base rates from similar past projects rather than inside-view optimism (combats planning fallacy)
- **Premortem technique**: Before committing to a plan, imagine it has failed -- activates System 2 against optimism bias

### Abuse Prevention and Fraud Detection
- **Exploiting biases**: Social engineers and abusers deliberately trigger cognitive biases in customer service agents -- urgency creates time pressure that keeps agents in System 1; emotional narratives exploit availability and representativeness
- **Debiasing agents**: Algorithmic decision support acts as externalized System 2 for human agents, providing base rates and pattern detection that compensate for human heuristic processing
- **Understanding abuser cognition**: Some buyer abuse may itself reflect cognitive biases (entitlement bias, self-serving bias) rather than calculated fraud -- this distinction matters for policy design

### Personal Decision Hygiene
- Kahneman's "decision hygiene" recommendations: use algorithms where possible, aggregate independent judgments, impose structure, and use mediating assessments before holistic evaluation

## Criticisms and Limitations

- **Ecological rationality critique (Gigerenzer)**: Many heuristics are well-adapted to real-world environments; the "bias" label reflects a narrow comparison to formal logic rather than ecological success
- **WEIRD samples**: Most bias research was conducted on Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic populations; cross-cultural generalizability is an ongoing concern
- **Replication concerns**: Some specific biases (particularly priming-based effects) have shown weaker effect sizes in replication attempts; the core heuristics (anchoring, availability, framing) have replicated well
- **Debiasing difficulty**: Simply informing people about biases rarely eliminates them; effective debiasing requires environmental or procedural interventions
