# Term: Interleaving

## Definition

**Interleaving** (also called **interleaved practice** or **mixed practice**) is a learning strategy in which the learner alternates between different topics, problem types, concepts, or skills within a single study or practice session, rather than completing all practice on one topic before moving to the next. The contrasting approach -- practicing one type exhaustively before switching -- is called **blocked practice** (or massed practice by topic).

The defining feature of interleaving is that it forces the learner to repeatedly switch between different categories or problem types, which engages two critical cognitive processes: (1) **discrimination** -- identifying which type of problem or concept is being encountered, and (2) **strategy selection** -- choosing the appropriate solution method from among multiple options. Blocked practice bypasses both of these processes because the learner always knows what type of problem is coming next, reducing the task to rote execution of a single procedure.

Research consistently demonstrates that interleaving produces superior long-term retention and transfer compared to blocked practice, despite the fact that interleaving typically results in **worse performance during the practice session itself**. This paradox -- feeling less fluent but learning more -- makes interleaving a core example of the desirable difficulties framework (Bjork, 1994). The strategy is featured prominently in Brown, Roediger, and McDaniel's *Make It Stick* (2014) as one of seven evidence-based learning strategies.

## Full Name

Also known as:
- **Interleaved practice** (full academic term)
- **Mixed practice** (common synonym)
- **Shuffled practice** (used in Rohrer & Taylor, 2007)
- **Varied practice** (related but distinct -- varying the conditions within a single topic)

Contrast with:
- **Blocked practice** -- completing all examples of type A before any examples of type B
- **Massed practice** -- concentrating all practice into a single session (related to blocked, but refers to temporal distribution)

## How Interleaving Works

### The Discrimination-Retrieval Hypothesis

Birnbaum et al. (2013) proposed that interleaving enhances learning through two complementary mechanisms:

1. **Discrimination**: When problem types are mixed, the learner must first identify *what kind* of problem they are facing before attempting a solution. In blocked practice, this step is unnecessary -- the learner always knows the category. Discrimination is a critical real-world skill because problems outside the classroom never come pre-sorted by type.

2. **Retrieval of strategy**: After identifying the problem type, the learner must retrieve the corresponding solution strategy from memory. In blocked practice, the strategy is already loaded in working memory from the previous identical problem. Interleaving forces repeated retrieval of different strategies, strengthening each one.

### Why Interleaving Feels Harder

During interleaved practice, learners experience:
- **More errors** during practice (because they must discriminate and select strategies under uncertainty)
- **Slower performance** (because of the additional cognitive steps)
- **Lower confidence** (because the experience feels less fluent)

These features cause learners to consistently rate interleaving as **less effective** than blocking -- even when their test scores prove the opposite. This metacognitive illusion is one of the most replicated findings in the interleaving literature.

### The Interleaving-Spacing Connection

Interleaving naturally introduces **spacing** between repetitions of any given topic. When a learner alternates between topics A, B, and C (A-B-C-A-B-C), there is a gap between each encounter with topic A. This built-in spacing contributes to the interleaving benefit, but research (Kornell & Bjork, 2008; Kang & Pashler, 2012) shows that interleaving produces benefits **beyond** what spacing alone would predict -- the discrimination component provides an independent contribution.

## Key Research and Evidence

### Landmark Experiments

| Study | Year | Domain | Key Finding |
|-------|------|--------|-------------|
| **Kornell & Bjork** | 2008 | Art (painting styles) | Interleaving artists' paintings led to better identification of painting styles on a novel test -- despite participants believing blocking was more effective |
| **Rohrer & Taylor** | 2007 | Mathematics | Shuffling math problem types tripled test scores compared to blocked practice, despite reducing practice-session performance |
| **Taylor & Rohrer** | 2010 | Mathematics | Interleaved practice produced 43% correct on a delayed test vs. 20% for blocked practice |
| **Birnbaum et al.** | 2013 | Category learning | Demonstrated that both discrimination and retrieval mechanisms independently contribute to the interleaving effect |
| **Rohrer, Dedrick, & Stencil** | 2015 | Mathematics (K-12) | 7th graders who interleaved math practice scored 72% on a surprise test 30 days later vs. 38% for blocked practice |
| **Brunmair & Richter** | 2019 | Meta-analysis | Confirmed a moderate positive effect of interleaving across 39 studies (d = 0.42) |

### The Kornell and Bjork (2008) Study in Detail

This influential study asked participants to learn to identify the painting styles of 12 different artists by viewing six exemplar paintings per artist. Half the artists' paintings were shown in **blocked** format (all six paintings consecutively), and half were shown in **interleaved** format (mixed with paintings by other artists).

On a later test with novel paintings by the same artists, participants were significantly more accurate at identifying artists whose paintings had been interleaved during study. Critically, when asked which study format they believed was more effective, **78% of participants chose blocking** -- the format that actually produced worse learning. This disconnect between subjective experience and objective performance is a hallmark of desirable difficulties.

### The Rohrer and Taylor (2007) Mathematics Study

College students practiced math problems involving volume calculations. In the blocked condition, students completed all problems of one type (e.g., spheroid volume) before moving to the next type. In the interleaved condition, problem types were shuffled randomly.

- **Practice session scores**: Blocked > Interleaved (89% vs. 60%)
- **Test scores one week later**: Interleaved > Blocked (63% vs. 20%)

Interleaving tripled test performance despite reducing practice performance -- a striking demonstration of the desirable difficulty principle.

## Practical Applications

### In Education

- **Math homework**: Assign problem sets that mix types from the current and previous chapters, rather than grouping by type
- **Science courses**: Interleave problems requiring different formulas or concepts within a single assignment
- **Language learning**: Mix vocabulary, grammar, reading, and listening activities rather than practicing each in isolated blocks
- **Medical training**: Present diagnostic cases from multiple specialties in randomized order, forcing trainees to determine the relevant specialty before diagnosing

### In Sports and Motor Learning

The *Make It Stick* baseball example: batters who practiced hitting fastballs, curves, and change-ups in random interleaved order hit better in subsequent games than those who practiced each pitch type in blocks. The beanbag toss study showed that children who practiced throwing at varied distances (2 and 4 feet) outperformed those who practiced only at the test distance (3 feet) -- even though they never practiced at that exact distance.

### In Knowledge Management and Zettelkasten

Interleaving maps naturally to Zettelkasten practice:

- **Note-taking across topics**: Processing notes from multiple sources/domains in a single session (rather than exhaustively processing one book before starting the next) is interleaved practice
- **Mixed review sessions**: Reviewing notes from different topic clusters in a single session forces discrimination and strengthens cross-domain connections
- **Cross-linking**: The Zettelkasten practice of creating links between notes from different domains is a form of interleaved elaboration -- it forces the learner to identify what different ideas share and how they differ
- **SlipBox graph traversal**: Walking a knowledge graph across topic boundaries naturally interleaves domains

## Criticisms and Limitations

- **Not always superior**: Interleaving is most beneficial for tasks that require discrimination between similar categories. For completely unrelated topics, the discrimination benefit is minimal
- **Higher cognitive load**: For novice learners with limited prior knowledge, interleaving may create unproductive cognitive overload rather than desirable difficulty
- **Initial performance costs**: The reduction in practice-session performance can be discouraging for learners and may lead to premature abandonment of the strategy
- **Metacognitive resistance**: Because interleaving feels less effective than blocking, both students and teachers tend to resist adopting it without explicit education about the research
- **Optimal interleaving ratio is unclear**: Research has not definitively established how many categories should be interleaved simultaneously, or whether the ratio should change with expertise
- **Effect size variability**: The 2019 Brunmair and Richter meta-analysis found that the interleaving benefit varies considerably across domains and task types
