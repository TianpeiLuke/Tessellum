# Term: Ethos

## Definition

**Ethos** (Greek: *ethos*, "character") is one of Aristotle's three modes of persuasion -- the rhetorical appeal based on the **speaker's credibility, character, and trustworthiness**. In Aristotle's *Rhetoric* (c. 350 BCE), persuasion is achieved through ethos "when the speech is so spoken as to make us think [the speaker] credible." Unlike logos (logical argument) and pathos (emotional appeal), ethos operates through the audience's perception of *who is speaking* rather than *what is said* or *how it makes them feel*.

Aristotle identified three components of ethos: **phronesis** (practical wisdom -- demonstrating relevant expertise and sound judgment), **arete** (moral virtue -- displaying integrity and good character), and **eunoia** (goodwill -- showing genuine concern for the audience's interests). A speaker who projects all three is perceived as credible, and credibility makes the audience more receptive to the speaker's logos and pathos appeals. Ethos is therefore both an independent persuasive force and a **force multiplier** for the other two modes -- a logical argument from a trusted source lands harder than the same argument from an unknown or distrusted one.

## Key Properties

- **Earned, not claimed**: Ethos is established through demonstrated expertise, consistent honesty, and fair treatment of opposing views -- not through self-declaration ("Trust me, I'm an expert")
- **Three Aristotelian components**: Phronesis (practical wisdom/expertise), arete (moral virtue/integrity), and eunoia (goodwill toward the audience) -- all three are necessary for full credibility
- **Extrinsic vs. intrinsic**: Extrinsic ethos is the speaker's pre-existing reputation; intrinsic ethos is the credibility demonstrated within the speech or argument itself -- a speaker with low extrinsic ethos can build intrinsic ethos through the quality of their reasoning
- **Temporal progression**: Credibility evolves through three stages -- initial (pre-existing reputation), derived (demonstrated during communication), and terminal (lasting impression left afterward)
- **Fallacy boundary**: Ethos becomes fallacious when it substitutes for evidence rather than complementing it -- the [appeal to authority](term_appeal_to_authority.md) fallacy is ethos divorced from relevant expertise or used as a replacement for independent reasoning
- **Complementary to logos and pathos**: The three modes work together -- ethos establishes *why to listen*, logos establishes *what to believe*, pathos establishes *why to care*

## Related Terms

- **[Persuasion](term_persuasion.md)**: Parent concept -- ethos is one of the three Aristotelian modes of persuasion alongside logos and pathos
- **[Appeal to Authority](term_appeal_to_authority.md)**: The fallacious form of ethos -- when authority or credibility substitutes for evidence rather than supporting it
- **[Appeal to Emotion](term_appeal_to_emotion.md)**: Pathos, the sister mode -- emotional appeal that becomes fallacious when it replaces rather than complements reasoning
- **[Logical Fallacies](term_logical_fallacies.md)**: The broader taxonomy; ethos-related fallacies include appeal to authority, ad hominem (attacking ethos to dismiss an argument), and bandwagon
- **[Steelmanning](term_steelmanning.md)**: A technique that builds intrinsic ethos -- engaging with the strongest version of an opposing argument signals fairness and intellectual honesty
- **[Active Listening](term_active_listening.md)**: Builds the eunoia (goodwill) component of ethos by demonstrating genuine interest in the other person's perspective
- **[Argumentation](term_argumentation.md)**: The broader discipline of constructing and evaluating arguments; ethos is one dimension that argumentation theory considers alongside logical structure (logos) and audience engagement (pathos)
- **[Cognitive Bias](term_cognitive_bias.md)**: Authority bias, halo effect, and social proof are the cognitive mechanisms through which ethos influences audiences
- **[Critical Thinking](term_critical_thinking.md)**: Critical thinking evaluates ethos claims — distinguishing legitimate expertise from unwarranted authority appeals
