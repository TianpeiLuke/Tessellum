# Term: Knowledge Building Blocks

## Definition

**Knowledge Building Blocks** are the six discrete types of knowledge atoms that constitute the structural units of a knowledge system. Developed by Sascha Fast in 2011 and published as part of his atomicity framework on zettelkasten.de, the inventory identifies: **premises**, **logical form**, **conclusions**, **definitions**, **distinctions**, and **heuristics**. These six types provide an objective, structural answer to the question "what is an atomic note?" -- replacing the vague heuristic "one idea per note" with concrete, verifiable criteria.

The fundamental knowledge atom is the **argument**, which is the composite of three building blocks: premises + logical form + conclusion. An argument has exactly three structural elements, giving practitioners a concrete test: "Does my note contain one complete argument?" This structural criterion eliminates the subjectivity that plagued earlier formulations of atomicity (what Sascha diagnosed as the "[beetle-in-a-box](term_beetle_in_a_box.md) problem" -- everyone uses the word "atomic" but privately defines it differently).

## Historical Context

Sascha Fast developed his inventory of knowledge building blocks in **2011**, well before publishing the framework publicly. The concept was formally articulated in his 2025 article ["The Principle of Atomicity -- On the Difference Between a Principle and Its Implementation"](https://zettelkasten.de/posts/principle-of-atomicity-difference-between-principle-and-implementation/) and expanded in ["The Complete Guide to Atomic Note-Taking"](https://zettelkasten.de/atomicity/guide/) on zettelkasten.de.

The framework was a direct response to a persistent community problem: Christian Tietze coined the "Principle of Atomicity" in 2013 as a practical workflow heuristic ("separate concerns into individual notes"), but the community lacked a shared definition of what constitutes an "atom." Sascha used Wittgenstein's beetle-in-a-box thought experiment to diagnose this confusion -- the term "atomic note" had become a floating signifier with no shared referent. Knowledge Building Blocks provide the concrete referent.

|------|-----------|
| 2011 | Sascha develops his inventory of knowledge building blocks |
| 2013 | Christian Tietze coins "Principle of Atomicity" (practical workflow) |
| 2025 | Sascha publishes the principle-vs-implementation article with the Six Building Blocks |
| 2025 | The Complete Guide to Atomic Note-Taking expands the framework with four levels of mastery |

## Taxonomy

The six knowledge building blocks divide into two functional groups: **argument components** (the three parts of the fundamental atom) and **supporting apparatus** (the three types that enable and refine arguments).

### Argument Components (The Fundamental Atom)

| Building Block | Role | Example |
|----------------|------|---------|
| **Premises** | Claims or facts that support a conclusion | "Luhmann wrote 90,000 notes over 40 years" |
| **Logical Form** | The reasoning structure connecting premises to conclusions | "If X and Y, then Z" (deductive); "All observed cases show X, therefore probably Y" (inductive) |
| **Conclusion** | The claim being argued for, derived from premises via logical form | "The Zettelkasten method enables prolific scholarly output" |

An **argument** = premises + logical form + conclusion. This is the fundamental knowledge atom. A purely atomic note contains exactly one argument -- nothing can be removed without rendering the idea incomplete, and nothing extraneous is present.

### Supporting Apparatus

| Building Block | Role | Example |
|----------------|------|---------|
| **Definitions** | Precise meanings of terms used in arguments | "Atomicity means one knowledge building block per note" |
| **Distinctions** | Boundaries between related concepts that prevent conflation | "Fleeting notes vs. permanent notes"; "principle vs. implementation" |
| **Heuristics** | Rules of thumb that guide practice without formal proof | "If a note says two things, split it"; "If it's easy to name, it's probably atomic" |

## Key Properties

- **Objective criteria**: The building blocks replace subjective assessments ("Does this feel like one idea?") with structural tests ("Does this contain one complete argument?")
- **Verifiable completeness**: An argument-atom can be checked -- are all premises stated? Is the logical form explicit? Is the conclusion clear?
- **Verifiable minimality**: Nothing can be removed without breaking the argument; nothing unrelated is included
- **Composable**: Building blocks combine -- an argument uses premises and logical form; premises require definitions; distinctions clarify boundaries between related arguments
- **Type-stable**: Sascha reports the inventory has remained stable since 2011 -- the six types have proven sufficient for classifying all knowledge atoms encountered in practice
- **Domain-independent**: The building blocks are epistemological universals, not tied to any specific discipline
- **Two-tier structure**: Three blocks form the fundamental atom (argument); three blocks serve as supporting infrastructure
- **Resolution of the beetle-in-a-box problem**: Provides a shared, inspectable definition of "atom" that eliminates the floating-signifier problem in community discourse

## Applications

| Application | How Building Blocks Apply |
|-------------|--------------------------|
| **Note atomicity testing** | Check: does the note contain exactly one argument (premises + logical form + conclusion)? If yes, it is atomic. |
| **Note splitting** | A note with two arguments should be split into two notes; a note with only premises but no conclusion is incomplete |
| **Quality assessment** | Notes can be evaluated by structural completeness: are premises explicit? Is the logical form visible? Is the conclusion stated? |
| **Teaching atomicity** | Provides concrete vocabulary and structural tests instead of vague advice ("one idea per note") |
| **Mastery progression** | Maps to Sascha's [Shuhari](term_shuhari.md) model: SHU-level uses heuristics ("easy to name"), HA-level identifies building block types, RI-level creates freely |
| **Vault architecture** | Term notes implement the definition/distinction blocks; digest notes implement argument-atoms; analysis notes synthesize multiple arguments |

## Challenges and Limitations

- **Learning curve**: Identifying building block types requires practice -- beginners may struggle to distinguish premises from conclusions or logical form from heuristics
- **Not all knowledge fits neatly**: Some knowledge (narratives, observations, empirical data) may resist clean classification into the six types
- **Expanded inventory in the Guide**: The Complete Guide to Atomic Note-Taking lists a slightly different set: concepts, arguments, counter-arguments, models, hypotheses/theories, and empirical observations -- suggesting the taxonomy is still evolving
- **Arguments are compound**: Since an argument is itself three building blocks combined, the "one building block per note" rule requires judgment about granularity
- **Heuristics vs. formal criteria**: Ironically, the framework uses heuristics (a building block type) to identify when to apply formal criteria (another building block type)
- **Community adoption**: Despite being published in 2025, the building blocks have not yet displaced "one idea per note" as the dominant atomicity heuristic in the broader PKM community

- **[Research Argument](term_research_argument.md)**: A specialized form of the argument-atom applied to academic research claims
