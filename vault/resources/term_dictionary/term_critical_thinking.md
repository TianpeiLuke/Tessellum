# Critical Thinking

## Definition

**Critical thinking** is the disciplined, self-directed process of analyzing and evaluating information, arguments, and evidence in order to form well-reasoned judgments and make sound decisions. The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy characterizes it as "careful goal-directed thinking" — active, persistent, and careful consideration of any belief or supposed form of knowledge in the light of the grounds that support it and the further conclusions to which it tends. Unlike passive acceptance of claims, critical thinking requires the thinker to deliberately examine assumptions, assess the quality and relevance of evidence, identify logical fallacies, and consider alternative perspectives before reaching a conclusion.

The concept encompasses two complementary dimensions: **dispositions** (the willingness to think critically — intellectual curiosity, open-mindedness, fair-mindedness, respect for evidence, tolerance of ambiguity) and **abilities** (the capability to think well — observation, inference, analysis, evaluation, explanation, self-regulation). A person with strong abilities but weak dispositions may reason skillfully yet selectively; a person with strong dispositions but weak abilities may be willing yet ineffective. Effective critical thinking requires both.

Critical thinking is distinct from but overlapping with several related constructs: **analytical thinking** (breaking problems into components), **creative thinking** (generating novel ideas), **[argumentation](term_argumentation.md)** (constructing and evaluating arguments), and **problem solving** (resolving specific challenges). Critical thinking provides the evaluative framework within which these other thinking modes operate — it is the quality control function of thought itself.

## Historical Context

| Period | Key Figure(s) | Contribution |
|--------|---------------|-------------|
| **Classical (c. 470 BCE)** | Socrates | Developed the method of systematic questioning (elenchus) to expose unjustified beliefs — the prototype of all critical thinking pedagogy |
| **Enlightenment (17th c.)** | Descartes, Locke | Descartes' methodical doubt; Locke's empiricism and emphasis on evidence-based reasoning |
| **Progressive Education (1910)** | John Dewey | Introduced "reflective thinking" (his preferred term for critical thinking) as an educational goal in *How We Think* (1910); defined it as "active, persistent, and careful consideration of any belief or supposed form of knowledge in the light of the grounds that support it" |
| **Taxonomy Era (1941-1962)** | Glaser, Bloom, Ennis | Glaser (1941) conducted first experiments on teaching critical thinking. Bloom et al. (1956) created the cognitive taxonomy (Knowledge → Comprehension → Application → Analysis → Synthesis → Evaluation). Ennis (1962) published landmark definition: "the correct assessing of statements" with 12 aspects across logical, criterial, and pragmatic dimensions |
| **Modern Frameworks (1980s-2000s)** | Richard Paul, Peter Facione, Matthew Lipman | Paul developed "strong-sense" critical thinking emphasizing fair-mindedness and empathic engagement with opposing worldviews. Facione (1990) led the APA Delphi consensus: critical thinking as "purposeful, self-regulatory judgment resulting in interpretation, analysis, evaluation, inference, explanation, and self-regulation." Lipman pioneered Philosophy for Children (P4C) to teach critical thinking from early ages |
| **Revised Taxonomy (2001)** | Anderson, Krathwohl | Revised Bloom's Taxonomy: Remember → Understand → Apply → Analyze → Evaluate → Create — with "Create" replacing "Synthesis" at the top |

## Taxonomy

### Major Frameworks

| Framework | Author(s) | Core Structure | Emphasis |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|----------|
| **Bloom's Taxonomy** (1956/2001) | Bloom; Anderson & Krathwohl | Six hierarchical cognitive levels: Remember → Understand → Apply → Analyze → Evaluate → Create | Classifying cognitive operations by complexity; curriculum design |
| **Paul-Elder Model** (1990s) | Richard Paul, Linda Elder | Elements of Thought (purpose, question, information, concepts, assumptions, inferences, point of view, implications) + Intellectual Standards (clarity, accuracy, precision, relevance, depth, breadth, logic, significance, fairness) | Self-directed metacognition; "strong-sense" engagement with opposing views |
| **Facione Delphi Consensus** (1990) | Peter Facione (APA panel) | Six core skills: Interpretation, Analysis, Evaluation, Inference, Explanation, Self-Regulation + Dispositions (inquisitiveness, systematicity, truth-seeking, open-mindedness, analyticity, confidence in reasoning, maturity of judgment) | Expert consensus definition; assessment instrument design (CCTST) |
| **Ennis Framework** (1962/1987) | Robert Ennis | "Reasonable, reflective thinking focused on deciding what to believe or do" — 12 aspects across logical, criterial, and pragmatic dimensions | Dispositions + abilities; transferability across domains |
| **Halpern Model** (1998) | Diane Halpern | Five categories: verbal reasoning, argument analysis, thinking as hypothesis testing, likelihood and uncertainty, decision-making and problem-solving | Empirical psychology of thinking; domain transfer |

### Facione's Six Core Skills (APA Delphi Consensus)

| Skill | Definition | Example |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| **Interpretation** | Comprehend and express the meaning of data, statements, experiences | "What does this metric actually measure?" |
| **Analysis** | Identify the intended and actual inferential relationships among statements | "What's the implicit warrant connecting the evidence to the claim?" |
| **Evaluation** | Assess the credibility of statements and the strength of arguments | "Is this sample representative? Is this source authoritative?" |
| **Inference** | Draw reasonable conclusions from evidence | "Given these three data points, what can we conclude?" |
| **Explanation** | State and justify reasoning clearly | "Here's my conclusion and here's how I reached it" |
| **Self-Regulation** | Monitor one's own cognitive processes for bias and error | "Am I being influenced by confirmation bias here?" |

## Key Properties

- **Trainable skill, not innate talent**: Critical thinking is a learnable set of skills and dispositions, not a fixed personality trait — Dewey, Ennis, and Paul all emphasized this
- **Dual-component structure**: Requires both **dispositions** (willingness) and **abilities** (capability); one without the other is insufficient
- **Domain-general and domain-specific**: The core skills transfer across contexts, but effective application requires domain-specific knowledge and warrants (the generalizability debate remains active)
- **Metacognitive**: Critical thinking includes monitoring one's own thinking — Facione's "self-regulation" component makes this explicit
- **Normative**: Critical thinking defines standards for what good thinking looks like (clarity, accuracy, precision, relevance, depth, breadth, logic, significance, fairness — Paul-Elder intellectual standards)
- **Antidote to cognitive bias**: Critical thinking is the deliberate engagement of System 2 reasoning to override the heuristic shortcuts and systematic errors of System 1 (Kahneman's dual-process framework)
- **Connected to democratic citizenship**: From Dewey onward, critical thinking has been linked to informed participation in democratic society — the ability to evaluate claims, resist manipulation, and make evidence-based decisions
- **Hierarchical**: Bloom's taxonomy establishes that critical thinking operates at the higher cognitive levels (Analyze, Evaluate, Create) — building on but going beyond knowledge recall and comprehension
- **Self-correcting**: A defining feature that distinguishes critical thinking from dogmatism — the willingness to revise conclusions when evidence warrants it
- **Emotionally engaged, not emotionally detached**: Paul's "strong-sense" critical thinking and Facione's dispositions both emphasize that good critical thinking requires empathy, fair-mindedness, and intellectual courage — not cold rationality

## Applications

| Domain | Application |
|--------|------------|
| **Education** | Curriculum design using Bloom's taxonomy; assessment with CCTST and Watson-Glaser instruments; Philosophy for Children (P4C) |
| **Research** | Evaluating claims, evidence, and warrants in academic arguments; peer review; experimental design |
| **Business** | Strategic analysis (SWOT, PESTLE, Porter's Five Forces); decision-making under uncertainty; root cause analysis |
| **Data science** | Evaluating model assumptions, distinguishing correlation from causation, assessing evidence quality and representativeness |
| **Medicine** | Clinical reasoning; differential diagnosis; evidence-based practice |
| **Policy** | Evaluating competing proposals; detecting fallacious reasoning in public discourse; cost-benefit analysis |
| **Personal** | Consumer decisions; media literacy; resistance to misinformation and propaganda |

## Challenges and Limitations

### Theoretical Debates
- **Generalizability controversy**: Whether critical thinking skills transfer across domains or require domain-specific knowledge remains unresolved (McPeck vs. Ennis debate)
- **Rationality bias**: Critics argue that mainstream critical thinking pedagogy overemphasizes formal reasoning while neglecting emotion, intuition, embodied cognition, and collaborative thinking
- **Cultural assumptions**: The Western philosophical tradition underpinning critical thinking (Socratic method, Enlightenment rationalism) may not be universally applicable or valued

### Practical Challenges
- **Assessment difficulty**: Measuring dispositions is harder than measuring skills; standardized tests (CCTST, Watson-Glaser) capture only a subset of critical thinking
- **Teaching paradox**: Students need domain knowledge to think critically about a domain, but are often taught critical thinking as a domain-independent "skill"
- **Disposition gap**: People may possess critical thinking abilities but fail to deploy them due to cognitive laziness, emotional commitment to existing beliefs, or social pressure

## Related Terms

### Thinking Frameworks
- **Cognitive Bias**: The systematic errors in System 1 thinking that critical thinking is designed to detect and override — biases are what happens when critical thinking is absent
- **WYSIATI**: "What You See Is All There Is" — the bias critical thinking most directly counteracts by asking "What information is missing?"
- **[Systems Thinking](term_systems_thinking.md)**: Complements critical thinking by providing tools for analyzing complex, interconnected systems rather than isolated claims
- **Design Thinking**: Complements critical thinking with creative and human-centered approaches; critical thinking evaluates what design thinking generates
- **[MECE](term_mece.md)**: A structural tool for decomposing problems that operationalizes the critical thinking principle of comprehensive analysis

### Argumentation and Logic
- **[Argumentation](term_argumentation.md)**: The broader discipline of constructing and evaluating arguments; critical thinking provides the evaluative standards that argumentation theory applies
- **Logical Fallacies**: Systematic violations of good reasoning that critical thinking training teaches people to recognize and avoid
- **Research Argument**: Booth's five-component model operationalizes critical thinking for academic writing — every component maps to a critical thinking skill
- **[Socratic Questioning](term_socratic_questioning.md)**: The foundational pedagogical method of critical thinking — probing assumptions, evidence, implications, and perspectives through systematic questioning
- **Steelmanning**: The practice of constructing the strongest version of an opposing argument — embodies Paul's "strong-sense" critical thinking

### Inquiry Methods
- **Five Whys**: An iterative questioning technique that operationalizes critical thinking's demand to probe beneath surface explanations
- **Question Storming**: Generating diverse questions before seeking answers — a practice that develops critical thinking dispositions (curiosity, open-mindedness)
- **Active Listening**: A prerequisite for critical evaluation — understanding a claim accurately before assessing it
- **Analytical Reading**: Adler's method of active reading maps directly to Facione's core skills — interpretation, analysis, evaluation, inference
