# Term: Research Problem Formula

## Definition

The **research problem formula** is a four-part sentence template from Booth et al.'s *The Craft of Research* that transforms vague interests into researchable problems by forcing the researcher to articulate not just what they're studying (topic) or what they want to know (question), but **why anyone should care** (significance). The formula is: **"I am studying [TOPIC] because I want to find out [QUESTION] in order to help my reader understand [SIGNIFICANCE] so that they might [APPLICATION]."** The "in order to" clause is the critical innovation — it demands a "So what?" answer before the research begins, preventing the common failure of producing research that answers a question nobody asked.

## Full Name

**Research Problem Formula** = Topic-Question-Significance-Application Template

Also known as: **Booth's Problem Formula**, **"So What?" Template**, **Research Problem Statement**

## The Formula

```
I am studying _____________ [TOPIC]
because I want to find out _____________ [QUESTION]
in order to help my reader understand _____________ [SIGNIFICANCE]
so that they might _____________ [APPLICATION — optional]
```

| Component | What It Does | Answers | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|---------|
| **Topic** | What you're looking at | "What?" | "...the role of caching in knowledge management" |
| **Question** | What you don't know | "What specifically?" | "...how LRU eviction policies affect retrieval quality" |
| **Significance** | Why it matters to the reader | "So what?" | "...the trade-offs between piling and filing systems" |
| **Application** (optional) | What readers might do with the answer | "What then?" | "...design better personal knowledge architectures" |

## The Narrowing Funnel

```
Broad Interest
    │
    ▼
Plausible Topic ──── "What am I looking at?"
    │
    ▼
Specific Question ── "What don't I know about it?"
    │
    ▼
Defined Problem ──── "Why does the answer matter?"  ← "So what?" test
    │
    ▼
Research begins
```

### Four Perspective Lenses for Narrowing

When stuck moving from topic to question, apply these lenses:

| Lens | Question Pattern | Example (Topic: "caching") |
|------|-----------------|---------------------------|
| **Parts and wholes** | What are its components? What is it part of? | "What layers make up the cache hierarchy?" |
| **History and change** | How has it changed? What caused the changes? | "How has LRU evolved since Denning's working set model?" |
| **Categories and characteristics** | What type is it? How does it compare? | "How does LRU compare to LFU and random eviction?" |
| **Value and evaluation** | Is it good or bad? By what criteria? | "When does LRU outperform other strategies?" |

## Practical vs. Research Problems

| Dimension | Practical Problem | Research Problem |
|-----------|------------------|-----------------|
| **Triggered by** | A condition in the world | A gap in knowledge |
| **Resolved by** | Action (doing something) | Answer (understanding something) |
| **"So what?" answer** | Consequence of inaction | Consequence of ignorance |
| **Example** | "Investigators are overwhelmed" | "We don't understand how to allocate investigation capacity" |

**Connection**: Practical problems motivate research problems. The answer to the research problem informs the solution to the practical problem. The optional fourth clause ("so that they might...") makes this chain explicit.

## Relationship to Other Research Frameworks

### Booth vs. Mullaney

| Dimension | Booth's Formula | Mullaney's Self-Centered Research |
|-----------|----------------|----------------------------------|
| **Starting point** | External: narrow from topic | Internal: discover from personal curiosity |
| **Problem definition** | "I am studying X because Y in order to Z" | "What keeps you up at night?" |
| **When to use** | When you have a topic and need to sharpen it | When you don't know what to research yet |
| **Complementarity** | Mullaney finds the problem → Booth structures it as an argument |

### Booth vs. Berger

| Dimension | Booth's Formula | Berger's Questioning Cycle |
|-----------|----------------|---------------------------|
| **Structure** | Linear: Topic → Question → Problem | Cyclical: Why → What If → How |
| **Focus** | Research communication | Innovation and discovery |
| **Complementarity** | Berger generates questions → Booth evaluates which are researchable |

## Applications

### Paper Triage
Apply the formula to evaluate whether a paper deserves [analytical reading](term_analytical_reading.md):
- Can you fill in all four clauses for the paper's research?
- If the "in order to help readers understand" clause is empty or trivial, the paper may lack significance.

### Research Planning
Before starting any research project, fill in the formula completely. If you can't articulate the "in order to" clause, you have a topic and a question but not yet a problem.

### Paper Review
When reviewing a paper, check whether the introduction follows the formula's logic:
1. Does it establish the **topic** (context)?
2. Does it identify the **question** (gap)?
3. Does it explain the **significance** (why the gap matters)?
4. Does it state the **response** (the paper's contribution)?
