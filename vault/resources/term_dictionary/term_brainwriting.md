# Brainwriting

## Definition

**Brainwriting** is a structured, silent ideation method where participants write ideas independently before building on each other's thinking — eliminating the "loudest voice wins" dynamic of traditional brainstorming. Unlike verbal brainstorming where extroverts, senior leaders, or fast talkers dominate airtime, brainwriting ensures **equal participation** by making every contributor's input visible and available for others to extend. Ideas are generated in writing, passed to peers, and iteratively enriched through successive rounds.

The technique was invented by **Bernd Rohrbach** in 1968/1969 as the **6-3-5 method** (6 participants, 3 ideas per round, 5 minutes per rotation). Each participant writes three ideas on a worksheet, passes it to the next person, who reads the existing ideas and adds three more — either new ideas or variations inspired by what they read. After six rounds, the group has generated up to **108 ideas in 30 minutes**. The written format creates a durable record of ideation, surfaces a wider range of ideas than verbal brainstorming, and reduces production blocking (waiting for your turn to speak).

## Taxonomy

Brainwriting encompasses several distinct variants, each suited to different group sizes and settings:

- **6-3-5 Classic**: The original Rohrbach format — 6 people, 3 ideas per round, 5-minute rotations, worksheets passed physically around the table
- **Collaborative Brainwriting**: Participants write ideas on cards or sticky notes, then place them in a shared pool; others draw from the pool and build on what they find
- **Digital Brainwriting**: Uses platforms like Miro, MURAL, Google Docs, or Slack threads to replicate the pass-and-build mechanic asynchronously across distributed teams
- **Round-Robin Brainwriting**: Each participant writes one idea and passes the sheet to the next person, who adds one more; continues until sheets return to origin

## Key Properties

- **Silent**: All idea generation happens in writing, removing verbal dominance and social pressure
- **Written record**: Every idea is captured from inception, eliminating the "lost idea" problem of verbal brainstorming
- **Equal participation**: Introverts, junior team members, and non-native speakers contribute on equal footing with extroverts and senior leaders
- **Builds on others' thinking**: Each round layers new perspectives onto existing ideas, producing richer and more diverse outputs than independent ideation
- **Anti-groupthink**: The written, independent format resists anchoring bias and conformity pressure that plague verbal group discussions
- **High throughput**: The 6-3-5 format generates up to 108 ideas in 30 minutes — far exceeding typical brainstorming output
- **Scalable**: Works for 4–30+ participants by adjusting round count, group size, or using digital tools
- **Time-boxed**: Fixed rotation intervals maintain momentum and prevent overthinking

## Applications

| Domain | Application | Example |
|--------|-------------|---------|
| **Product Design** | Feature ideation sprints where cross-functional teams generate and refine product concepts | Design sprint workshops using 6-3-5 to generate UI/UX alternatives |
| **Strategic Planning** | Exploring strategic options before convergent evaluation or 6-pager writing | Leadership offsites using brainwriting to surface strategic pivots |
| **Abuse Prevention** | BAP uses brainwriting as the divergent ideation phase for durable problem identification, generating a wide range of problem statements and potential solutions before converging via Shark Tank evaluation | Quarterly innovation sessions where abuse analysts and scientists brainwrite on emerging fraud vectors |
| **Education** | Classroom engagement where students build on peers' written responses | Think-Write-Pass exercises in university seminars |
| **Process Improvement** | Generating root-cause hypotheses and countermeasures during operational reviews | Kaizen events using brainwriting for broad hypothesis generation |

## Challenges

- **Social loafing**: Some participants may contribute low-effort ideas when anonymity reduces accountability; mitigated by limiting rounds and reviewing all contributions
- **Idea quality vs. quantity**: Brainwriting optimizes for volume; without structured evaluation (e.g., dot-voting, Shark Tank), teams may drown in undifferentiated ideas
- **Requires facilitation**: Setting up worksheets, timing rounds, and synthesizing outputs demands a skilled facilitator — the method is not truly self-organizing
- **Legibility and clarity**: Handwritten ideas can be ambiguous or illegible; digital variants solve this but introduce tooling overhead
- **Convergence gap**: Brainwriting is purely divergent — teams must pair it with a convergent method (affinity mapping, voting, or pitch evaluation) to reach decisions
