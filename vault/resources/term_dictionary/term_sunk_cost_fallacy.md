# Sunk Cost Fallacy

## Definition

The sunk cost fallacy is the tendency to continue investing in a decision --- pouring in additional time, money, or effort --- based on the cumulative prior investment ("sunk costs") rather than on the prospective future returns. Rational decision-making, as defined by standard economic theory, requires that only *marginal* (future) costs and benefits should influence a choice; past expenditures that cannot be recovered are economically irrelevant. Yet humans routinely violate this principle, treating irrecoverable investments as reasons to persist.

The bias is also called the "Concorde fallacy," named after the British-French supersonic jet project. By the mid-1970s it was clear that the Concorde would never be commercially viable, yet both governments continued funding because abandoning the project would "waste" the billions already spent. The billions were already gone regardless --- the only relevant question was whether *future* spending would produce positive returns. It would not, but the project continued to completion.

Psychologically, the sunk cost fallacy is driven by loss aversion (abandoning feels like "losing" the investment), cognitive dissonance (admitting the investment was wrong is painful), and the desire to appear consistent. In organizational settings it takes the form of "escalation of commitment," where managers double down on failing strategies to justify earlier decisions, especially when their reputation is tied to the project.

## Historical Context

The sunk cost effect was experimentally demonstrated by Hal Arkes and Catherine Blumer in their landmark 1985 paper "The Psychology of Sunk Cost," where participants who paid more for theater tickets attended more performances --- even bad ones --- than those who paid less. Richard Thaler's 1980 paper "Toward a Positive Theory of Consumer Choice" provided an early theoretical treatment, connecting sunk costs to broader patterns of mental accounting.

Barry Staw's 1976 paper "Knee-Deep in the Big Muddy" studied escalation of commitment in organizational contexts, showing that decision-makers who felt personally responsible for an initial investment were especially likely to escalate. The concept has roots in economic theory going back centuries --- the idea that "bygones are bygones" appears in classical economics --- but behavioral economists demonstrated that humans systematically fail to follow this rational prescription.

The sunk cost effect has since been studied in children (who show it by age five), in non-Western cultures, and even in some animal species, suggesting it may have deep evolutionary roots. Some evolutionary psychologists have argued that in ancestral environments with high uncertainty, persistence signals commitment to social partners and may have had reputational benefits that partially offset its economic irrationality.

## Key Properties

- **Violates rational choice theory**: standard economics holds that only marginal costs and benefits matter; sunk costs are, by definition, irrecoverable and should be ignored
- **Driven by loss aversion**: abandoning an investment *feels* like a loss, even though the resources are already gone regardless of the decision
- **Amplified by personal responsibility**: people escalate more when they made the original investment decision themselves (Staw, 1976)
- **Cognitive dissonance plays a role**: continuing justifies the earlier decision and avoids the psychological pain of admitting error
- **Manifests across all domains**: relationships ("we've been together so long"), careers ("I've spent years in this field"), software ("we've already written so much code"), warfare ("we've already lost so many soldiers")
- **Organizational escalation**: in institutions, sunk cost reasoning becomes "escalation of commitment," where political and reputational incentives compound the psychological bias
- **Mental accounting amplifies it**: when costs are mentally "booked" to a specific account, the pressure to see a return on that account intensifies
- **Not universal across species**: some animal studies show sunk cost behavior, but it is debated; the effect may be uniquely strong in humans due to self-narrative
- **Time sunk costs are especially powerful**: people are often more influenced by time already invested than money, because time feels more personal and irreplaceable
- **Antidote is the "fresh eyes" test**: ask "if I were starting from scratch today, with none of this prior investment, would I choose this path?"

## Applications

| Domain | How It Manifests | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Software Engineering | Continuing to build on a failing architecture because of prior development effort | "We can't rewrite; we've spent two years on this codebase" |
| Investing | Holding losing stocks to "get back to even" rather than reallocating capital | Refusing to sell a declining stock because "I've already lost so much" |
| Relationships | Staying in an unhappy relationship because of years already invested | "We've been together ten years, I can't just walk away now" |
| Military/Policy | Continuing a war or policy because of lives and money already spent | Vietnam War escalation: "We can't let those sacrifices be in vain" |
| Career Decisions | Staying in a mismatched career because of education and time invested | "I spent seven years becoming a lawyer, I can't switch now" |
| Consumer Behavior | Finishing a bad meal, book, or movie because you paid for it | Eating food you do not enjoy because it was expensive |

## Related Terms

- **[Cognitive Bias](term_cognitive_bias.md)**: parent concept; sunk cost fallacy is a specific systematic decision-making error
- **[Loss Aversion](term_loss_aversion.md)**: the primary emotional driver; abandoning sunk costs triggers the pain of perceived loss
- **[Cognitive Dissonance](term_cognitive_dissonance.md)**: continuing investment reduces the dissonance between "I made a good decision" and evidence of failure
- **[Endowment Effect](term_endowment_effect.md)**: related overvaluation of what we already possess or have invested in
- **[Planning Fallacy](term_planning_fallacy.md)**: optimistic forecasts about future returns can perpetuate sunk cost reasoning
- **[Overconfidence Effect](term_overconfidence_effect.md)**: overconfidence in eventual success justifies continued investment in failing projects
- **[Hindsight Bias](term_hindsight_bias.md)**: after abandoning a sunk cost, hindsight bias may make the "right" choice seem obvious, distorting future learning
- **[Confirmation Bias](term_confirmation_bias.md)**: selectively seeking evidence that the investment will eventually pay off
- **[Survivorship Bias](term_survivorship_bias.md)**: success stories of those who persisted through sunk costs and won can encourage irrational persistence in others
- **[Status Quo Bias](term_status_quo_bias.md)**: the preference for the current state of affairs overlaps with sunk cost reasoning when the current path involves prior investment
